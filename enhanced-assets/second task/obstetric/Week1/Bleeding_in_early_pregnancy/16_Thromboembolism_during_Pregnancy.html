Thromboembolism during Pregnancy

Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state — and the leading non-obstetric cause of maternal mortality. The three clinical syndromes — superficial thrombophlebitis, deep venous thrombosis, and pulmonary embolism — share Virchow's triad of hypercoagulation, vascular damage, and venous stasis, but demand very different urgency of management.

2Superficial Thrombophlebitis

Common in veins of calf, thigh, inguinal region and vulva.

Clinical picture

  • Localized discomfort.
  • Erythema.
  • Localized superficial tenderness.
  • Pain.
  • Palpable lump or palpable cord over the course of the affected superficial veins.
  • Low grade fever.

Complications

  • Superficial venous thrombosis increases the risk of DVT.

Treatment

  • Bed rest.
  • Analgesic.
  • Elastic support.
  • Give heparin if DVT is confirmed.
PearlSuperficial thrombophlebitis is often considered a "benign" condition, but in pregnancy it is a marker of prothrombotic risk — confirmed DVT triggers a switch from local measures to therapeutic anticoagulation. The single most important clinical action: rule out DVT (compression duplex US) before settling on a management plan.

3Deep Venous Thrombosis (DVT)

Definition

A medical condition that occurs when a blood clot forms in a deep vein (in the lower leg, thigh, or pelvis).

Predisposing factors in pregnancy — Virchow's triad

Hypercoagulation

Increased procoagulant activity in pregnancy.

Vascular damage

Endothelial injury at delivery or surgery.

Venous stasis

Mechanical compression of the left iliac vein in pregnancy.

Prothrombotic changes in pregnancy

  • Increased procoagulants such as fibrinogen.
  • Decrease fibrinolysis like decrease protein S.
  • Mechanical factors such as compression of left iliac vein.
PearlThe mechanical compression of the left iliac vein by the right iliac artery and the gravid uterus (May–Thurner physiology) is the reason most DVTs in pregnancy are left-sided — the most common in deep veins of the left side (see §5 below).

PearlThe single most powerful predictor of a pregnancy-associated DVT is a prior VTE — recurrent risk is markedly elevated. Combined with thrombophilia (factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid syndrome, protein C/S deficiency), the recurrence risk may justify prophylactic LMWH throughout pregnancy.

5DVT Clinical Picture

Most common in deep veins of left side.

1

Fever.

2

Leg swelling and pain.

3

Hotness and cyanosis of the leg.

4

Tender calf muscle.

!

Positive Homan's sign (calf pain with foot dorsiflexion).

%

50 % of cases are asymptomatic.

Danger50 % of DVTs in pregnancy are asymptomatic — the absence of leg swelling or pain does not rule out DVT. A high index of suspicion is required, especially in patients with risk factors (see §4) or unexplained tachycardia, tachypnea, or hypoxia (consider concurrent PE).
NoteThe source contains the typographical variant "dorsiflexsion" (Homan's sign) — clinically the correct term is dorsiflexion. Preserved verbatim in card #5 for source-faithfulness, and corrected in the visual label.

6DVT Investigations

Compression Duplex ultrasonography

Venography

MRI in suspected pelvic veins thrombosis

If pulmonary embolism is obvious → start heparin immediately, perform perfusion (V/Q) scan, CT, MRI

CBC, coagulation profile, D-dimer

(Imaging tail — when PE suspected)

PearlCompression duplex ultrasonography is the first-line imaging modality for suspected DVT in pregnancy — non-invasive, no ionizing radiation, and high sensitivity for proximal lower-limb DVT. Venography and MRI are reserved for suspected pelvic-vein thrombosis (the duplex window for iliac and IVC thrombi is limited).
NoteThe "If pulmonary embolism is obvious → start heparin immediately" line in the source is presented as an investigation step in the original layout, but the action it describes (starting heparin) is a treatment decision that precedes definitive imaging. The card preserves the source's ordering while a NOTE callout in §10 (PE) reminds readers that PE is a clinical diagnosis that may require empirical anticoagulation before imaging returns.

7Management of DVT — Prophylactic & Active

A · Prophylactic Treatment

  • Early ambulation after delivery.
  • Prophylactic heparin in risky cases.

B · Active Management

  • Graduated compression stockings.
  • Start anticoagulant therapy (see §8 UFH and §9 LMWH).
PearlGraduated compression stockings are an adjunct to anticoagulation — they reduce post-thrombotic syndrome and edema but do not dissolve or prevent propagation of the clot. Anticoagulation (heparin, never warfarin in pregnancy) is the primary active treatment.

8Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)

Mode of action

  • Enhance antithrombin activity.
  • Increase factor Xa inhibitor activity.
  • Inhibit platelet aggregation.

Dose

Start with a bolus of 5,000 IU, then give IV Heparin at a dose of 15,000 IU by intravenous drip every 12 hours for 5–10 days, or until clinical improvement is noted, then give maintenance therapy by subcutaneous injection of 10,000 IU / 8–12 hours to keep a PTT 1.5–2.5 times control.

Complications

  • Hemorrhage.
  • Osteoporosis.
  • Thrombocytopenia.
  • Skin necrosis.

Antidote

1 % protamine sulphate solution 10 mg.

DangerHeparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) is a paradoxical, immune-mediated reaction in which heparin therapy itself triggers platelet activation and thrombosis. Platelet counts should be monitored periodically throughout UFH therapy; a > 50 % fall from baseline is a red flag and mandates immediate switch to a non-heparin anticoagulant (e.g. argatroban, fondaparinux).
PearlProtamine sulphate 1 % — 10 mg neutralizes approximately 1,000 IU of UFH (1 mg of protamine per 100 IU of heparin given in the last 2 hours). It is the only clinically available UFH antidote. There is no comparable effective antidote for LMWH (only partial reversal with protamine).

9Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH) — Enoxaparin

Mode of action

  • Inhibit factor Xa activity.
  • Given SC once or twice daily.

Advantages of LMWH over UFH

  • Better bioavailability.
  • Longer plasma half-life.
  • Lower risk of hemorrhagic complications.

Dose of Enoxaparin (Calexane)

Prophylactic dose: 40 mg daily.

Therapeutic dose: 1 mg / kg body weight / 12 hours.

Treatment in relation to pregnancy should continue for 6–12 weeks after delivery.

PearlEnoxaparin is the most commonly used LMWH in pregnancy because it does not cross the placenta (heparin molecules are too large to cross) and is not teratogenic. Warfarin is contraindicated in pregnancy (teratogenic, crosses placenta, causes warfarin embryopathy and fetal hemorrhage) — UFH and LMWH are the only safe options for anticoagulation in pregnancy.
NoteThe 6–12 week postpartum extension reflects the prolonged prothrombotic window of the puerperium — the maternal VTE risk remains elevated for at least 6 weeks after delivery, and remains above baseline until 12 weeks postpartum.

10Pulmonary Embolism

Clinical picture

Symptoms

  • Dyspnea.
  • Cough.
  • Chest pain.
  • Frothy blood-stained sputum.
  • Hemoptysis.

Signs

  • Mild pyrexia.
  • Tachycardia.
  • Tachypnea.
  • Cyanosis.
  • Raised jugular venous pressure.
  • Pleural friction.
  • Pleural effusion.

Investigations

Arterial blood gases (hypoxia and hypercapnea)

ECG

Ventilation / perfusion imaging

Chest spiral CT

Pulmonary angiography

MRA (magnetic resonance angiography)

Investigations for DVT (see §6)

— imaging tail —

Treatment

1

Stabilization of the patient

2

Start immediately anticoagulant (intravenous unfractionated heparin is the preferred treatment)

3

IVC filter

4

Thrombolysis

5

Pulmonary embolectomy

—

Last-resort surgical / interventional

DangerMassive PE (sustained hypotension, shock, cardiac arrest) is a clinical diagnosis — do not delay empirical anticoagulation while awaiting definitive imaging. The first two treatment steps (stabilization + IV UFH) are time-critical and should be initiated on clinical suspicion alone. Thrombolysis and pulmonary embolectomy are last-resort interventions reserved for massive PE with hemodynamic collapse.
NoteThe source's "IVC filter" step is included as a treatment option in pregnancy when anticoagulation is contraindicated (e.g. active hemorrhage, very recent surgery, or recurrent PE despite therapeutic anticoagulation). The filter traps thrombi before they reach the pulmonary circulation, but does not treat the existing clot.

NoteThe algorithm should branch by clinical syndrome: (1) superficial thrombophlebitis → exclude DVT → local measures; (2) suspected DVT → compression duplex US → therapeutic anticoagulation; (3) suspected PE → stabilize + IV UFH → imaging (V/Q or CTPA) → consider thrombolysis / embolectomy / IVC filter as indicated. Each branch should include pregnancy-specific dose adjustments and a delivery planning checkpoint.
