Diabetes Mellitus with Pregnancy

A carbohydrate-metabolism disorder in which pregnancy and diabetes interact in both directions — the disease threatens the fetus (macrosomia, anomalies, IUFD, neonatal hypoglycemia) and pregnancy modifies the disease (insulin requirements rise, microangiopathy worsens, ketoacidosis can occur at lower glucose levels).

2Definition & Incidence

Definition

A disorder in the carbohydrate metabolism characterized by hyperglycemia and glucosuria.

Incidence

  • 3–5 % of pregnancies are associated with gestational diabetes.
  • 0.5–1 % are complicated by pregestational diabetes.
PearlGestational diabetes is roughly 5–10× more common than pregestational diabetes, because the placenta is a strong insulin-resistant organ in the second half of pregnancy — any woman with even subtle pre-existing β-cell dysfunction will be unmasked.

3Classification

[A] Pregestational diabetes

Diabetes that exists before pregnancy.

  • Type 1 → Juvenile onset (autoimmune).
  • Type 2 → Adult onset (insulin resistance).

[B] Gestational diabetes

First diagnosed in pregnancy.

  • Definition: Carbohydrate intolerance of variable severity first diagnosed during pregnancy.
  • Classic GDM diagnosed at 24–28 weeks.
  • GDM diagnosed before 24 weeks → probably undiagnosed type 2 DM and is managed as pregestational diabetes.
NoteAny GDM diagnosed before the 24-week threshold is reclassified as pregestational because the placental insulin-resistant hormones have not yet had time to drive the hyperglycemia — so the disease must have pre-existed.

4Physiologic Changes During Pregnancy

  • Hyperplasia of the pancreatic islet cells which leads to a doubling in insulin production between the first and the third trimesters.
  • Initial increase in insulin sensitivity during first trimester.
  • Progressive glucose intolerance with advanced gestation due to release of insulin-resistant hormones (HPL, glucagon, progesterone and corticotrophin releasing hormone) from the placenta.
  • Increased glucose uptake of the fetus and increased peripheral uptake.
  • Increased glycogenesis and reduced hepatic gluconeogenesis.
  • Reduced renal tubular threshold for glucose → glucosuria.
  • Overall, fasting glucose levels fall by 10–20 %, and postprandial levels are higher.
PearlThe placenta is the main driver of pregnancy-induced insulin resistance via human placental lactogen (HPL) — HPL acts as a "counter-insulin" hormone to shunt glucose preferentially to the fetus. The clinical implication: insulin requirements rise through pregnancy and the diabetic woman's doses must be titrated upward.

5Effect of Pregnancy on Diabetes Mellitus

  • The disease may appear for the first time during pregnancy.
  • The insulin requirements gradually increase after the 3rd month until term and ketoacidosis can occur with lower levels of blood glucose.
  • Aggravation of diabetic microangiopathy (nephropathy, retinopathy & neuropathy).
  • During delivery, there is liability to hypoglycemia because of uterine activity.
  • After delivery, insulin requirement decreases due to drop in the placental hormones.
DangerDiabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) in pregnancy can occur at lower blood glucose levels than in non-pregnant patients (sometimes < 200 mg/dL) because of the accelerated starvation metabolism and the buffering effect of the fetus. Any unexplained nausea, vomiting, or Kussmaul breathing in a pregnant diabetic is DKA until proven otherwise.
WarnInsulin requirements fall sharply within hours of placental delivery — resume pre-pregnancy doses or lower in the immediate postpartum to avoid hypoglycemia.

6Effects of DM on Pregnancy

Fetal Risks

  • Miscarriage.
  • Congenital anomaly (doubled) (cardiovascular & NTDs).
  • Macrosomia.
  • Prematurity.
  • Shoulder dystocia & birth injury.
  • Respiratory distress.
  • Neonatal hypoglycemia.
  • IUFD.
  • IUGR.
  • Neonatal hyperbilirubinemia, hypocalcemia, and hypothermia.
  • Risk of having diabetes: 15 % in type 2 & 2 % in type 1.

Maternal Risks

  • Hypoglycemia.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis.
  • Polyhydramnios.
  • Obstructed labor.
  • Operative delivery.
  • Worsening of retinal disease.
  • Worsening of renal disease.
  • Pre-eclampsia.
  • Postpartum hemorrhage, and infection.
DangerThe single highest-yield maternal-fetal risk combination: poor first-trimester glycemic control → doubled congenital anomaly rate (cardiovascular & neural tube defects). This is the clinical rationale for preconception counseling and tight HbA1C < 6.5 % before pregnancy.
PearlMacrosomia (Pedersen hypothesis) is driven by fetal hyperinsulinemia in response to maternal hyperglycemia → anabolic growth. The same fetal hyperinsulinemia is what causes neonatal hypoglycemia in the first 24 h after delivery (insulin is still high but the maternal glucose supply is cut).

7Risk Factors for GDM

  • BMI > 30 kg/m².
  • Previous macrosomic infant ≥ 4.5 kg.
  • Previous G.D. (30–84 %).
  • First degree relative with diabetes.
  • Glycosuria 2+.
  • Unexplained IUFD.
  • Current macrosomia and polyhydramnios.
PearlThe two highest-yield risk factors for boards are prior macrosomic infant ≥ 4.5 kg and previous GDM (recurrence 30–84 %) — both indicate pre-existing β-cell dysfunction that pregnancy will unmask.

8Screening & Diagnosis — GCT & GTT

One-hour Glucose Challenge Test (GCT)

  • A 50-g one-hour glucose challenge test at 24 to 28 weeks of gestation.
  • Patients do not have to fast for this test.
  • To be considered normal, serum or plasma glucose values should be less than 140 mg/dL.
  • Positive screening test require confirmation with oral glucose tolerance test.

Three-hour Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (GTT)

  • If the GCT ≥ 140 mg/dL, then the 3-hour diagnostic GTT should be done by administering 100 g of glucose orally after fasting for 8 hours.
  • The American Diabetes Association has recommended the use of Carpenter and Coustan values.
  • If any two or more of the diagnostic values are met or exceeded, then the diagnosis of GDM is made.

Diagnostic Cut-offs (NDDG vs. Carpenter–Coustan)

Blood sample National Diabetes Data Group Carpenter and Coustan
Fasting 105 mg/dl (5.8 mmol/l) 95 mg/dl (5.3 mmol/l)
1-hour 190 mg/dl (10.5 mmol/l) 180 mg/dl (10.0 mmol/l)
2-hour 165 mg/dl (9.2 mmol/l) 155 mg/dl (8.6 mmol/l)
3-hour 145 mg/dl (8.0 mmol/l) 140 mg/dl (7.8 mmol/l)
NoteThe Carpenter–Coustan thresholds are lower than the older NDDG values, so use of Carpenter–Coustan detects more GDM. Any two or more of the four time-point values must be met or exceeded for the diagnosis.

9Management of Gestational DM

Antenatal care by:

  1. Frequent antenatal care visits.
  2. Glycemic control.

Targets for daily capillary plasma glucose

TimeTarget
Fasting95 mg/dl.
1 hour after meals140 mg/dl.
2 hours after meals115 mg/dl.

GDM is divided into two categories

I. GDM-A1 — Euglycemia achieved by diet alone

Diet: Daily intake of 1,800 to 2,400 kilocalories (30 kcal/kg/day) made up of:

  • 15 % to 20 % protein
  • 50 % to 60 % carbohydrates
  • 20 % fat
  • A nutritional consultation should be provided.

Exercise: Moderate exercise has been shown to lower maternal glucose concentrations in women with GDM.

II. GDM-A2 — Glycemic status inadequately controlled by diet alone

Glucose monitoring: is carried out using a glucometer. The patient should record fasting glucose values and 1-hour (or 2-hour) postprandial glucose values with each meal to determine the adequacy of diabetic control for goal values.

Goal values for adequacy of control:

  • Fasting: 60–90 mg/dl.
  • 1 hour postprandial: < 140 mg/dl.
  • 2 hours postprandial: < 120 mg/dl.
  • Bedtime: < 120 mg/dl.
  • 2:00–6:00 A.M.: 60–90 mg/dl.

Oral hypoglycemic agents: Metformin (an oral agent) for women who decline insulin therapy.

3 · Assessment of fetal wellbeing

Ultrasound and nonstress test in the last trimester every 2 weeks.

PearlThe mnemonic "A1 = diet, A2 = diet + meds" captures the entire GDM classification. GDM-A1 is by far the more common and milder form.

10Management of Pregestational DM

HbA1C

HbA1C reflects glycemic control over the past 8 to 12 weeks, and is used to assess risk of fetal anomalies in the first trimester of pregnancy. Measurement of HbA1C is advised to be done every trimester. A diagnosis of overt diabetes is made when A1C is ≥ 6.5 %.

Insulin dosage — the two dose regimen (the classical method)

  • Depending on the maternal weight and recorded glucose levels, the insulin dosage should be initiated as follows:

1st Trimester

0.7

units / kg

2nd Trimester

0.8

units / kg

3rd Trimester

0.9 – 1.1

units / kg

  • Total daily dose should be divided in half, given every morning and evening.
Morning dose (before breakfast)

Two-thirds of the dose is given as intermediate acting insulin (NPH), and one-third of the dose is given as a rapid acting insulin.

Evening dose (before dinner)

One-half of the dose is given as NPH and one-half of the dose is given as rapid acting insulin.

PearlThe morning dose is 2/3 NPH + 1/3 rapid (NPH-heavy to cover breakfast and lunch); the evening dose is ½ NPH + ½ rapid (more balanced to avoid overnight hypoglycemia).

11Management of Labor & Delivery

Timing of delivery

TimingIndication
At 38 weeks In controlled DM with no fetal or maternal complications.
Delivery before 38 w In uncontrolled DM, fetal complications (as IUGR), maternal complications (as preeclampsia).

Route of delivery

  • Induction of labor: in controlled DM (no maternal or fetal indications as macrosomia or malpresentation).
  • C.S.: uncontrolled DM, maternal or fetal complications, failed induction, macrosomia, malpresentations, presence of obstetric indication.

Glucose control and monitoring during labor

  • The patient must be euglycemic during the intrapartum period because maternal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinemia → neonatal hypoglycemia → neonatal seizures and death.
  • The infusion fluid is adjusted based on blood glucose levels. Short-acting insulin boluses may be added to bring glucose levels to the target range of 80 to 100 mg/dl. Blood glucose values should be checked every 1 to 2 hours.
  • The patient should receive her normal insulin dose during the previous evening. On the morning of her induction, the patient's usual insulin dose should be withheld.

Management of elective cesarean section

  • The patient should withhold her morning insulin dose. Glucose levels should be monitored frequently during and immediately after surgery.
DangerThe Pedersen cascade (maternal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinemia → neonatal hypoglycemia → seizures & death) is the entire rationale for tight intrapartum glucose control (80–100 mg/dL, checked every 1–2 h). Failure to achieve this is a leading cause of preventable neonatal morbidity.

12Postpartum Care, Breastfeeding & Contraception

Glucose monitoring

  • After delivery, glucose levels should be checked every 4 to 6 hours, while administering 5 % dextrose with lactated Ringer's or normal saline (at approximately 125 mL/hr).
  • During the initial postpartum period, short-acting insulin is used only when glucose levels are higher than 150 mg/dL.
  • Once the patient is taking a full diabetic diet, insulin can be started at one third to one-half of the antepartum dosage.

Breastfeeding

  • Breastfeeding increases the frequency of hypoglycemia in insulin-dependent diabetics, thus women should have a snack before or during breastfeeding.
  • Women with pre-existing type 2 diabetes can safely take metformin while breastfeeding.

Contraception

  • Progesterone only methods and mechanical methods can be used.
  • The IUCD can cause pelvic inflammatory disease and combined pills are contraindicated.

Postpartum evaluation in GDM

  • In the postpartum period, a woman with GDM should have a follow-up fasting blood glucose and 2-hour 75-g glucose tolerance test (6 to 12 weeks postpartum) to assess for persistent diabetes.
  • If the threshold values are met or exceeded in postpartum testing, the patient is given a diagnosis of type 2 diabetes.
PearlCombined oral contraceptives are contraindicated in diabetic women (estrogen + progestin increase thrombotic and vascular risk in a population already predisposed). Progesterone-only pills (POP) and barrier methods are the preferred options.
