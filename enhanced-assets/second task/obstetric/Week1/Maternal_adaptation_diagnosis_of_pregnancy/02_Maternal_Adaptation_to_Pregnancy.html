Intended Learning Objectives (ILOs)

At the end of this chapter, the student should be able to:

  • Understand the genital and other maternal organs changes during pregnancy to help body adaptation to this important event of woman life.
  • Describe the hematological, metabolic and endocrinal changes of pregnancy.

1. Genital Changes

I. Whole Uterus

  • Position: Dextrorotation.
  • Size: 2.5 × 5 × 7.5 cm (non-pregnant) → 20 × 25 × 35 cm at term.
  • Shape: Pyriform (non-pregnant) → Globular at 8 weeks → Pyriform at 16 weeks till term.
  • Weight: 50 g (non-pregnant) → 1000 g at term.
  • Volume: 10 mL (non-pregnant) → 5000 mL at term.
  • Consistency: softer due to increased vascularity and presence of amniotic fluid.
  • Contractility: Braxton Hicks contractions that cause discomfort late in pregnancy and may account for false labor pains.

II. Myometrial Changes

  • Hypertrophy: estrogenic effect.
  • Hyperplasia: progesterone effect.
  • Isthmus: area of the cervix between anatomical internal os above and the histological internal os below. It is 4–5 mm and lined by atrophic endometrium.
Significance — Lower Uterine Segment

The isthmus and lower ½ inch of the body of the uterus develop to form the lower uterine segment, which starts to be formed at 12 weeks and gradually increases to 10 cm at full term.

III. Uterine Vasculature

Uterine artery lumen is doubled and uterine blood flow increases progressively, reaching about 500 mL/min at term.

IV. Cervix

Hypertrophy, soft and bluish due to increased vascularity.

V. Vagina

Violet and edematous due to increased vascularity.

VI. Vulva

Violet and edematous due to increased vascularity.

VII. Fallopian Tube

Elongated, tortuous.

VIII. Ovary

Increased size due to increased vascularity, particularly the ovary that contains the corpus luteum.

2. Hematological Changes

2.1 Blood Volume

Increases gradually above the non-pregnant level by 30%–40%, reaching its maximum by 32 weeks, then remains constant till full term.

  • Plasma volume: 45% increase.
  • Red cell mass: 30% increase.

The increase in plasma volume is more than the increase in red blood cell mass, resulting in hemodilution (physiological anemia).

2.2 Blood Indices

  • RBCs and hemoglobin concentrations decrease due to hemodilution.
  • WBCs increase from 7,000 /mm³ to 10,500 /mm³, and up to 16,000 /mm³ during labor.

2.3 Coagulation Factors

  • Platelets: increase or decrease (controversial).
  • Fibrinogen: increases nearly doubled (from 200–400 mg% to 400–600 mg%).
  • Factor VIII: tripled.
  • Factor VII and X: doubled.
  • Fibrinolytic activity: decreased.
Warning — Hypercoagulable State

Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state — increased clotting factors + decreased fibrinolysis → ↑ VTE risk.

3. Cardiovascular System

3.1 Heart

  • Position: the uterus elevates the diaphragm and the apex beat is displaced upwards and to the left.
  • Pulse rate: increases by 10 beats /min.
  • Heart sounds: splitting of the 1st heart sound and appearance of 3rd heart sound.

Most of the changes of the pulse and heart sound are due to hyperdynamic circulation caused by decreased peripheral resistance from peripheral vasodilation due to increased progesterone during pregnancy.

3.2 Cardiac Output (COP)

Increases above the non-pregnant level to reach its maximum of 40% above the non-pregnant level at 20 weeks, then remains constant till term (6.5 L/min). This is due to increased stroke volume and heart rate.

During labor: COP increases more, especially during the second stage of labor, due to uterine contractions and bearing-down efforts.

3.3 Arterial Blood Pressure

Although COP is increased, blood pressure is decreased, especially in the second trimester, due to decreased peripheral resistance.

4. Respiratory System

4.1 Anatomical Changes

The enlarged uterus displaces the diaphragm up to 4 cm, leading to reduced diaphragmatic mobility and a widened subcostal angle.

4.2 Respiratory Functions

  • Tidal volume (amount of gas inspired or expired in each respiration) is increased throughout pregnancy by about 40%.
  • Residual volume is reduced.

4.3 Hyperventilation

Due to increased tidal volume from increased sensitivity of the respiratory center to excess progesterone.

5. Urinary System

5.1 Kidney

Renal blood flow and glomerular filtration rate (GFR) increase by 50%. This leads to increased excretion, so:

  • Decreased creatinine.
  • Decreased urea.
  • Increased excretion of glucose → reduced renal glucose threshold → renal glucosuria.

5.2 Ureters

Dilated due to the relaxing effect of progesterone and pressure against the pelvic brim by the uterus, especially on the right side due to dextrorotation of the uterus.

5.3 Urinary Bladder

Frequency is common in early pregnancy due to pressure on the bladder by the enlarging uterus, congestion of the bladder mucosa, and increased glomerular filtration rate.

6. Gastrointestinal Tract

  • Gingivitis: due to increased vascularity and hypertrophy of interdental papillae.
  • Ptyalism: increased salivation due to increased vascularity of salivary glands.
  • Appetite changes (longing): the pregnant woman likes some foods. Reduced taste buds create the desire for markedly sweet, sour and salt foods.
  • Nausea (morning sickness) and vomiting (emesis gravidarum): starts between 4th and 6th weeks, improves by about 12th week of pregnancy.
  • Heart burn: due to reflux of acidic gastric contents to the esophagus from the relaxing effect of progesterone on the esophageal sphincter.
  • Indigestion and flatulence: due to reduced gastric motility and reflux of alkaline intestinal contents to the stomach from the relaxing effect of progesterone.
  • Constipation: due to reduced large-intestinal motility from the relaxing effect of progesterone.
  • Piles: due to laxity of the wall of the veins by progesterone.
  • Appendix: is displaced upward and laterally.
  • Gall bladder: more tendency for gall bladder stones due to delayed emptying of the gall bladder.
  • Liver: decreased albumin and increased globulin (↓ A/G ratio) and increased alkaline phosphatase enzyme.
Clinical Pearl — Progesterone Is the Common Denominator

Many of the GI/urinary smooth-muscle changes of pregnancy (heartburn, constipation, piles, ureteric dilatation, biliary stasis) are driven by the same mechanism: progesterone-induced smooth-muscle relaxation.

7. Metabolic Changes

  • Weight gain: increased by 10–12 kg in pregnancy.
  • Water metabolism: tendency to water and sodium retention due to increased aldosterone.
  • Protein metabolism: positive nitrogen balance and increased daily requirements for protein intake.
  • Carbohydrate: mild fasting hypoglycemia and postprandial hyperglycemia. Pregnancy is diabetogenic due to pregnancy hormones.
  • Lipid: increased levels of total lipids and cholesterol.

9. Endocrine Changes

  • Anterior pituitary: hypertrophy and increased vascularity, and increased number of prolactin-secreting cells.
  • Thyroid gland: slight enlargement of the gland due to increased vascularity → increased gland activity: ↑ TBG, ↑ total T3 and T4, ↓ TSH, and ↑ BMR.
  • Parathyroid gland: hypertrophy with increased demand for calcium.
  • Suprarenal gland: hypertrophy due to increased vascularity leading to increased aldosterone and cortisol.

10. Skin Changes

  • Palmar erythema: due to increased estrogen.
  • Chloasma gravidarum: on the face.
  • Linea nigra pigmentation: in midline below the umbilicus.
  • Stria gravidarum: pigmentation in the lower abdomen flanks, inner thigh and buttocks due to mechanical stretching.

11. Breast Changes

I. First Month

  • Increased size and vascularity.
  • Mastodynia (from tingling to frank pain).

II. Second Month — Skin Pigmentation

  • Increased pigmentation of nipple and primary areola.
  • Montgomery tubercles: hypertrophy of the lips of the orifices of the lacteal ducts.

Colostrum secretion.
