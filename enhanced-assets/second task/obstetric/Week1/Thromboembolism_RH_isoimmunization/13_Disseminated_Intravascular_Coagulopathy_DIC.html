Disseminated Intravascular Coagulopathy (DIC)

A consumption coagulopathy unique to its obstetric triggers — abruptio placentae, amniotic fluid embolism, severe preeclampsia, vesicular mole, and IUFD. Recognizing the lab pattern (↑PT/↑PTT/↑D-dimer, ↓platelets/↓fibrinogen) early is the difference between life and death.

2Definition of DIC

Definition

A disorder of hemostasis in which intravascular activation of both clotting and fibrinolytic systems occurs leading to consumption of coagulation factors and platelets.

DangerDIC is a consumption coagulopathy — both the clotting and the fibrinolytic cascades are activated simultaneously, so the patient bleeds and micro-thromboses at the same time. The single best description is "running out of coagulation factors while the system is still turned on."

4Etiology — Obstetric Triggers

DIC in obstetrics is almost always secondary — the trigger is one of a small list of well-defined obstetric events.

1

Preeclampsia and eclampsia.

2

Amniotic fluid embolism.

3

Abruptio placenta.

4

Vesicular mole.

5

IUFD and missed abortion.

6

Incompatible blood transfusion.

7

Rupture uterus and placenta accreta.

PearlThe two highest-yield triggers to memorize are abruptio placentae and amniotic fluid embolism — both release massive thromboplastin into the maternal circulation and can flip a stable patient into fulminant DIC within minutes.
PearlRetained dead fetus syndrome is the chronic form — low-grade consumptive coagulopathy over days, classically seen with IUFD retained > 4 weeks. Coagulopathy corrects within hours of delivery of the fetus.

5Investigations — Lab Pattern

The classical DIC panel is a mirror image — clotting factors up, the components that get consumed down.

TestDirection in DICMechanism
Prothrombin time (PT) ↑ Increased Consumption of extrinsic-pathway factors (V, VII, X, II / fibrinogen).
Partial thromboplastin time (PTT) ↑ Increased Consumption of intrinsic-pathway factors (VIII, IX, XI, XII).
Fibrin degradation products (D-dimer) ↑ Increased Fibrinolysis of cross-linked fibrin — the most sensitive single marker.
Platelet count ↓ Decreased Consumed in micro-thrombi and by splenic/hepatic sequestration.
Fibrinogen ↓ Decreased Converted to fibrin faster than the liver can replace it.
PearlThe single most useful test to order first is the D-dimer — a normal D-dimer effectively rules out DIC in pregnancy (it has very high negative predictive value). A fibrinogen < 100 mg/dL in pregnancy is highly suggestive of DIC, because normal pregnancy fibrinogen is elevated (400–600 mg/dL).

6Treatment

Three pillars — and the order matters: stop the trigger first, then replace what's been consumed.

1 · Treatment of the cause

Remove the obstetric trigger (deliver the fetus in abruptio, evacuate the mole, deliver in IUFD, etc.).

2 · Replacement transfusion

Transfusion of fresh blood, fresh frozen plasma, cryoprecipitate and platelets.

3 · Anticoagulant

Used selectively — typically low-dose heparin in chronic consumptive states (e.g., retained IUFD, vesicular mole) to slow further consumption. Avoid in active hemorrhage.

DangerTreatment of the cause comes first — no amount of plasma or platelets will stop DIC if the trigger (e.g., retained dead fetus, undiagnosed abruption, molar tissue) is still in place. Delivery of the fetus in abruptio placentae is itself the cure.
WarnAnticoagulation in DIC is a double-edged sword — useful in chronic low-grade consumptive states, but dangerous in fulminant obstetric DIC with active bleeding. Junior clinicians often over-anticoagulate. Follow local Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP) and hematology guidance.
