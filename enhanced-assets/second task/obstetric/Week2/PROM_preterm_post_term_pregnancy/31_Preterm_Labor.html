Preterm Labor

Regular uterine contractions associated with cervical change occurring between 20 and 37 weeks' gestation — the leading cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality worldwide. This chapter covers the definition (preterm labor vs premature infant), the maternal and fetal causes/risk factors (with idiopathic as the most common), clinical diagnosis (symptoms & signs + ultrasound/TUS + fetal fibronectin), prophylaxis (smoking cessation, cerclage, progesterone), curative management (bed rest, hydration, antenatal corticosteroids, tocolytics, antibiotics), the 5 classes of tocolytic drugs with doses and contraindications, GBS antibiotic prophylaxis, and mode of delivery (cephalic → vaginal; breech → vaginal only if > 34 weeks, else cesarean).

2Definition (preterm labor + premature infant)

📋 Preterm Labor (verbatim from source)

"Regular uterine contractions associated with cervical change occurring between 20-37 weeks' gestation."

It means onset of labor (regular painful uterine contractions associated with effacement and the dilatation of the cervix) prior to completion of 37 weeks.

Premature infant: infant born before 37 completed weeks of gestation.

🔑 KEY DEFINITION — memorize this

The 20-week lower bound distinguishes preterm labor from late miscarriage. The 37-week upper bound distinguishes preterm labor from term labor. Effacement + dilatation distinguishes true labor from Braxton-Hicks contractions.

3Causes & risk factors — Maternal

⚠️ MEMORY ANCHOR — Most common cause

Most common cause of PTL is idiopathic — i.e., when no specific risk factor is identified, idiopathic remains the leading cause.

Previous preterm laborA history of preterm delivery in a prior pregnancy is a major recurrent risk factor.
Local or systemic infectionIncludes UTI, bacterial vaginosis, chorioamnionitis, periodontal disease, and any systemic infection that may trigger prostaglandin release.
Congenital anomalies of uterus & incompetent osSeptate uterus, bicornuate uterus, diethylstilbestrol (DES) exposure, and cervical incompetence (incompetent os).
Smoking, obesity, diabetes, low SESLifestyle and metabolic risk factors that cluster together in low-socioeconomic-status populations.
Uterine overdistensionMultiple gestations (twins, triplets) and polyhydramnios mechanically stretch the myometrium.
Maternal age extremesAge < 18 years or > 35 years — both ends of the reproductive spectrum carry elevated risk.
Second- or third-trimester bleedingPlacenta previa or placental abruption — bleeding triggers uterine irritability and prostaglandin release.

5Diagnosis — Symptoms & signs

Symptoms & signs (verbatim from source)

🤰 Clinical features of preterm labor

  • Uterine contractions occurring at a frequency of 4 in 20 minutes or 8 in 60 minutes.
  • Lower back pain — often dull, rhythmic, radiating to the lower abdomen.
  • Passage of blood-stained vaginal discharge (show) — release of the cervical mucus plug, often blood-tinged.
  • Sensation of pelvic pressure — feeling that the fetus is pushing down.
  • Bulging membranes / rupture of membranes — visible bulging of the forewaters or frank ROM (PROM).

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — "4 in 20 / 8 in 60" rule

This contraction-frequency threshold is the textbook definition of active preterm labor — memorize it because it is the trigger to start tocolysis + corticosteroids. Below this frequency, Braxton-Hicks contractions or false labor are more likely.

6Diagnosis — Investigations

1. Ultrasound (obstetric)

🩺 What ultrasound evaluates in suspected PTL

  • Identify obvious fetal malformations.
  • Identify abnormalities in fetal growth.
  • Identify abnormalities in amniotic fluid volume (oligohydramnios or polyhydramnios).
  • Confirm fetal presentation to aid in planning mode of delivery.
  • Evaluate the placenta and its location.

A thorough obstetrical ultrasound is helpful in evaluating the etiology of preterm labour.

2. Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS)

🔬 Transvaginal cervical assessment

  • Dilatation of cervix > 2 cm.
  • Shape of cervix on USG: there is bulging of fetal membranes into a widened internal os in case of PTL.
  • Cervical shape progression: the normal shape of cervix is T shape and it changes to Y shape then to V shape and finally to U shape.

📝 MEMORY ANCHOR — Cervical shape progression

T → Y → V → U — the cervix progressively shortens and opens as labor approaches. A U-shaped cervix on TVUS = imminent delivery.

3. Fetal fibronectin test

🧪 Fetal fibronectin assay

Presence of fibronectin glycoprotein produced by fetal amnion in the cervico-vaginal discharge between 24 and 34 weeks is a predictor of PTL.

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — Fibronectin window

The test is useful only between 24 and 34 weeks — before 24 weeks, fibronectin may normally be present; after 34 weeks, its presence is no longer predictive of preterm labor because term delivery is imminent regardless.

7Management — Prophylactic

Prophylactic measures (verbatim from source)

  1. Stop smoking.
  2. Cerclage surgery if cervical incompetence is present.
  3. Progesterone.

📝 Note on cerclage

Cerclage (e.g., McDonald or Shirodkar suture) is placed prophylactically in women with a history of cervical insufficiency, typically at 12–14 weeks, and removed at 36–37 weeks. It mechanically keeps the cervical os closed.

8Management — Curative

I. Bed rest and hydration

Hydration, both orally and intravenously. Bed rest reduces the gravitational and mechanical stimulus on the cervix.

II. Drug therapy

  • Antenatal corticosteroids.
  • Tocolytic therapy.
  • Antibiotic therapy.

⚠️ Sequence of drug therapy

In established preterm labor, all three drug-therapy pillars are often combined: corticosteroids (for fetal lung maturity), tocolytics (to delay delivery 48 hours — the time window corticosteroids need to act), and antibiotics (for GBS prophylaxis).

9Antenatal Corticosteroids

💊 Purpose

Corticosteroids decrease the chance of respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) and other prematurity complications (IVH, NEC, neonatal mortality).

Dose of corticosteroids

Betamethasone

2 doses of 12 mg, intramuscularly, 24 hours apart.

Betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 (24 h apart)

Dexamethasone

4 doses of 6 mg, intramuscularly, 12 hours apart.

Dexamethasone 6 mg IM × 4 (12 h apart)

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — Why 48 hours?

Corticosteroids need ~48 hours to complete their effect on fetal lung surfactant production. This is also why tocolytics are given — to buy those 48 hours even when labor is actively established.

10Tocolytic drugs & contraindications

Drugs used for tocolysis (5 classes)

  1. Prostaglandin synthetase inhibitor: Indomethacin.
  2. Calcium channel blocker: Nifedipine.
  3. Oxytocin antagonist: Atosiban.
  4. Magnesium sulfate.
  5. Nitric oxide donor: Glyceral trinitrite.
  6. Beta agonist: Ritodrine, Terbutalin.

Contraindications of tocolytics

🚫 Absolute contraindications (tocolysis must NOT be used)

  • Chorioamnionitis.
  • Advanced labor.
  • Pregnancy > 34 weeks.
  • Pre-eclampsia and eclampsia.
  • Abruptio placenta.
  • Fetal demise and distress.
  • Congenital anomaly of fetus not compatible with life.

🚨 DANGER — when NOT to use tocolytics

In each of the 7 conditions above, attempting to delay delivery would harm the mother or fetus (e.g., infection would worsen, abruption would hemorrhage, lethal fetal anomaly would prolong suffering). Always rule out these conditions before starting tocolysis.

11Tocolytic dosing details

Calcium channel blocker — Nifedipine (Nifidipine in source)

  • Initial: 20 mg orally, followed by another 20 mg after 30 minutes.
  • Maintenance: 20 mg every 4-8 hours (maximal 160 mg/day).
  • Side effects: Low incidence — hypotension, dizziness & flushing.
Nifedipine 20 mg PO → 20 mg PO @ 30 min → 20 mg PO q4-8h (max 160 mg/day)

Prostaglandin synthetase inhibitor — Indomethacin

  • Loading: 50-100 mg twice daily orally or rectally.
  • Maintenance: 25-50 mg orally every 6 hours for 48 hours.
  • Limit: Not used after 30 weeks' gestation (risk of premature closure of ductus arteriosus).
Indomethacin 50-100 mg PO/PR → 25-50 mg PO q6h × 48h (stop @ 30 wk)

Beta agonists — Ritodrine

  • Dangerous complications — heart failure (and other β-adrenergic side effects: tachycardia, pulmonary edema, hyperglycemia).
  • Historical use only; largely abandoned because of maternal cardiotoxicity.
⚠️ Use with extreme caution — risk of heart failure

Oxytocin antagonist — Atosiban

  • Bolus: 6.75 mg IV.
  • Infusion: 300 μg/min IV for 3 hours, then 100 μg/min.
  • Drawback: Very expensive.
Atosiban 6.75 mg IV bolus → 300 μg/min × 3h → 100 μg/min

Magnesium sulphate

  • Loading dose: 4-6 g IV.
  • Maintenance: 1-2 g IV for 24-48 hours.
  • Also provides fetal neuroprotection (reduces risk of cerebral palsy in preterm infants < 32 weeks).
MgSO4 4-6 g IV load → 1-2 g IV × 24-48 h (monitor reflexes + RR + urine output)

⚠️ Note on the source spelling "Nifidipine"

The source text spells the calcium channel blocker as "Nifidipine" — this is a typographic error. The correct INN (international nonproprietary name) is Nifedipine. The corrected spelling is used in the dosing card; the source spelling is preserved verbatim in the references block for transparency.

12Antibiotic therapy (GBS) + Mode of delivery

Antibiotic therapy for prevention of group B streptococci infection

🦠 GBS prophylaxis in established PTL

Benzyl penicillin or ampicillin should be given for an established PTL as a prophylaxis against early onset of neonatal sepsis due to group B streptococcus.

In cases of penicillin allergy, clindamycin can be used.

Mode of delivery

Cephalic presentation

With cephalic presentation, the preferred mode is vaginal delivery even if delivery at < 34 weeks.

Breech presentation

Vaginal delivery is allowed if more than 34 weeks, but if < 34 weeks, cesarean section is done.

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — 34-week rule for breech PTL

Before 34 weeks, the preterm fetal head and body are disproportionately small and fragile — vaginal breech delivery carries higher risk of head entrapment, intracranial hemorrhage, and birth trauma. Cesarean is the safer route. After 34 weeks, fetal size is large enough for safe vaginal breech delivery.
