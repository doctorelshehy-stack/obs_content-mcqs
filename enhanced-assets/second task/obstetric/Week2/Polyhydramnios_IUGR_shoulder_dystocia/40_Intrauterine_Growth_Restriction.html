Definition

KEY DEFINITION A fetus with a fetal abdominal circumference (AC) or estimated fetal weight (EFW) less than the 10th centile for its gestational age.

The fetus may be constitutionally small without being at increased risk of complications. Fetuses with IUGR have a 2- to 6-fold increase in perinatal morbidity and mortality.

WARN IUGR is not simply "small for gestational age" (SGA). A constitutionally small fetus has normal growth velocity and Doppler studies. IUGR implies pathological growth restriction with abnormal Doppler findings.

Types
1 — Asymmetric IUGR

Disproportionate growth restriction with a greater decrease in fetal body and limbs compared to head circumference.

  • Caused by extrinsic factors such as placental insufficiency.
  • Oxygen and nutrients are directed towards vital organs (brain and heart) by passing other organs (e.g. liver, muscle, and fat tissue).
  • Affects the fetus later in gestation.
2 — Symmetric IUGR

Proportional growth restriction in all parts of the fetus.

  • Caused by intrinsic factors such as genetic abnormalities and intrauterine infections.
  • Affects the fetus early in gestation.
MEMORY ANCHOR A-S-L-E: Asymmetric = Spare the head, Later in gestation, Extrinsic (placental). Symmetric = Small everywhere, Early in gestation, Intrinsic (genetic/infection).
CLINICAL PEARL Asymmetric IUGR shows "brain-sparing" — the fetus preferentially shunts blood to the brain and heart. This produces a high head circumference-to-abdominal circumference ratio.

Etiology — Maternal Risk Factors
1 — Maternal Risk Factors

Risk factors for IUGR can be divided into maternal, uteroplacental and fetal risk factors.

  • Maternal medical conditions: pre-existing diabetes mellitus, chronic hypertension, gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, systemic lupus erythematosus, antiphospholipid syndrome, sickle cell disease and severe anemia.
  • Substance use: tobacco, alcohol, cocaine, or narcotics.
  • Exposure to teratogenic drugs: ACE-inhibitors, warfarin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, cyclophosphamide or valproic acid.
  • Previous pregnancy with IUGR.
MEMORY ANCHOR M-S-T-P: Medical conditions (DM, HTN, pre-eclampsia, SLE, APS, SCD, anemia) → Substance use (tobacco, alcohol, cocaine, narcotics) → Teratogenic drugs (ACE-i, warfarin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, cyclophosphamide, valproic acid) → Previous IUGR.

Etiology — Placental & Fetal Risk Factors
2 — Placental Risk Factors
  • Placental insufficiency.
  • Placenta previa or placental abruption.
  • Multiple gestation.
3 — Fetal Risk Factors
  • Congenital/early intrauterine infections: toxoplasmosis, rubella, cytomegalovirus infection, varicella, herpes infections, HIV infection, syphilis, or malaria.
  • Genetic abnormalities: aneuploidy.
  • Congenital anomalies: cyanotic congenital heart disease.
DANGER TORCH infections (Toxoplasmosis, Other [syphilis, varicella, parvovirus], Rubella, CMV, Herpes) are a major fetal cause of symmetric IUGR — early screening and treatment can prevent severe growth restriction.

Diagnosis — History & Clinical Examination
History

A thorough obstetric history is crucial to identify any risk factors for IUGR.

Clinical Examination
  • Symphysial-fundal height (SFH) is decreased compared to gestational age (at least 3 cm less than gestational age in weeks).
  • Fetus is small for gestational age.
  • Fetal movements are reduced or absent.
WARN SFH lag of ≥ 3 cm below gestational age in weeks is the classic clinical sign. However, SFH measurement has poor sensitivity — serial measurements and ultrasound are more reliable.
CLINICAL PEARL Reduced or absent fetal movements in the context of a small-for-dates fetus should raise immediate suspicion of IUGR with fetal compromise — this is an obstetric emergency.

Investigations
Serial Ultrasound Scans

To assess fetal biometry and estimated fetal weight. It also provides detailed information on fetal anatomy, placental morphology, and amniotic fluid volume.

Findings may include:

  • Decreased fetal growth and fetal weight
  • Small placenta
  • Oligohydramnios
Umbilical Artery Doppler

Shows abnormalities such as reduced or reversed diastolic flow.

Biophysical Profile (BPP)

A non-invasive test that examines many parameters to assess fetal well-being.

Typical findings in IUGR include:

  • Oligohydramnios
  • Absent fetal breathing movements
  • Decreased fetal movements and tone
DANGER A BPP score of ≤ 4 indicates the need for delivery.
MEMORY ANCHOR U-A-B: Umbilical Doppler (reduced/reversed diastolic flow) → Amniotic fluid (oligohydramnios) → BPP (≤ 4 = deliver). These three investigations form the IUGR assessment triad.

Complications
Perinatal Complications
  • Preterm labor and delivery.
  • Stillbirth.
  • Perinatal asphyxia.
  • Necrotising enterocolitis.
  • Cognitive delay and behavioral issues.
  • Adult-onset diseases (e.g. diabetes mellitus, obesity, coronary artery disease, hypertension).
  • Motor and neurological disabilities.
  • Intrauterine/neonatal death.
DANGER IUGR is not just a perinatal problem — it programmes the fetus for adult-onset diseases (Barker hypothesis). Adults who were IUGR fetuses have increased risks of diabetes, obesity, coronary artery disease, and hypertension.
CLINICAL PEARL The Barker hypothesis (thrifty phenotype): fetal undernutrition leads to permanent metabolic adaptations that increase risk of chronic disease in adulthood — IUGR is a lifelong condition, not just a delivery concern.

Management — Antenatal · Intranatal · Postnatal
A — Antenatal
  1. Treatment of the underlying cause and no smoking.
  2. Monitoring of fetal wellbeing.
  3. Good diet.
B — Intranatal
  1. Mode of delivery: Cesarean section is the rule as the fetus does not tolerate the reduced oxygen supply and the birth trauma encountered during vaginal delivery.
  2. Continuous intranatal monitoring.
WARN Cesarean section is the rule — the IUGR fetus has limited oxygen reserves and cannot tolerate the hemodynamic stress of labor and vaginal delivery.
C — Postnatal

Identification and management of problems of dysmaturity.

NOTE Postnatal management focuses on dysmaturity complications: hypoglycemia, hypothermia, polycythemia, feeding difficulties, and long-term neurodevelopmental follow-up.
