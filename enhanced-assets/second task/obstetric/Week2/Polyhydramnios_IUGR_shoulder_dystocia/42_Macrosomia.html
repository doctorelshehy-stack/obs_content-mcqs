2 Definition

Macrosomia: A fetal weight > 4,000 grams.

3 Risk factors

  • History of fetal macrosomia.
  • Maternal obesity.
  • Excessive weight gain during pregnancy.
  • Previous pregnancies: The risk increases with each pregnancy.
  • Male infants: typically weigh slightly more than female infants.
  • Maternal age: Women older than 35 are more likely to have a baby diagnosed with fetal macrosomia.
📝 Question 1 of 5
Which is a risk factor for fetal macrosomia?
Risk factors: history of macrosomia, obesity, excessive weight gain, multiparity, male infants, and maternal age > 35.
📝 Question 2 of 5

Male infants typically weigh slightly more than female infants, increasing macrosomia risk.
📝 Question 3 of 5

The risk of macrosomia increases with each subsequent pregnancy.

4 Causes

  1. Genetic factors.
  2. Maternal conditions such as diabetes can cause fetal macrosomia.
  3. Multiparity.
  4. Postterm pregnancy.
📝 Question 1 of 5
Which maternal condition can cause fetal macrosomia?
Maternal diabetes can cause fetal macrosomia. Other causes: genetic factors, multiparity, and postterm pregnancy.
📝 Question 2 of 5
All are causes of macrosomia EXCEPT:
Placental insufficiency is associated with IUGR, not macrosomia.
📝 Question 3 of 5
Which is a cause of macrosomia?
Postterm pregnancy causes continued fetal growth beyond term, resulting in a large-for-gestational-age fetus.

5 Clinical picture

  • Large fundal height.
  • Excessive amniotic fluid (polyhydramnios).
📝 Question 1 of 5
Which is a clinical feature of macrosomia?
Clinical features: large fundal height and excessive amniotic fluid (polyhydramnios).
📝 Question 2 of 5
Which finding on examination may suggest macrosomia?
Polyhydramnios (excessive AF) is a clinical feature of macrosomia along with large fundal height.
📝 Question 3 of 5
The most common presenting symptom is:
Clinical presentation varies — pain and bleeding are common but not universal symptoms.

6 Complications

  • Prolonged and obstructed labor.
  • Shoulder dystocia.
  • Fetal injuries.
  • Meconium aspiration syndrome.
  • Genital tract lacerations.
  • Postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Uterine rupture.
📝 Question 1 of 5
Which is a maternal complication of macrosomia?
PPH is a maternal complication. Fetal injuries, meconium aspiration, and shoulder dystocia are fetal/neonatal complications.
📝 Question 2 of 5
Shoulder dystocia in macrosomia can cause:
Shoulder dystocia can cause fetal injuries including fractures and brachial plexus injuries.
📝 Question 3 of 5
All are complications of macrosomia EXCEPT:
IUGR/fetal growth restriction is the opposite condition. Macrosomia complications include shoulder dystocia, fetal injuries, PPH, and uterine rupture.

7 Treatment

  1. Proper antenatal care: to prevent macrosomia and diagnose it before labor.
  2. Cesarean section: is safe for both mother and fetus.
  3. Postnatal: proper examination of the neonate and management of any problem.
📝 Question 1 of 5
Which delivery mode is recommended for macrosomia?
Cesarean section is safe for both mother and fetus in cases of macrosomia.
📝 Question 2 of 5
Which is part of antenatal management of macrosomia?
Proper antenatal care aims to prevent macrosomia and diagnose it before labor.
📝 Question 3 of 5
Postnatal management of a macrosomic neonate includes:
Postnatal management involves proper examination of the neonate and management of any identified problems.
📝 Question 4 of 5
In obstetric practice, this knowledge helps:
Comprehensive knowledge enables prevention, early diagnosis, and optimal management.
📝 Question 5 of 5
When is conservative management appropriate?
Conservative management is reserved for mild, uncomplicated cases.
