3Normal Hemoglobin Values in Pregnancy

Trimester-specific hemoglobin cutoffs define who is anemic and who needs treatment. Note that the second-trimester cutoff is lower than the first and third trimesters because of physiologic plasma-volume expansion.

1st Trimester > 11 g/dL
2nd Trimester > 10.5 g/dL
3rd Trimester > 11 g/dL
Postpartum > 10 g/dL
PearlThe 2nd-trimester cutoff of 10.5 g/dL (not 11) is the most commonly forgotten — any value below this in the middle three months is anemia, even though 10.6 g/dL would be acceptable in early pregnancy.

4Signs and Symptoms

1 · Headache, fatigue and lethargy

Low oxygen delivery to the brain and skeletal muscle — the most common presenting complaints.

2 · Tachycardia and tachypnea

Compensatory cardiovascular response to maintain tissue oxygenation.

3 · Paresthesia and pallor

Tingling/numbness and the classic pale mucous membranes, palms and nail beds.

5 · Tongue glossitis

Smooth, beefy-red, sore tongue — a classical finding in iron and B-vitamin deficiency anemias.

DangerIn severe cases, congestive heart failure can occur. Always auscultate the heart and check for a flow murmur / gallop rhythm in any pregnant patient with profound anemia.

5Causes — Mechanism-Based Classification

Anemia results from impaired production of red blood cells, increased destruction of red blood cells or blood loss.

1 · Impaired Production

Iron deficiency, B12 / folate deficiency, marrow failure, chronic disease.

2 · Increased Destruction

Hemolysis — sickle cell, thalassemia, G6PD deficiency, autoimmune hemolysis.

3 · Blood Loss

Antepartum hemorrhage, gastrointestinal bleeding, hookworm.

Anemia can also be classified by inheritance pattern:

  • Congenital — sickle cell anemia and thalassemia.
  • Acquired — iron-deficiency anemia.

6Physiologic (Dilutional) Anemia

Why Hb "drops" in a normal pregnancy

There is an increase in overall blood volume during pregnancy, there is an increase in plasma more than red blood cell mass. The red cell mass rises, but plasma rises more — the resulting hemodilution lowers the hemoglobin concentration without any true RBC deficit.

PearlDilutional anemia is physiologic, not pathologic — the body's way of improving placental perfusion by reducing blood viscosity. The mid-pregnancy Hb nadir (~10.5 g/dL) is when it is most pronounced.

7Effects of Anemia on Pregnancy

Maternal anemia endangers both mother and fetus at every stage — pregnancy, labor, puerperium, and the fetus itself.

Maternal Effects

During pregnancy
  • Pregnancy induced hypertension.
  • Accidental hemorrhage.
  • Preterm labor.
During labor
  • Prolonged labor and inertia.
During puerperium
  • Atony and postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Puerperal sepsis.

Fetal Effects

  • Abortion.
  • IUGR (Intrauterine Growth Restriction).
  • IUFD (Intrauterine Fetal Death).
WarnUterine inertia and postpartum atony share the same mechanism — the myometrium is a working muscle that fails in hypoxia. Anemic mothers bleed more, contract less, and tolerate blood loss worse than non-anemic mothers.

  • Aggravation of pre-existing anemia.

9Iron Deficiency Anemia

The single most common cause of anemia in pregnancy worldwide. This can occur from the increased production of red blood cells, which requires a lot of iron and also from inadequate intake of iron during pregnancy.

Diagnosis

TestFinding
Blood filmMicrocytic hypochromic anemia
Serum iron concentrationDecreased (n = 60–170 µmg/dL)
Iron binding capacityIncreased (n = 200–450 µg/dL)
Serum ferritin(Diagnostic) less than 30 ng/mL

Treatment

Prophylactic

  • Eating iron-rich foods. This includes dark green leafy vegetables, eggs, meat, fish, dried beans, and fortified grains.
  • Supplemental oral iron 27–30 mg daily.

Therapeutic

  • Oral iron (ferrous sulphate, ferrous gluconate) ranges from 40–200 mg elemental iron daily. This should be on an empty stomach, 1 hour before meals, with a source of vitamin C (ascorbic acid) such as orange juice to maximize absorption.
  • Adverse effects in some cases may include gastrointestinal side effects, nausea, diarrhea, and constipation.

Factors Influencing the Absorption of Iron

Factors that inhibit iron absorption

Foods rich in calcium, Tannins in tea and Phytates in cereals.

Factors that enhance iron absorption

Heme iron, Ferrous iron (Fe²⁺) and Ascorbic acid.

Parenteral Iron

For those with iron deficiency that cannot be managed with oral therapy because of lack of compliance, severe gastrointestinal side effects, malabsorption, or continuing significant blood loss.

RouteAgentNotes
Intramuscular (IM) Low molecular weight iron dextran However, injections tend to be painful and there is a significant risk of permanent skin staining.
Intravenous (IV) Iron hydroxide sucrose complex Can be used in cases of severe iron deficiency anemia.

Contraindications for IV iron

  • History of anaphylaxis or reactions to parenteral iron therapy
  • First trimester of pregnancy
  • Active acute or chronic infection
  • Chronic liver disease

Blood Transfusion

Reserved for those with imminent cardiac compromise or low levels of iron immediately before delivery at 37 weeks of pregnancy.

PearlThe 1-hour-before-meals rule plus vitamin C co-administration can double oral iron absorption — the cheapest intervention in iron-deficiency anemia, often missed.
DangerAnaphylaxis to IV iron dextran, while uncommon, is the reason it is contraindicated in the 1st trimester — every maternal life lost this way is preventable by using the safer sucrose complex in the 2nd/3rd trimesters only.

10Megaloblastic Anemia — Folic Acid Deficiency

The second most common nutritional anemia of pregnancy. Without folate, DNA synthesis stalls in the bone marrow — producing the characteristic oversized, immature red cell precursors (megaloblasts).

Daily Requirement

The daily requirement during pregnancy is 200–300 μg/day.

Etiology

  1. Inadequate intake.
  2. Defective absorption.
  3. Increased demand e.g. pregnancy.
  4. Drugs: folic acid antagonists as anti-epileptic drugs.

Clinical Picture

  • General symptoms of anemia.
  • GIT manifestations in the form of:
    • Dyspepsia.
    • Anorexia.
    • Nausea.
    • Vomiting.
    • Diarrhea.

Investigations

  1. Blood film:
    • Macrocytic hyperchromic RBCs.
    • Hypersegmented neutrophilic nuclei (>5 lobes).
  2. Serum folate level: is low measured by radioimmunoassay.
  3. Bone marrow: abnormal red cell precursors (megaloblasts).

Treatment

  • Diet rich in folic acid as liver, kidney and meat.
  • Folic acid 5–15 mg/day orally.
PearlHypersegmented neutrophils (>5 lobes) on a routine blood film are often the earliest sign of folate deficiency — they appear before the macrocytosis and before the hemoglobin falls.

11Vitamin B12 Deficiency Anemia

Rarer in pregnancy than folate deficiency — but uniquely dangerous because of the irreversible neurologic damage it can cause if missed.

Etiology

  1. Inadequate intake (rare).
  2. Deficient intrinsic factor as in atrophic gastritis or gastrectomy.
  3. Malabsorption syndrome.
  4. Increased demand e.g. pregnancy.

Clinical Picture

  • General symptoms of anemia.
  • GIT manifestations: as folic acid deficiency.
  • Nervous manifestations:
    • Subacute combined degeneration.
    • Peripheral neuritis.

Investigations

As folic acid deficiency + decreased serum vit. B12.

Treatment

Vit. B12 IM injection.

WarnSubacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord (dorsal columns + corticospinal tracts) is the neurologic hallmark of B12 deficiency — numbness, paresthesia, ataxia, and spastic weakness. It is potentially irreversible if treatment is delayed, distinguishing B12 deficiency from the more easily corrected folate deficiency.

12Thalassemia — Definition and Inheritance

An autosomal inherited disorder resulted from failure of production of either α chain (α-thalassemia) or β chain (β-thalassemia) of the hemoglobin molecule and their replacement with other polypeptide chains.
PearlThalassemia is a quantitative disorder — the globin chains are made in the wrong numbers — whereas sickle cell disease is a qualitative disorder — the chains are made but defective. Both are autosomal, but the underlying molecular pathology is different.

13α-Thalassemia — Major and Minor

(I) α-Thalassemia Major (homozygous)

  • The fetus with this disorder is affected in utero showing polyhydramnios, erythroblastosis, anemia and hydrops.
  • The fetus does not survive due to inability of oxygen transfer as the α-chain is responsible for O2 carrying capacity.

(I) α-Thalassemia Minor (heterozygous)

  • Patient develops mild progressive anemia during pregnancy.
Dangerα-Thalassemia major is incompatible with extrauterine life — the fetus cannot make any functional hemoglobin (HbF still requires α-chains). The obstetric implication is severe fetal hydrops and stillbirth, and the family must be offered genetic counseling.

14β-Thalassemia — Major and Minor

(II) β-Thalassemia Major (homozygous)

  • The disorder starts in childhood leading to death of the patient.

(II) β-Thalassemia Minor (heterozygous)

  • As α-thalassemia minor. — mild progressive anemia during pregnancy.
PearlUnlike α-thalassemia major, β-thalassemia major is not a fetal disease — the fetus survives on HbF (γ-chains) and only becomes symptomatic in the first year of life as HbF declines. Hence the source's note "starts in childhood."

15Thalassemia — Investigations and Treatment

Effect on Pregnancy

Anemia becomes severe in the second trimester and heart failure may occur.

Investigations

TestFinding
Blood filmShows microcytic hypochromic anemia
ElectrophoresisDetect type of hemoglobin

Treatment

  • Blood transfusion: in acute attacks.
  • Folic acid: may be indicated.
  • Mode of delivery: CS.
WarnThalassemia and iron-deficiency anemia share a microcytic hypochromic blood film. The discriminating test is not the MCV or the MCH — it is serum ferritin (low in IDA, normal/high in thalassemia trait) and hemoglobin electrophoresis (diagnostic in thalassemia).
