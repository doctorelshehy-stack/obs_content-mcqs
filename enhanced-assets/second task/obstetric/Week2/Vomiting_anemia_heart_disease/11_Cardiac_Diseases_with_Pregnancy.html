2Cardiovascular Physiology During Pregnancy

Pregnancy is a high-output, low-resistance state — the cardiovascular system is remodeled to perfuse the placenta. These adaptations mimic disease in the unwary examiner.

Key hemodynamic changes

  • Increase in cardiac output due to increased stroke volume and increase in heart rate.
  • Reduced systemic vascular resistance.
PearlThe cardiac output rises ~30–50% by the third trimester, peaking at delivery. This is why cardiac decompensation most often occurs at 28–32 weeks, during labor, and immediately postpartum — when blood volume and venous return are at their highest.

3Incidence and Etiology

Cardiac disease complicates 1–4% of pregnancies in most populations. Two etiological groups account for nearly all cases.

Incidence: 1–4 %

Congenital

The most common congenital heart disease are ASD, VSD and PDA.

Acquired

Rheumatic heart (85%, the commonest is mitral stenosis), ischemic heart, cardiomyopathies.

DangerRheumatic heart disease accounts for 85% of acquired cardiac disease in pregnancy — and mitral stenosis is the most common valve lesion. In any pregnant patient with new dyspnea + opening snap + diastolic rumble, the diagnosis is mitral stenosis until proven otherwise.

4NYHA Functional Classification

The single most important bedside tool. It is the language that cardiologists, obstetricians, and anesthetists all use to communicate severity.

CLASS I

No symptoms & no limitation in ordinary physical activity.

CLASS II

Mild symptoms & slight limitation during ordinary physical activity.

CLASS III

Marked limitation in activity, even during less than ordinary activity. Only comfortable at rest.

CLASS IV

Severe limitations, symptoms even at rest.

PearlNYHA class is dynamic in pregnancy — a Class II patient can deteriorate to Class III as blood volume rises, then improve again postpartum. Re-classify at every antenatal visit, not just at booking.

6Diagnosis — Examination

  • Murmur
  • Accentuated heart sound
  • Arrhythmia
  • Central cyanosis
  • Displaced apex beat
  • Manifestations of left side heart failure e.g. gallop rhythm, crepitations over lung bases and pleural effusion.
  • Manifestations of right side heart failure e.g. congested neck veins, enlarged tender liver, ascitis and oedema lower limbs.
WarnThe classic triad of decompensation is tachycardia >100/min + crepitations at lung bases + displaced apex. This is the "earliest evidence of decompensation" the source flags — admit to hospital immediately when it appears.

7Diagnosis — Investigations

  • Chest X-ray: may show cardiac enlargement, pulmonary congestion or pleural effusion.
  • Electrocardiography (ECG).
  • Echocardiography: for cardiac structure and function.
PearlEchocardiography is the investigation of choice in pregnancy — it is non-ionizing, gives structural and functional information, and is safe to repeat serially. Reserve CXR and CT for cases where the echo is inconclusive.

8Misleading Findings in Pregnancy

Three classes of "abnormality" that are normal in pregnancy. Knowing them prevents over-investigation and unnecessary admission.

1 · Dyspnea and tachycardia

2 · Increased neck (jugular) venous pressure

During normal pregnancy up to +5 cm is not uncommon due to high cardiac output.

3 · Auscultation changes due to hyperkinetic circulation

  • Systolic ejection murmur.
  • Splitting of the first heart sound.
  • Early diastolic murmur due to increased velocity of the blood passing through the aortic and pulmonary valves.
  • Presence of third heart sound.
WarnA systolic ejection murmur and a third heart sound are physiologic in most pregnancies. Do not admit every pregnant woman with a "new murmur" — but do an echo to confirm, because missing a true MS or AS is far more dangerous than over-imaging.

9Effect of Pregnancy on Heart Disease

  • Deteriorate the patient one functional class.
  • May precipitate heart failure mostly at 28–32 weeks, 2nd, 3rd, 4th stage of labor due to high blood volume which increase cardiac output.
  • Increase risk of subacute bacterial endocarditis, thromboembolism and rheumatic attacks.
DangerThree high-risk windows for decompensation: 28–32 weeks (peak blood volume), the 2nd stage of labor (Valsalva + tachycardia), and the immediate postpartum (autotransfusion from the contracted uterus). Plan extra monitoring in all three.

10Effect of Heart Disease on Pregnancy

Maternal / Obstetric Effects

  • Polyhydramnios.
  • Preterm labor.
  • Postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Abortion.
  • Excess weight gain during pregnancy.
  • Preeclampsia.
  • Placental abruption.
  • Gestational diabetes.
  • Progressive heart failure.
  • Maternal or fetal death.

Fetal Effects

  • Increase incidence of congenital malformation (CFMF).
  • IUGR and IUFD.
DangerCongenital heart disease in the mother is associated with an increased incidence of congenital malformation (CFMF) in the fetus — preconception counseling and a detailed anatomy scan at 20–22 weeks are mandatory in this group.

11Management — During Pregnancy

1 · Frequent antenatal visits

2 · Rest

3 · Prevent and treat anemia

As it may induce heart failure.

4 · Hospitalisation on decompensation

If signs of decompensation occur, the earliest evidence is tachycardia exceeding 100 beats/minute and crepitations at the lung bases. Rest in a hospital is better in the last 2 weeks of pregnancy.

5 · Heparin

Is indicated in patients with artificial valves or atrial fibrillation.

6 · Digoxin and diuretics

In cases of heart failure.

PearlThe simplest, cheapest, and most evidence-based intervention: prevent and treat anemia. Volume overload of pregnancy is poorly tolerated when every extra gram of hemoglobin is already missing.
WarnWarfarin is teratogenic in the 1st trimester (warfarin embryopathy) — switch to heparin before conception or as soon as pregnancy is confirmed in patients with mechanical valves.

12Management — During Delivery

Delivery of a cardiac patient is a planned, multidisciplinary event — not an emergency unless it has to be.

Team & Setting

  • Should be done in high risk unit, multidisciplinary team (senior obstetrician, cardiologist, anesthesiologist).

Route of Delivery

Vaginal delivery is allowed except in patients with restricted cardiac output as:

  • Tight MS
  • Severe AS
  • Primary pulmonary hypertension
  • Eisenmenger syndrome
  • Marfan's syndrome with aortic root > 4 cm

Timing of Delivery

Usually at term except if heart failure happened which may need preterm termination of pregnancy. Patient is not allowed to go postterm.

DangerThe "no postterm" rule matters — every additional day of pregnancy adds blood volume, cardiac output, and risk of decompensation. Induce at 38–39 weeks in compensated cardiac patients; do not wait for spontaneous labor.

13Management — Intrapartum (Stages 1–3)

During 1st Stage

  • Semi-setting position.
  • O2 mask-sedation.
  • Control fluid intake.
  • Infective endocarditis (IE) prophylaxis.

During 2nd Stage

  • Sedation.
  • Shorten 2nd stage.
  • Close observation.

During 3rd Stage

  • Avoid ergot alkaloids.
  • Diuretics can be given to decrease blood volume and cardiac output.
WarnAvoid ergot alkaloids (methylergometrine) at the 3rd stage — they cause vasoconstriction and hypertension that can precipitate acute decompensation. Use oxytocin infusion and controlled cord traction instead.
Pearl"Shorten the 2nd stage" is a euphemism for instrumental delivery (forceps or ventouse) at the right station — a long, exhausting 2nd stage is the single most common precipitant of acute pulmonary edema in labor.

14Puerperium & Contraception

Puerperium

  • Postpartum observation for 48 hours is essential as the risk of heart failure is high in this period.
  • Breast feeding is allowed unless there is heart failure.

Contraception

  • Progesterone only methods can be used.
  • Sterilization is recommended if decompensation occurred in this pregnancy.
DangerThe 48-hour postpartum window is when autotransfusion from the contracted uterus can throw a borderline cardiac patient into frank pulmonary edema. Continuous monitoring for 48 hours postpartum is not optional — it is the standard of care.
PearlAvoid estrogen-containing contraceptives in any patient with a high thromboembolic risk (mechanical valves, atrial fibrillation, Eisenmenger, Fontan). Progesterone-only methods (DMPA, implant, levonorgestrel IUD) are safe.

15Student Activity

Bedside Clinical Task

Each student is requested to identify a pregnant woman with cardiac disease among departmental admitted cases then assigns her cardiac disease condition into a certain grade of NYHA by the aid of the bedside teaching tutor during clinical rounds.

NoteCarry the NYHA classification (§4) to the bedside. Assigning the wrong class under-treats a Class III or over-restricts a Class I — both are common errors in undergraduate teaching.
