Introduction & Pre-pregnancy Preparation

KEY POINT
Maternal nutrition before and during pregnancy influences the course of pregnancy, fetal development, and the child's health in early and adult life. Maternal underweight before pregnancy (BMI <19.8 kg/m²) and low pregnancy weight gain increase risk of low infant birth weight.
Pre-pregnancy Preparation
  1. Achieve and maintain healthy body weight
  2. Choose an adequate and balanced diet
  3. Be physically active
  4. Receive regular medical care
  5. Manage chronic conditions
Energy & Nutrition Requirements
  • To meet basic metabolic needs
  • Blood production and cell growth
  • Fetal growth and development
  • Maternal red blood cell mass
  • DNA and new cell synthesis (Folate, Vitamin B12, Iron, Zinc)

MEDICAL, SURGICAL & OBSTETRIC HISTORY
Conditions causing nutrition-related health risks (Table 2):
Medical Conditions
Type 1/2 diabetes, inborn metabolic errors (PKU, maple syrup urine disease)
Obstetric History
Previous child with NTD, gestational diabetes, hyperemesis gravidarum, hypertensive disorders, multifetal pregnancy
Surgical / GI
Bariatric surgery, bowel resection, Crohn disease, ulcerative colitis, cystic fibrosis, celiac disease
Lifestyle & Diet
Obesity/overweight, eating disorders (past/current), supplement use, food avoidances, skipping meals, lack of resources, low nutrient-dense foods, high added sugars/fats, cigarettes, alcohol, illicit drugs
B. Physical Examination
BMI ASSESSMENT
Centers on height and weight to calculate BMI. Use pre-pregnancy weight (at last menstrual period) as baseline. Weight gain is monitored throughout pregnancy.
C. Laboratory Investigations
Hemoglobin / Hematocrit
Routinely checked at first visit and late 2nd/early 3rd trimester to assess for anemia.
Anemia in pregnancy (Hb <11 g/dL by Egyptian guidelines):
  • 1st trimester: Hb <11 g/dL
  • 2nd trimester: Hb <10.5 g/dL
  • 3rd trimester: Hb <11 g/dL
  • Postpartum: Hb <10 g/dL
Vitamin D
Routine broad-based screening not necessary. Measure in: obesity, minimal sun exposure, malabsorption (celiac, IBD), northern latitudes, vegan diet, dark skin.

Nutrition Education

OPPORTUNITY
Nutrition education during pregnancy provides an excellent opportunity to improve the nutritional status for the whole family.

Key components of healthy eating:

Limit added sugars, saturated fat, and sodium
Appropriate vitamin & mineral supplementation
Appropriate gestational weight gain
Avoid alcohol and harmful substances (e.g., mercury)
Limit caffeine to <200–300 mg/day
Safe food handling — avoid foodborne infections (congenital disease, miscarriage, preterm labor, fetal death)

Food Guide Pyramid During Pregnancy

Figure (1): The Food Guide Pyramid During Pregnancy

The general principle is to select foods according to the Food Guide Pyramid with modifications:

🥖 Base: Cereals
Bread, rice, pasta.
6–11 servings/day
🥦 Vegetables & Fruits
Vitamins, minerals, fiber. Deep green veg = iron source. Colored veg/fruits + dairy + egg yolk + liver = vitamin A.
Veg: 3–5, Fruit: 3–4 servings/day
🥛 Milk Group
Milk, yogurt, cheese, milk-based desserts (mehlabeya). Fat-free cheese (Gebna Kareesh) is useful — low salt.
4 servings/day
🥩 Meat & Protein
Meat, poultry, eggs, beans supply proteins.
3–4 servings/day
🫒 Fats (Top Layer)
If mother receives enough animal protein (⅔ of protein requirements), fat content is adequate.
Occasionally
💧 Water
Pregnant mothers should receive enough water daily.

Nutrition Supplementation

💊 Iron

All pregnant mothers (after 3 months):
220 mg ferrous fumarate/sulphate + folic acid once daily

NICE note: Iron supplementation should not be offered routinely to all — may not benefit and may have unpleasant side effects.

💊 Folic Acid

Routine: 400 µg (0.4 mg) once daily
High-risk (NTD): 4 mg once daily prior to conception through first 12 weeks
Multivitamins may also be given

💊 Calcium

If unsure the mother consumes enough milk:
500 mg tablets twice daily in the 2nd trimester

⚠ WARNING — VITAMIN A
Pregnant women should be informed that vitamin A supplementation (intake above 700 micrograms) might be teratogenic and should therefore be avoided.

Correction of Nutritional Deficiencies — Anemia

MOST COMMON
The most common deficiency is anemia. Correction by higher doses of iron + folic acid.
Curative Management of Iron Deficiency Anemia
  • Give ferrous fumarate or sulphate 200 mg 3 times daily
  • GI intolerance: change to another variety or decrease dose
  • Improve absorption: avoid coffee, tea, milk at the time of iron intake
Oral vs IV Iron
Oral Iron (First-Line)
Used for most pregnant women with iron deficiency who can tolerate it. Used for all individuals in the 1st trimester.
IV Iron — Indications
  • Beyond 1st trimester & cannot tolerate oral iron
  • Severe or symptomatic anemia (especially later in pregnancy)
  • Oral iron ineffective (impaired absorption/adherence)
  • Can fully correct deficiency in a single administration
Assessing Response to Treatment
1 Week
Start reticulocytosis
2–3 Weeks
Hb increase ≥ 1 g/dL
Within 3 Weeks
Serum ferritin → normal range

Response is similar with oral or IV. Reasons for lack of response: non-adherence, reduced absorption, ongoing bleeding, or other cause of anemia.

REFERRAL INDICATIONS
  • Hb < 7 g/dL
  • Intolerance to oral iron
  • Persistent anemia

Folate Deficiency

MOST COMMON CAUSE
Folate deficiency is the most common cause of megaloblastic anemia during pregnancy, often associated with diets low in animal proteins, fresh leafy vegetables, and legumes.
Effects on Pregnancy Outcome
🧠 Neural Tube Defect
Incomplete closure of neural tube
🫁 Anencephaly
Brain is either missing or fails to develop
🦴 Spina Bifida
Incomplete closure of spinal cord & its bony encasement
Folic Acid Supplementation Recommendations
  • Periconceptional/first-trimester: reduce occurrence and recurrence of NTDs
  • Most women: 0.4 mg folic acid once daily beginning at least 1 month prior to conception through first trimester
  • All women of childbearing potential (not just those attempting to conceive) should receive folic acid — unplanned pregnancies are common and neural tube closes very early
  • Previous NTD or NTD in either parent: 4 mg once daily from at least 1 month prior to conception through first trimester, then decrease to 0.4 mg/day after 1st trimester
  • Measurement of maternal folate levels before or after supplementation is not recommended
  • In the absence of vitamin B12 deficiency, there are no clear harms from folic acid supplementation
