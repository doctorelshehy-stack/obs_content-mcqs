Definition & Objectives

DEFINITION
The postpartum period (puerperium) starts about two hours after the delivery of the placenta and continues for six weeks. It is the period when anatomic and physiologic changes of pregnancy are reversed, and the body returns to the normal non-pregnant state.
Objectives of Postnatal Care
👶 For the Newborn
  1. Early detection of any health problems
  2. Establishment and promotion of successful breast feeding
  3. Counseling and initiation of proper infant care by the mother and family
👩 For the Mother
  1. Reproductive health promotion (physical, psychological, social)
  2. Prevention of postpartum complications
  3. Early detection of complications and referral
  4. Psychological support by health care providers
  5. Counseling for support by husband and family
  6. Social adjustment and re-integration in family and society
  7. Counseling for contraception for spacing
  8. Providing appropriate health education messages

Clinical Picture — History Taking

Day 1 (Home)
  • Abnormal bleeding, # pads/day
  • Severe headache, visual disturbances
  • Epigastric pain
  • Urination
  • ✅ Vitamin A capsule (200,000 IU) given
Day 4 (Home)
  • Calf pain (thrombotic)
  • Chest pain (thrombotic)
  • Foul smelling discharge
  • Fever or chills (sepsis)
Day 7 (Home)
  • Calf / chest pain
  • Foul smelling discharge
  • Fever or chills
  • ✅ Check umbilical stump fallen off
Day 40 (Clinic)
  • Complete assessment
  • Initiation of contraception
  • Hb + urine analysis

Physical Examination — Mother

🩺 General
  • Vital signs: BP, pulse, temperature
  • Breasts: retracted nipples
  • Lower limbs: edema, tender calf muscles
Abdominal
  • Day 1: Fundal height & firmness by palpation, abdominal tenderness, check uterine involution
  • Day 40: Abdominal musculature tone & condition; C-section incision inspection
Local (Perineal)
  • Amount of vaginal bleeding
  • Perineum: infections, lacerations, hematomas
Laboratory Investigation (Day 40)
Hemoglobin level
Complete urine analysis

Infant Assessment

ORDER
After examining the mother, the health care provider's attention should be focused on the infant.
📋 History Taking
  • Ask about danger symptoms
  • Any concerns the mother has
🩺 Physical Examination
  • General: Tone, color, weight, temperature, respiratory rate
  • Chest: Retracted chest wall during respiration
  • Abdominal: Umbilical stump — signs of infection/bleeding; Day 7: should have fallen off or totally dried
🧪 Lab — Day 1
Counsel mother to have infant screened for congenital hypothyroidism within the first week.
🧪 Lab — Day 7
Check that infant was screened; if not, perform the screening (blood sample from heel for hypothyroidism).

Danger Symptoms & Red Flags

⚠️ Danger Symptoms — When to Seek Medical Care

👩 Mother

  • Vaginal bleeding
  • Severe headache, blurring of vision, severe epigastric pain, or convulsions
  • Fever or malodorous vaginal discharge
  • Painful calf muscle
  • Abdomen very tender when pressed
  • Chest pain or dyspnea
  • Fainting

👶 Infant

  • Convulsions or continuous sleep
  • Refusal of feeding for two successive times
  • Delayed passage of stool or urine > 24 hours
  • Diarrhea or excessive vomiting
  • Fever or cold skin
  • Abnormal color — pale, dusky, yellow, blue, or mottling
  • Umbilical stump red, oozing, draining pus
  • Rapid respirations (panting)
  • Poor/weak sucking (inability to latch)
  • Lethargy (does not grasp finger, floppy)
  • Weak and/or constant painful cry
  • Swollen, sticky eyes draining pus
🚩 RED FLAGS — Referral Criteria

Detected during home visits — refer to health care facility immediately.

👩 Mother Red Flags
  • Pulse > 100 bpm
  • BP > 140/90 mmHg
  • Temperature > 38 °C
  • Tender or hard breast
  • Tender calf muscles
  • Lower abdominal tenderness
  • Malodorous vaginal discharge or infected perineal wound (episiotomy/tear)
👶 Infant Red Flags
  • Temperature < 36 °C or > 38 °C
  • Respiratory rate > 60/min
  • Flaccid, non-reactive infant
  • Jaundice, cyanosis, or pallor
  • Nasal flaring + chest retraction (difficulty breathing)
  • Bloody or purulent discharge from umbilical stump
  • Severe abdominal distention
  • Ambiguous genitalia

Health Education & Counseling

👩 Mother
  1. Encourage early ambulation
  2. Explain benefits, initiation and establishment of successful breastfeeding — periodic suckling and complete emptying of breasts
  3. Stress importance of seeking medical aid if danger symptoms/signs appear
  4. Explain importance of good nutrition
  5. Explain abdominal muscle tone and exercises
  6. Counsel at day 7 about contraception
  7. Encourage day 40 visit — explain its importance
👶 Infant
  1. Proper temperature regulation
  2. Care of the umbilical cord
  3. Care of the eyes
Additional Health Education Messages
Signs of postnatal complications — seek early care
Lactating mother: nutrition requirements & lifestyle
Initiation & technique of breastfeeding
Breast & nipple care
Baby care: bathing, skin, eyes, nose, ears, umbilical stump
Stimulating activities for baby development
Well-baby care: first visit schedule, vaccination schedule, MCH visits
Birth spacing

Timing of Postpartum Care

HOME DELIVERIES
Nurse visits on Day 1 (within 24 hours), Day 4, and Day 7. Then mother visits the center on Day 40 for complete assessment and initiation of contraception.
HOSPITAL DELIVERIES
Care in hospital until discharge; then same schedule as home deliveries.
What Each Visit Covers
👩 Mother Assessment
  • Temperature
  • Blood pressure
  • Involution of uterus
  • Discharges
👶 Infant Assessment
  • General condition, color, vitality
  • Feeding
  • Umbilical stump
  • Passing urine and stools
  • Congenital abnormalities
DAY 3–7
Blood sample from heel for congenital hypothyroidism screening (per protocol).
