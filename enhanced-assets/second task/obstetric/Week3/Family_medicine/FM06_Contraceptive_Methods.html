1 Family Planning Overview

WHO Definition: Family planning is the ability of individuals and couples to anticipate and attain their desired number of children and the spacing and timing of their births.

Importance of Family Planning

High fertility can affect the health of mothers, present child, and coming child:

  • Mother: Malnutrition (IDA, osteomalacia), post-partum depression
  • Present child: Deprived of proper breastfeeding period and care — prone to malnutrition
  • Coming child: Mother lacks time to restore Iron and Calcium stores

The Four "TOO" — Target Groups for Contraception

Maternal and child mortality are increased by:

TOO Young< 20 years
TOO SoonInter-pregnancy spacing < 2 years
TOO Many5th pregnancy or more
TOO Old> 35 years

3 Physiological Methods

Note: These methods have a high failure rate and are best used with backup methods.

1. Periodic Abstinence (Fertility Awareness)

The female identifies the fertile time of her menstrual cycle and avoids unprotected intercourse during that period.

Calendar-Based Methods

Standard Days Method: Avoid unprotected intercourse on days 8 through 19 of regular menstrual cycles.

Symptoms-Based Methods

Observing signs of ovulation:

  • Cervical secretions: Increase and become thin
  • Basal Body Temperature (BBT): Rises by 0.5°C after ovulation

2. Withdrawal (Coitus Interruptus)

The penis is withdrawn before ejaculation ("pulling out"). High failure rate due to pre-ejaculate fluid.

3. Lactational Amenorrhea (LAM)

A temporary method based on the natural effect of breastfeeding on fertility. All 3 conditions must be met:

  • Mother's monthly bleeding has not returned
  • Baby is fully or nearly fully breastfed, fed often day and night
  • Baby is less than 6 months old

4 Barrier Methods

Male Condoms

Thin sheath worn on the penis. Provides STI protection. Non-prescription, low cost.

Vaginal Diaphragm & Cervical Cap

Shallow cup placed in vagina before intercourse. Use with spermicides for increased efficacy.

Female Condom

Pouch inserted into the vagina. Provides dual protection.

Spermicides

Foam, gel, cream, film, suppository, or tablet. Placed in vagina ≤1 hr before intercourse, left in place ≥6-8 hrs after.

Non-Contraceptive Advantages

  • Ease of use, non-prescription, low cost
  • Lubrication
  • Possible protection against some STIs (GC, Chlamydia, Trichomonas)
  • Reduced cervical cancer risk
  • Reversible and inexpensive

Side Effects

  • Regular use with spermicide may cause skin irritation or tiny abrasions (increases STI risk)
  • Latex allergy or contact dermatitis
  • Increased vaginal infection and UTI

5 Hormonal Methods — Mechanism of Action

Triple Mechanism

  1. Suppression of ovulation (primary mechanism for combined methods)
  2. Thickened cervical mucus — interferes with sperm movement to uterus
  3. Endometrial changes — interfere with implantation
📝 Question 1 of 5
What is the primary mechanism of action of combined hormonal contraceptives?
The triple mechanism of hormonal methods: (1) Suppression of ovulation (primary for combined), (2) Thickened cervical mucus, (3) Endometrial changes.
📝 Question 2 of 5
Which of the following is NOT a mechanism of action of hormonal contraceptives?
The three mechanisms are: ovulation suppression, thickened cervical mucus, and endometrial changes. Direct killing of sperm is not a mechanism.

6 Combined Estrogen & Progesterone

Combined Oral Contraceptives (COCs)

Low-dose estrogen + progestin pills. Taken daily. <1 pregnancy/100 women/year with perfect use.

Combined Monthly Injectable

Similar to COCs. Given every 4 weeks (±7 days). Does not require daily administration.

Combined Patch

Small adhesive patch worn on the body. New patch weekly × 3 weeks, then patch-free week. Side effect: skin irritation.

Combined Vaginal Ring

Flexible ring placed in vagina. Releases 2 hormones absorbed through vaginal wall. 3 weeks in, 1 week off.

📝 Question 1 of 5

The combined monthly injectable is given every 4 weeks (±7 days) and does not require daily administration.
📝 Question 2 of 5

The combined vaginal ring is used for 3 weeks in, 1 week off.

7 Combined Oral Contraceptives (COCs)

When to Start

First Time / Switching from Non-Hormonal

Within 5 days of menses: No backup needed.

>5 days: Start any time if pregnancy is reasonably excluded. Use backup method for first 7 days.

Switching from Hormonal Method

Begin at the repeat time if used consistently and correctly, or if pregnancy is excluded.

Fully Breastfeeding

Start 6 months after giving birth.

Not Breastfeeding

Start 4 weeks after giving birth.

After Miscarriage or Abortion

Start within 7 days. If >7 days, use backup for first 7 days.

Side Effects

Lighter bleeding
Irregular bleeding
Headaches
Dizziness
Nausea
Breast tenderness
Weight change
Mood changes
BP increase (few mmHg)
DVT (very rare)
Stroke (very rare)
MI (very rare)

Managing Side Effects

  • Keep taking COCs
  • Take at same time daily to reduce irregular bleeding
  • Take with food or at bedtime to avoid nausea

Non-Contraceptive Advantages

Protects Against:

  • Endometrial cancer
  • Ovarian cancer
  • Ovarian cysts
  • Iron-deficiency anemia

Reduces:

  • Menstrual cramps
  • Menstrual bleeding problems
  • Hirsutism
  • PCOS symptoms (irregular bleeding, acne, excess hair)
  • Endometriosis symptoms (pelvic pain, irregular bleeding)

Contraindications (11 Points)

  1. Breastfeeding a baby <6 months old
  2. Age ≥35 years and smokes cigarettes
  3. Liver cirrhosis, hepatitis, liver tumor, or history of jaundice with COCs
  4. Hypertension
  5. Diabetes >20 years or with micro/macro vascular complications
  6. Gallbladder disease (current or medically treated)
  7. History of stroke, DVT, PE, MI, or other serious heart problems
  8. Personal history of breast cancer
  9. Migraine headache
  10. Taking seizure medications (barbiturates, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin) or rifampicin/rifabutin
  11. Planning major surgery with ≥1 week immobility — start COCs 2 weeks after mobile again

Special Situations

Managing Missed Pills

Key message: Take a missed pill ASAP. Keep taking one pill daily — 2 pills can be taken the same day.

Missed 1–2 Pills

Take the most recent pill ASAP, continue the rest as usual.

Missed ≥3 Pills (Weeks 1–2)

Take most recent pill ASAP. Use backup method for next 7 days.

Missed ≥3 Pills (Week 3)

Take most recent pill ASAP. Finish all pills. Start new pack immediately without 7-day break. Use backup for 7 days.

Irregular Bleeding

  • Reassure: common in first few months, not harmful, usually resolves
  • Other causes: missed pills, inconsistent timing, vomiting/diarrhea, anticonvulsants/rifampicin
  • Management: Take pill at same time daily; proper missed-pill correction; ibuprofen 800 mg TID × 5 days when bleeding starts
  • If persists > few months and NSAIDs fail → consider different COC formulation

8 Progestin-Only Methods

1. Progestin-Only Injectable (DMPA / NET-EN)

DMPA

Given every 3 months (±2 weeks)

NET-EN

Given every 2 months (±2 weeks)

  • Contains only progesterone — can be used throughout breastfeeding
  • Primary mechanism: prevents ovulation
  • Effectiveness: <1 pregnancy/100 women/year with on-time injections

Side Effects (DMPA)

First 3 Months

  • Irregular bleeding
  • Prolonged bleeding

At One Year

  • No monthly bleeding
  • Infrequent/irregular bleeding
  • Other: Weight gain, headaches, dizziness, abdominal bloating, mood changes, reduced libido
  • Return of fertility is often delayed (several months longer on average)
  • Loss of bone density (possible)

Non-Contraceptive Advantages

  • Protects against: Endometrial cancer, uterine fibroids, symptomatic PID, iron-deficiency anemia
  • Reduces: Endometriosis symptoms

Contraindications

As COCs, but with these specifics:

  • Hypertension: If controlled → can use; if uncontrolled → should not use
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding suggesting pregnancy or underlying condition

When to Start

Non-Hormonal → Switching

≤7 days of menses: no backup. >7 days: pregnancy excluded, backup 7 days.

Hormonal → Switching

Immediately if used consistently. No backup needed.

Fully Breastfeeding

Start at 6 weeks postpartum.

Not Breastfeeding

Start at 4 weeks postpartum.

After Miscarriage/Abortion

Within 7 days.

Special Situation — No Monthly Bleeding

Reassure: Most women stop having monthly bleeding over time. This is not harmful (similar to pregnancy). She is not infertile. Blood is not building up inside. If bothers her → consider switching to monthly injectable.

2. Implants

  • Small plastic rods placed in subcutaneous space of upper arm
  • Provide long-term protection for 3–5 years (depends on type)
  • Immediately reversible upon removal
  • Mechanism: Prevents ovulation + thickens cervical mucus
  • Effectiveness: Far less than 1 pregnancy/100 women/year

Side Effects

  • Similar to progestin-only injectable, but:
  • No effect on bone density
  • No delay in regaining fertility after removal
  • More changes in bleeding patterns
  • Infection at insertion site
  • Difficult removal (rare if properly inserted by skilled provider)

3. Progestin-Only Pills (Minipills)

  • Very low dose progestin
  • Primary mechanism: Thickening cervical mucus
  • Secondary: Prevents ovulation in ~50% of women

Effectiveness

Breastfeeding Women

~1 pregnancy/100 women/year

Non-Breastfeeding Women

~7 pregnancies/100 women/year

Side Effects

  • Changes in bleeding patterns (frequent, irregular, infrequent, prolonged, or absent)
  • Headaches, dizziness, mood changes, breast tenderness, abdominal pain, nausea

Contraindications

  • Liver cirrhosis or tumor
  • History of stroke, DVT, PE, MI
  • Personal history of breast cancer
  • Taking seizure medications or TB medications

When to Start (Fully Breastfeeding, <6 months)

No Monthly Bleeding

Start any time between birth and 6 months (better at 6 weeks). No backup needed.

Monthly Bleeding Returned

≤5 days of menses → no backup. >5 days → backup for first 2 days of pills.

Critical: Take at same time daily! Being >3 hours late reduces effectiveness.

Missed Pills

  • If ≥3 hours late or missed completely: take missed pill ASAP
  • Breastfeeding with regular menses: backup method for 2 days
  • Non-breastfeeding: take missed pill ASAP + backup for 2 days

4. Progesterone-Releasing Vaginal Ring

  • Suitable for postpartum women who are actively breastfeeding (≥4 times/day)
  • Flexible ring inserted in vagina, left in place for 90 days
  • Up to 4 rings can be used consecutively (~1 year postpartum)
  • Start new ring immediately after removing previous one

9 Intrauterine Devices (IUDs)

1. Copper-Bearing IUD (Cu-IUD)

T-shaped device wrapped with copper wire. Works by inducing inflammatory reaction in endometrium, hostile to sperm motility — prevents fertilization and implantation.

ParameterDetails
Effectiveness<1 pregnancy/100 women/year over first year; ~2/100 over 10 years
DurationTCu-380A effective for 10 years
Return of fertilityNo delay after removal
Side EffectsMenorrhagia, irregular bleeding (first 3–6 months), dysmenorrhea

Complications

  • Menorrhagia may contribute to anemia if iron stores are low
  • Infections: PID, tubo-ovarian abscesses
  • Rare: Perforation (usually heals without treatment)
  • Rare: Miscarriage/preterm birth/infection if pregnancy occurs with IUD in place
  • Difficult removal
  • Unrecognized expulsion → unintended pregnancy

Contraindications

  • Between 48 hours and 4 weeks postpartum
  • Puerperal sepsis or septic abortion (re-evaluate after treatment)
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding suggesting pregnancy or underlying condition
  • Current cervical, endometrial, or ovarian cancer
  • High risk for STIs (unless gonorrhea/chlamydia ruled out)
  • History of ectopic pregnancy (not advisable — if IUD fails, 6–8% of pregnancies are ectopic)

Special Situations

Heavy / Prolonged Bleeding

Reassure: common, usually resolves after first few months.

Management (try one at a time):

  • Tranexamic acid 1500 mg TID × 3 days, then 1000 mg daily × 2 days
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400 mg or indomethacin 25 mg) BID × 5 days — avoid aspirin
  • Iron supplements + iron-rich foods
  • If persists → consider removal

Cramping Pain

Expected first 1–2 days after insertion. Also common in first 3–6 months during menstruation. Decreases over time.

Ibuprofen 200–400 mg or paracetamol 325–1000 mg.

If severe cramping beyond 2 days → evaluate for partial expulsion or perforation.

Missing Strings

Ask: Saw IUD come out? Last menstruation? Pregnancy symptoms? Backup method used?

Check cervical canal with forceps (~50% found here). If not found → refer for ultrasound (or X-ray if pregnancy excluded).

2. Levonorgestrel IUD (LNG-IUD / Hormonal IUD)

T-shaped plastic device that steadily releases levonorgestrel. Works by preventing fertilization — profound change in cervical mucin making it impenetrable by sperm. Local effect on endometrium diminishes menstrual bleeding.

ParameterDetails
Effectiveness<1 pregnancy/100 women/year
DurationMirena & Kyleena — up to 5 years
Return of fertilityNo delay after removal

Non-Contraceptive Advantages

  • Helps protect against: Iron-deficiency anemia
  • May help protect against: Endometrial cancer, cervical cancer
  • Reduces: Dysmenorrhea, heavy menstrual bleeding, endometriosis symptoms

Side Effects

  • Bleeding pattern changes (lighter, fewer days, infrequent, irregular, or absent; prolonged — least common)
  • Acne, headaches, breast tenderness, nausea, weight gain, dizziness, mood changes

Contraindications

  • Between 48 hours and 4 weeks since giving birth
  • History of DVT or pulmonary embolism
  • History of breast cancer
  • Liver cirrhosis or tumor
  • Current ovarian or endometrial cancer
  • High risk for STIs at insertion

11 Permanent Methods

Intended to provide lifelong, permanent, and very effective protection against pregnancy. Reversal is usually not possible.

Female Sterilization

Tubal ligation — the fallopian tubes are tied, blocked, or cut to prevent eggs from reaching the uterus.

Male Sterilization

Vasectomy — the vas deferens is cut or blocked to prevent sperm from entering the semen.

📝 Question 1 of 5
What is the male sterilization procedure?
Vasectomy is the cutting or blocking of the vas deferens to prevent sperm from entering the semen.
📝 Question 2 of 5
Which statement about permanent contraceptive methods is TRUE?

12 WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC)

WHO categories for initiating or continuing contraceptive use:

1 No restrictions for use
2 Generally use; some follow-up may be needed
3 Usually not recommended; requires clinical judgment & access to services
4 Method should not be used

The WHO Quick Reference Chart provides detailed condition-by-condition recommendations for initiating or continuing COCs, DMPA, implants, and Cu-IUD across a wide range of medical conditions (hypertension, DVT, diabetes, liver disease, HIV, STIs, etc.).

Important: A woman may fall into different categories depending on whether she is initiating or continuing a method. Each condition is assessed individually against the chosen method.

13 Review Question

Question: Describe different types of contraceptive methods.

Key areas to cover: physiological (periodic abstinence, withdrawal, LAM) → barrier (condoms, diaphragm, spermicide) → hormonal (combined pills/injectable/patch/ring, progestin-only injectable/implants/pills/ring) → IUDs (copper, LNG) → emergency contraception (POPs, COCs, IUD) → permanent (tubal ligation, vasectomy). For each method, know: mechanism, effectiveness, side effects, advantages, contraindications, and special situations.

📝 Question 1 of 5

📝 Question 2 of 5
What does WHO MEC Category 4 indicate?
Category 4 means the method should not be used due to unacceptable health risk.
