Cesarean Section — Definition & Incidence

Definition: It is delivery of a viable fetus through an abdominal and uterine incision. It's the most common major operation performed globally.

KEY DEFINITION
Delivery of a viable fetus through an abdominal and uterine incision.

Incidence: Globally, in the last 35 years, the rate of cesarean section (CS) has steadily increased from 5% to 30%. Egypt has a higher incidence of cesarean section rate approaching 70%.

CLINICAL PEARL
Egypt's CS rate (≈70%) is more than double the global average (≈30%), reflecting a significant trend toward operative delivery.

Cesarean Section — Indications

A — Fetal Indications:

  • Non reassuring fetal heart rate tracing.
  • Non vertex presentation.
  • Fetal anomalies, such as hydrocephalus that would make successful vaginal delivery difficult.
  • Umbilical cord prolapse.
  • Conjoined twins.

B — Maternal Indications:

  • Obstruction of lower genital tract (e.g. large cervical fibroid).
  • Previous CS (if vaginal birth after CS {VBAC} is declined or not appropriate).
  • History of severe pelvic floor injury from a prior vaginal delivery.
  • Abdominal cerclage.

C — Maternal and Fetal Indications:

  • Abruptio placentae.
  • Active herpes simplex virus infection.
  • Labor dystocia or cephalopelvic disproportion.
  • Placenta previa or known vasa previa.
MEMORY ANCHOR
The four major indications accounting for >70% of operations:
1. Previous C-section
2. Dystocia
3. Malpresentation
4. Suspected acute fetal compromise

Absolute Indications of CS:

  1. Extreme degree of pelvic contraction.
  2. Soft tissue obstruction of lower genital tract (e.g. huge cervical fibroid).
  3. Gross anomalies of the fetus.
  4. Major degree of placenta previa.

Contraindications of CS:

🚫 Contraindication 1
Dead fetus.
🚫 Contraindication 2
Gross anomalies of the fetus.

Timing of the Operation:

(1) Elective
The operation is done before the onset of labor.
(2) Selective
The operation is done after the onset of labor.
ELECTIVE CESAREAN SECTION
If it is decided during pregnancy that CS will be the method of delivery, the operation may be done about the end of pregnancy before the onset of labor (usually one week before the calculated date of delivery) and is called elective cesarean section.

Anesthesia for CS:

  • General inhalation anesthesia.
  • Regional (spinal and epidural anesthesia).

Types of CS (according to uterine incision):

  1. Lower segment cesarean section: It is the operation of choice nowadays.
  2. Upper segment cesarean section (classical cesarean section).

Lower Segment Cesarean Section — Steps

OPERATION OF CHOICE
Lower segment cesarean section is the operation of choice nowadays.
  1. Disinfection of abdominal wall and toweling. Most commonly used skin incision is Pfannenstiel incision (transverse suprapubic incision) is done.
  2. After opening the abdomen, the loose peritoneum over the lower segment is incised transversely and retracted downwards with the bladder by Doyen's retractor.
  3. The lower part of lower uterine segment is incised transversely in the middle and the incision is widened (to become about 10 cm) by two fingers, or it may be widened by the scissors but in such cases, the incision should be curved with the concavity upwards to avoid injury to the uterine vessels. The membranes are ruptured.
  4. The head is extracted slowly by putting 2 fingers or one blade of forceps below it and applying moderate pressure on the fundus. If the head is impacted in the pelvis it may be extracted by the Wrigley's forceps through the uterine incision, or an assistant may push the head upwards by a hand in the vagina to help its extraction.
  5. In breech presentation, the fetus is extracted gently after grasping the feet. In shoulder presentation, the hand is introduced through the uterine incision to grasp a foot and the fetus is extracted gently as breech.
  6. The umbilical cord is cut between 2 kochers and the infant is given to a nurse while the placenta and membranes are removed.
  7. The uterine incision is closed in 2 layers:
    • The 1st layer for the deep portion of the muscle.
    • The 2nd layer for the rest of the muscle.
  8. The blood and liquor amnii from the abdomen are removed and the abdominal wound is closed in layers.

Upper Segment Cesarean Section (Classical CS)

Definition: It is a cesarean section where a median vertical uterine incision is done through the upper uterine segment.

Indications:

Placenta previa and accreta.
The presence of extensive adhesions.
Large fibroid in the lower segment.
Repaired high vesicovaginal or vesicocervical fistula.
Cases of neglected shoulder presentation.
If hysterectomy is planned to be done after CS.
Cases needing very rapid delivery as severe fetal distress or massive antepartum hemorrhage.
Contraction ring.

Technical Points of Upper Segment CS:

  • The abdominal skin is opened by either a paramedian incision 1/3 above and 2/3 below the umbilicus (or may be by a midline subumbilical incision).
  • The upper segment is opened by a vertical incision (about 12 cm) in the middle line midway between the two round ligaments as it is the least vascular area.
  • The buttocks are usually encountered and so the infant is delivered as breech.
  • The uterine incision is closed in 3 layers:
    • The first for deep layer of the muscles.
    • The second for the main bulk of the muscle layer.
    • The third for the superficial muscle layer and the peritoneum.

CS — Skin Incisions

REMEMBER
The skin incisions of CS will vary according to the type of cesarean section whether lower segment or upper segment:
Lower Segment CS
Transverse skin incisions: Pfannenstiel incision (mainly) or Joel Cohen incision.
Upper Segment CS
Subumbilical median or paramedian incisions.
NOTE
A and B incisions refer to both Pfannenstiel and Joel Cohen incisions respectively. C incision refers to midline subumbilical incision.

CS — Complications & VBAC

Complications of Cesarean Section:

Hemorrhage: which is mainly due injuries of uterine arteries.
Injury to the bladder leading to fistula.
Complications of anesthesia.
Shock due significant intraoperative blood loss especially due to uterine injuries or intraoperative uterine atony.
Postoperative infection.
Paralytic ileus.
Postoperative thrombosis and pulmonary embolism.
Adhesions and intestinal obstruction.
Rupture of the scar in subsequent pregnancies.

Vaginal Birth After CS (VBAC):

  • The dictum "once cesarean always cesarean" is changed now to "once lower segment cesarean always hospital delivery (in a well-equipped hospital)."
  • Repeated CS is done if it is indicated.
  • If there is no indication for repeated CS, a trial of vaginal delivery is allowed in hospital and the patient should be carefully observed during labor (VBAC).
  • This applies for lower segment cesarean section otherwise no vaginal delivery trial is allowed for women who had been delivered by upper segment CS because of higher incidence of ruptured uterus (4% or more).
DANGER
No vaginal delivery trial is allowed for women who had been delivered by upper segment CS because of higher incidence of ruptured uterus (4% or more).

Cesarean Hysterectomy & Sterilization

Indications of Cesarean Hysterectomy (hysterectomy during CS):

Uncontrolled postpartum hemorrhage.
Concealed accidental hemorrhage when the uterus doesn't respond to the usual measures to control atony.
Operable carcinoma of the cervix.
Placenta accreta.
Multiple fibroids in a relatively old patient.
Postmortem CS: It's emergency CS within 10 minutes of the death of the mother in order to save the living fetus.
POSTMORTEM CS
Emergency CS within 10 minutes of the death of the mother in order to save the living fetus.

Sterilization during CS:

  • The patient may ask for sterilization during the third or fourth c-section.
  • Sterilization is performed by excision of a portion of both tubes and ligation or bilateral excision of interstitial portion of the fallopian tube.

Dilatation & Curettage (D&C)

DEFINITION
It is a surgical procedure in which evacuation of uterine contents or products of conception is performed when indicated.

Steps:

  1. Dorsal lithotomy position.
  2. General, regional or local anesthesia after Betadine disinfection of the perineum and vagina.
  3. Sounding of the uterus after introduction of self-retaining vaginal speculum.
  4. General gradual dilatation of the cervix up to Hegar's 10 or more.
  5. The ovum forceps introduced into the uterus and then opened to grasp the contents, which is extracted while rotating the forceps.
  6. IV infusion of 10 IU oxytocin in 500 cc glucose 5% or saline.
  7. The uterus is curetted to remove any remaining products of conception fragment and decidua.

Causes of Bleeding During or After Evacuation:

Remaining parts of products of conception.
Trauma of the cervix or uterine perforation.
Atony of the uterus.

Treatment of Bleeding after D&C:

  • Check for missed uterine contents by more curettage.
  • Repair (suturing) of traumatized cervix.
  • Ecbolics for uterine atony.

Complications:

  • Anesthetic complications.
  • Uterine trauma (perforation - cervical tears).
  • Infection.

Forceps Delivery

DEFINITION
It is a double-bladed metal instrument used for fetal head extraction.

Indications:

  • A) Prolonged second stage.
  • B) To shorten the second stage.
  • C) Fetal indications: such as fetal distress, prolapsed pulsating cord, preterm delivery and after-coming head in breech delivery.

Types of Forceps:

Long curved obstetric forceps.
Wrigley / Simpson forceps.
Kielland's forceps — used in cases of deep transverse arrest of fetal head.
Piper forceps — characterized by long perineal curve for assisting the delivery of after coming head during breech assisted vaginal delivery.

Conditions That Should Be Fulfilled Before Forceps Application:

Anesthesia.
Adequate pelvic outlet.
Aseptic measures.
Bladder and bowel evacuation.
Contractions of the uterus.
Fully dilated cervix.
Engaged head and favorable positions.
Rupture membranes.

Complications of Forceps Delivery:

Complications of anesthesia.
Lacerations.
Bone injuries and pelvic nerves injury.
Postpartum hemorrhage.
Puerperal infections, genital prolapse and vesicovaginal fistula.
Fracture fetal skull and cephalohematoma.
Fetal intracranial hemorrhage and facial nerve palsy.
Trauma to fetal face or eyes and asphyxia.

Vacuum Extraction

DEFINITION
Traction of fetal head using a cup applied near occiput by negative pressure.

Indications: The same as forceps.

Contraindications:

1 — Severe cephalo-pelvic disproportion.
2 — Other presentation than vertex.
3 — Premature fetus.
4 — Intact membranes.

Complications:

Fetal:
  • Cephalohematoma.
  • Scalp laceration.
  • Rarely, intracranial hemorrhage.
Maternal:
  • Lacerations.
  • Annular detachment of cervix.

Episiotomy

DEFINITION
An incision into the perineal body to enlarge the outlet area and facilitate delivery.

Types:

Median Type
Easier to be repaired but there is a possible risk for unintended extension to the anal sphincter complex.
Mediolateral Type
It requires more skills for its repair.

Anesthesia: Local infiltration anesthesia.

Indications:

  1. When there is impending perineal tear.
  2. Vaginal delivery after complete perineal tear repair.
  3. In cases of Forceps or ventouse delivery, breech and preterm delivery.
SOURCE IMAGE · img-013
Figure: Types of episiotomy — median (midline) and mediolateral incisions through the perineal body.
Source: Provided Material (p. 256)

Anesthesia: Local infiltration anesthesia.

  • There are three parts: vaginal mucosal, perineal part and skin of perineal part.
  • The vaginal part is repaired by continuous absorbable sutures meanwhile the second part (perineal part) is repaired by either continuous absorbable sutures or intermittent sutures. The skin is preferably closed by subcutaneous absorbable sutures.

Complications:

  • Vulvar hematoma (due to lack of good surgical hemostasis).
  • Infection of episiotomy wound.
  • Rectovaginal fistula in case of bad surgical repair.
  • Allergic reaction to local infiltration anesthesia.

Version

DEFINITION
Version is turning out of fetus from one presentation to another and may be done either externally or internally by the physician.

If the aim is to make the head the presenting part, it is called cephalic version and if the breech will be the presenting part it is called podalic version.

Types of Version:

External cephalic version.
Internal cephalic version.
Bipolar version.

External Cephalic Version: (No need for anesthesia)

PROCEDURE
It is a procedure done antenatally after 36 weeks gestation in order to turn a fetus inside the uterus through "external" maneuvers from a breech position or transverse position into a cephalic presentation.

Indications:

  • Breech presentation.
  • Transverse lie or oblique lie.

Internal Podalic Version: during labor (General anesthesia is required)

PROCEDURE
Turning the fetus inside the uterus through "internal" maneuvers to achieve breech presentation followed by breech extraction.
NOTE
Nowadays, the only remaining indication for such procedure is the presence of transverse lie of a second co-twin after delivery of the first twin.

Bipolar Version: during labour (General anesthesia is required)

PROCEDURE
It is a procedure done for correction of a transverse lie of a dead fetus where one or two fingers are introduced through the cervix with the other hand on the abdomen. At least two fingers are passed through partially dilated cervix where a fetal foot is grasped and pulled through the cervix while the other physician hand is assisting the version externally.
