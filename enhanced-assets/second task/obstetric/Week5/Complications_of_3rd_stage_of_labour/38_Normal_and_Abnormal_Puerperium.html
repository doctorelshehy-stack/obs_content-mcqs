2 Definition of the Puerperium

Puerperium is the time from the delivery till 6 weeks (42 days) post-partum, during which most of the changes of pregnancy, labor, and delivery have resolved and the body has reverted to the non-pregnant state.

Why "6 weeks"? By the end of 6 weeks the uterus has reached its non-pregnant size, the genital tract has largely healed, and the systemic adaptations of pregnancy have reverted. The puerperium is therefore also called the "fourth trimester" — a period of profound physiological readjustment rather than a passive recovery phase.
The 6 W's of puerperium recall: Womb involution (uterus back to 60-80 g), Wound healing (cervix, vagina, perineum), Weaning preparation (lactation physiology), White striae (striae albicans), Weight loss (fluid + uterine), Watch for sepsis (puerperal pyrexia rule).

3 Genital tract changes

Five organ systems undergo characteristic involutional changes during the puerperium:

1. Uterus

Weight

  • Immediately after labor: 1000 gm
  • After 1 wk: 500 gm
  • After 2 wks: 250 gm
  • By the end of 6 wks: 60-80 gm (non-pregnant weight)

Level (height of the fundus)

  • Immediately after labor: at the umbilicus
  • By the end of 6 wks: reaches the non-pregnant size
Involution rule of thumb: the uterine fundus descends by ~1-2 cm (one finger-breadth) per day in the first 10-14 days. By day 10-14 it should no longer be palpable abdominally. Failure to follow this pattern = suspect subinvolution (§7).

2. Cervix

  • After 1 wk: the external os is closed to the extent that a finger could not be easily introduced

3. Vagina

  • Diminishes in size and the rugae begin to reappear in the woman who is not breastfeeding
Lactation suppresses estrogen-driven vaginal mucosal recovery. The rugae (vaginal folds) reappear faster in non-lactating women because lactating mothers remain in a hypo-estrogenic state for as long as they breastfeed. This also explains the dyspareunia commonly reported by breastfeeding mothers.

4. Vulva and perineum

  • Swelling and engorgement are completely gone within 1-2 weeks

5. Ovary

Return of ovulation and menses will be:

  • 1.5-2 months — if non-lactating
  • 6 months or earlier — if lactating
Lactational amenorrhea is contraception (LAM): Exclusive breastfeeding in the first 6 months, with amenorrhea preserved, is ~98% effective as contraception — but only if all 3 LAM criteria are met (exclusive, amenorrhea, <6 months). Lactating women can therefore ovulate as early as 6 months, hence the §6 emphasis on counseling about future contraception BEFORE discharge.

4 General body changes

Puerperal changes extend well beyond the genital tract. Every system mentioned below must be reviewed in the daily ward round.

SystemExpected puerperal change
Pulse Normal. Tachycardia (>100 b/m) indicates infection or anemia.
Temperature May increase in the 1st 24 hrs (after muscular effort during labor) but should be < 38°C and lasting < 24 hrs.
Breasts Larger, firmer, and heavier. The colostrum (already present in the breasts and suckling by the newborn triggers its release) is the liquid that is initially released by the breasts during the first 2-3 days after delivery.
After pains Painful uterine contractions occur in early puerperium increasing with suckling due to oxytocin release.
Abdominal wall Gradual regain of tone. Striae become white (albicans).
Urinary tract Increase diuresis after delivery.
GIT Constipation may occur due to:
  • Atony of the intestine
  • Laxity of the abdomen
  • Decreased fluid intake
Blood Increased liability to thrombosis during the first two weeks especially with decreased fluid intake. Hematological changes gradually reversed.
Early-postpartum pyrexia ≠ puerperal sepsis. A temperature < 38°C lasting < 24 hrs after labor is physiological (muscular effort). It becomes pathological (puerperal pyrexia — see §7) only if it reaches 38°C AND lasts 24 hrs OR more AND occurs within the first 10 days.
Postpartum thromboembolism window: Pregnancy is hypercoagulable; this state persists for ~2 weeks postpartum. Combine with relative immobility and the dehydration of labor → the early puerperium is the highest-risk window for DVT/PE in the entire pregnancy-postpartum continuum. §6 ambulation advice is therefore not optional.
Colostrum vs milk: Colostrum is the first 2-3 days' secretion, rich in IgA, leukocytes and protein, lower in fat. "True" milk (mature milk) comes in around day 3-5 and may be preceded by engorgement — which is one cause of puerperal pyrexia (see §7).
After-pains are not a complication — they are a sign of effective involution. Each painful contraction is the uterus shrinking. They are typically more severe in multiparous women (less uterine muscle tone) and worsen during suckling (oxytocin surge). Reassurance + simple analgesia is the appropriate response.

What the figure is telling you: At delivery the fundus is at the umbilicus and the uterus is palpable suprapubically. By Day 9 it has retracted entirely into the true pelvis and is no longer palpable above the symphysis pubis. This 9-day descent mirrors the weight numbers in §3 (1000 g → ~500 g by 1 week).
Clinical use: Daily fundal-height measurement in the first 10 postpartum days is the bedside equivalent of this diagram. A fundus that fails to descend (or that is boggy/tender) = suspect retained products, infection, or both — the two top causes of subinvolution in §7.

6 Management of normal puerperium

The 11 pillars of routine postpartum care, all stated verbatim in the source. Every item should appear in the discharge checklist.

1Vital signs — frequent observation is done after delivery.
2Uterus — Uterine massage with monitoring of the amount of vaginal bleeding should be done every 15 min. for at least 1 hr.
3Position in bed — Semi-sitting position encourages drainage of lochia.
4Ambulation — Movement in and outside the bed and breathing exercises are advised to minimize the risk of deep venous thrombosis (DVT).
5Diet — 2 hrs after uncomplicated vaginal delivery, a woman is allowed to eat with no dietary restriction.
6Perineal care — The vulva and perineum are washed with antiseptic lotion. If there is perineal stitches, add local antibiotic.
7Breasts care — Before each feed, wash the nipple and areola with warm water and soap.
8Bowel care — Advice the patient to eat green vegetables and fruits to avoid constipation.
9Newborn care — If the mother is Rh –ve and her baby Rh +ve then anti-D should be given in the 1st 72 hours post-delivery.
10Pelvic floor exercise — By alternating contraction and relaxation of the pelvic floor muscles.
11Future contraception — It is very important to counsel the mother about birth control options before she leaves the hospital.
Anti-D timing is non-negotiable: 72 hours = the maximum window. Give within that window, dose by Kleihauer-Betke test when fetomaternal hemorrhage is suspected (e.g. abdominal trauma, antepartum hemorrhage). Do not wait until discharge to sort this out.
Why "every 15 min for at least 1 hour"? This is the highest-risk window for primary postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) — the first hour after delivery of the placenta. The 15-minute interval catches early atony before it becomes catastrophic. After the first hour, the 4th-stage observation can be relaxed to 30 min, then hourly.
Diet — "2 hrs after uncomplicated vaginal delivery" is conditional. It does NOT apply after a cesarean section (NPO until bowel sounds return), and it should be delayed in any delivery complicated by hemorrhage, sepsis, or prolonged labor. Verify the delivery was truly uncomplicated before clearing oral intake.
The 11-pillar discharge checklist is the minimum, not the maximum. Add: rubella vaccine if non-immune, tetanus toxoid if wound care is needed, family-planning counseling (item 11), screening for postnatal depression, and scheduled follow-up at 1 week and 6 weeks.

7 Abnormal puerperium · Subinvolution & Puerperal pyrexia

A. Uterine sub-involution

Sub-involution: The uterus did not regress to its pre-pregnancy size by the end of the puerperium.

Etiology (4 causes)

  1. Retained placental fragments
  2. Infection
  3. Antepartum overdistension e.g. multiple pregnancy
  4. Non-lactating women

Treatment

  • Treatment of the cause
  • Ecbolics and antibiotics
The 4 etiologies = R-I-A-N mnemonic: Retained products · Infection · Antepartum overdistension · Non-lactating. The single most common cause is retained products — and the single most dangerous is infection, because the open uterine sinuses of an involuting uterus are a direct route to puerperal sepsis.
"Ecbolics" = uterine-contracting drugs (oxytocin, methylergonovine, misoprostol, carboprost). They are used here to accelerate involution AND to control the secondary bleeding that often accompanies subinvolution. Antibiotics are added because the most common underlying cause (retained products) is also a culture medium for ascending infection.

B. Puerperal pyrexia

Puerperal pyrexia: a rise of temperature reaching 38°C or more and lasting 24 hours or more during the first 10 days of puerperium.

Causes (7 in source order)

  1. Milk engorgement (most common)
  2. Puerperal sepsis (most serious)
  3. Urinary tract infection
  4. Breast infection
  5. Respiratory infection
  6. Deep venous thrombosis
  7. Intercurrent febrile illness as typhoid
Rule that overrides the differential: "Any case of puerperal pyrexia should be considered puerperal infection (sepsis) until proved otherwise." (verbatim source sentence). In practice this means: take cultures, start broad-spectrum antibiotics, and only then narrow the differential.
Milk engorgement — the common cause
Typically days 3-5 postpartum. Breasts become hard, full, warm and tender. Low-grade fever possible. Managed by frequent feeding/expression, cold compresses between feeds, warm compresses before feeds, simple analgesia. NOT a reason to give antibiotics.
Puerperal sepsis — the serious cause
Endometritis is the classic lesion (polymicrobial ascending infection of the decidua). High fevers, foul lochia, uterine tenderness, tachycardia. Blood cultures, IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (ampicillin + gentamicin + clindamycin is a textbook choice), source control (evacuation of retained products if present).
Most common ≠ most serious. Milk engorgement is by far the most frequent cause of puerperal pyrexia but is benign. Puerperal sepsis is rarer but historically the leading cause of maternal mortality — hence the "sepsis until proved otherwise" rule that appears as the last line of the source.
Mnemonic for the 7 causes — "M-P-U-B-R-D-I": Milk engorgement · Puerperal sepsis · Urinary tract · Breast · Respiratory · DVT · Intercurrent illness. "MPUB-RDI" rolls off the tongue and keeps the order straight.
Septic pulmonary emboli from DVT can present as puerperal pyrexia + pleuritic chest pain + hypoxia in the first 2 postpartum weeks — exactly when §4 said thrombosis risk is highest. Don't anchor on endometritis in a woman with unilateral leg swelling.

C. Summary table — normal vs abnormal puerperium

Domain Normal puerperium Abnormal puerperium
Uterine size 1000 g → 60-80 g over 6 weeks; fundus at umbilicus at delivery, not palpable by Day 10-14 Sub-involution: uterus fails to regress to pre-pregnancy size by end of puerperium
Temperature May rise < 38°C in first 24 hrs after labor, lasting < 24 hrs (muscular effort) Puerperal pyrexia: ≥ 38°C and lasting ≥ 24 hrs in first 10 days
Lochia / bleeding Red → serous → white, decreasing over 2-6 weeks Excess bleeding, foul-smelling lochia, retained clots
Thrombosis risk Increased in first 2 weeks; mitigated by ambulation DVT/PE in same window if immobility, dehydration, thrombophilia
First clinical rule-out — Any puerperal pyrexia = puerperal sepsis until proved otherwise

8 Student activity · Questions · References

📘 Student activity

Clinical skills rotation: The students are requested to attend outpatient clinic in order to check with the clinic specialist the physiological changes which occur in the puerperium of postpartum attendant women.

What to do in the clinic: For each postpartum woman, perform a structured 6-week review: (1) menstrual return (lactation status), (2) uterine size on bimanual exam, (3) lochia pattern, (4) perineal healing, (5) bladder/bowel function, (6) breastfeeding, (7) mood screen (postnatal depression), (8) contraception plan. Document findings and compare to the §3-§4 expected changes.
