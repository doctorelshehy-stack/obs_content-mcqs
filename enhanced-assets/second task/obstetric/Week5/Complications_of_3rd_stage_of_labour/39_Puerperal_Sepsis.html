Definition

KEY DEFINITION It is a genital tract infection resulted from bacterial invasion during or after labor or abortion.

CLINICAL PEARL "Puerperal sepsis" is specifically a genital tract infection — distinct from generalised sepsis which can originate elsewhere. The timing window is broad: during labor, after labor, or after abortion.

Causative Organisms
Aerobic Organisms
Gram +ve
  • Hemolytic streptococcus group A (severe cases)
  • Non-hemolytic streptococci
  • Staphylococcus aureus
Gram −ve
  • E. coli
  • Pseudomonas
  • Klebsiella
Anaerobic Organisms
Gram +ve
  • Anaerobic streptococci (the commonest)
Gram −ve
  • Cl. Welchii
  • Bacteroids
MEMORY ANCHOR Remember A-G-A: Aerobic Gram+ / Gram− / Anaerobic Gram+ (commonest) + Gram− (Cl. Welchii, Bacteroids). Anaerobic streptococci = the single most common organism.

Mode of Infection
Endogenous Origin

It may be present in the genital tract as anaerobic streptococci which are normal non-pathogenic commensals that become pathogenic in presence of devitalized tissues.

Exogenous Origin

From infected attendants, dust, instruments…etc.

CLINICAL PEARL The endogenous pathway is the more insidious: organisms that were harmless commensals before delivery become virulent once devitalised tissue (lacerations, retained products) provides a substrate.

Predisposing Factors
General

Bad general condition as anemia, diabetes and debilitating diseases.

Local — 7 Risk Factors
  • Premature rupture of membranes.
  • Prolonged labor.
  • Instrumental delivery.
  • Lacerations.
  • Marked blood loss.
  • Retained products of conception.
  • Improper aseptic techniques.
MEMORY ANCHOR Mnemonic P-P-I-L-M-R-I: PPROM → Prolonged labor → Instrumental delivery → Lacerations → Marked blood loss → Retained products → Improper aseptic technique.

Sites of Infection & Pathology
I — Primary Sites
  1. Lacerations of the vulva, vagina or cervix.
  2. Uterus: placental site, retained placental tissue or membranes.
II — Secondary Sites

Usually occur as an extension from the primary sites via:

  1. Pelvic spread: Acute PID, Parametritis, pelvic abscess.
  2. Blood spread: may → bacteremia or septicemia.
DANGER Blood spread can rapidly progress to septicemia → septic shock → multi-organ failure. Early recognition of secondary spread is critical.

Diagnosis
A — History: of Risk Factors

Symptoms: Appear shortly (48–72 hours) following labor or abortion.

  1. Fever and rigors.
  2. Abdominal pain and tenderness.
  3. Offensive vaginal discharge.
  4. Symptoms of complications.
B — Examination
  • General: Temperature > 38°C for 24 hours.
  • Abdominal: Subinvoluted tender uterus.
  • Local: Offensive vaginal discharge, 2ry postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Speculum Examination: to inspect cervix and vagina.
WARN The 48–72 hour window after labor/abortion is the classic presentation time. Any fever > 38°C persisting for 24 hours in this window should raise immediate suspicion of puerperal sepsis.

Complications
A — Early Complications
  • Spread to secondary sites e.g. pelvic abscess.
  • Complications of septicemia and pyemia.
B — Remote Complications
  • Infertility.
  • Ectopic pregnancy.
  • Asherman's syndrome.
  • Sheehan's syndrome.
DANGER Remote complications (infertility, ectopic pregnancy, Asherman's, Sheehan's) can appear months to years after the initial infection — long-term follow-up is essential.
CLINICAL PEARL Sheehan's syndrome = postpartum pituitary necrosis due to hemorrhage + hypotension + sepsis — remember the triad: hemorrhage + sepsis + hypotension.

Investigations
Laboratory & Imaging
  • Blood picture: leucocytosis.
  • Urine analysis and culture: midstream or catheter specimen.
  • Swab and culture: from the cervix and upper vagina for aerobic and anaerobic cultures.
  • Blood culture.
  • U/S: retained parts and pelvic abscess.
NOTE Swab must cover both aerobic and anaerobic cultures — most cases are caused by anaerobic streptococci, which a standard aerobic-only culture would miss.

Prevention — 3 Phases
Antenatal
  • Proper diet, vitamins and minerals.
  • Anemia and diabetes should be treated.
  • Local or distant infection should be treated.
Intranatal
  • Strict aseptic measures for the patient, attendants and instruments.
  • Minimize vaginal examinations.
  • Avoid excessive blood loss.
  • Lacerations should be properly sutured immediately.
  • Prophylactic antibiotics in PROM and prolonged or instrumental delivery.
Postnatal
  • Maintenance of aseptic precautions.
  • Care of the perineal or abdominal wounds.
  • Early isolation of cases of puerperal sepsis.
MEMORY ANCHOR Prevention follows a chronological triad: Antenatal → Intranatal → Postnatal (A-I-P). Each phase has its own specific measures.

Treatment
General Measures
  • Isolation: in a separate room or fever hospital.
  • Diet: light diet rich in vitamins and minerals with plenty of fluids.
  • Supportive treatment: restoration of fluid and electrolyte balance and correction of anemia.
Symptomatic Treatment
  • Analgesics for pain.
  • Antipyretics and cold fomentations for fever.
Observations

Pulse, temperature, blood pressure, vaginal bleeding, lochia, manifestations of DVT.

Antibiotic Therapy
  • Broad spectrum antibiotic (ampicillin or cephalosporin) + gentamycin + metronidazole
  • or Clindamycin + gentamycin.
  • Antitoxin serum is given in Cl. welchii infection.
WARN Metronidazole covers anaerobes (the commonest organisms). Do not use metronidazole alone — always combine with a broad-spectrum agent + gentamycin for aerobic Gram −ve coverage.
Promotion of Drainage
  • Fowler's or semi-sitting position.
  • Removal of stitches if there is purulent discharge from a wound.
  • Misoprostol or ergometrine: for drainage of lochia.
Incision and Drainage of the Abscess
  • In pelvic abscess: posterior colpotomy + drain.
  • In parametric abscess: incision + drain at the pointing point.
Treatment of Complications
  • Treatment of any other complications as DIC or renal failure.
  • Treatment of thrombophlebitis: by anticoagulants.
DANGER Failure to drain a pelvic/parametric abscess leads to ongoing sepsis despite antibiotics. Drainage is definitive — antibiotics alone are insufficient for abscess.
