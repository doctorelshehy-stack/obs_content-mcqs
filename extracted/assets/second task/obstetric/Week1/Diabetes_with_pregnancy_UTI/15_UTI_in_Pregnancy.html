1Diagnostic Investigations

Three investigations are mentioned in the source for the work-up of bacteriuria in pregnancy. Each is appropriate to a different clinical scenario.

Mandatory

MSU culture

Culture of a midstream urine (MSU) sample is mandatory. Presence of > 100,000 organisms / mL is diagnostic of asymptomatic bacteriuria.

If pyrexia present

CBC + RFT

CBC and renal function tests (RFT) are indicated in the presence of pyrexia (rule out systemic involvement and renal impairment).

If infections are repeated

U/S of urinary tract

Ultrasound of the urinary tract is indicated in repeated infections (look for structural abnormality, calculus, obstruction, or residual urine).

PearlThe > 100,000 organisms / mL threshold is the classic Kass count for asymptomatic bacteriuria in any patient — but in pregnancy the threshold for treatment is far lower, because pregnancy predisposes to ascending infection. Any bacteriuria in pregnancy should be treated (see §2).
NoteSource abbreviates the ultrasound as U/S (not the radiology-standard "US"). Preserved verbatim in the card to maintain faithfulness to the source's typographic convention.

2Treatment

Antibiotic choice will depend on the sensitivity — i.e. guided by culture & sensitivity results when available. The four tiered regimens below are taken verbatim from the source page.

A · Asymptomatic bacteriuria

Oral antibiotic for 3 days.

3 days · oral

B · Cystitis

Patient with cystitis should receive treatment for 7 days.

7 days · oral

C · All bacteriuria in pregnancy

All bacteriuria in pregnancy should be treated to prevent pyelonephritis and preterm delivery.

Universal · guided by C&S

D · Pyelonephritis

Patient with pyelonephritis should be hospitalized and receive IV fluid, antipyretics, and parental [parenteral] broad-spectrum antibiotics as cephalosporins till the results of the culture appears or fever settles; then oral antibiotic according to the sensitivity should be continued for a total of 10–14 days.

10–14 days · IV → oral step-down

Step-down pathway (pyelonephritis)

  1. Hospitalize the patient.
  2. IV fluids + antipyretics.
  3. Parenteral broad-spectrum antibiotics — cephalosporins — start empirically.
  4. Continue parenteral therapy until either (a) culture results return, or (b) fever settles.
  5. Step down to oral antibiotic selected according to the sensitivity for a total of 10–14 days (IV + oral combined).
DangerPyelonephritis in pregnancy is a leading cause of septic shock, preterm labor, and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). The threshold to hospitalize is low, the threshold to image with renal/urinary-tract ultrasound is low, and the threshold to escalate to parenteral antibiotics is low. Any pregnant woman with fever + flank pain + pyuria is pyelonephritis until proven otherwise.
NoteThe source contains the typographical variant "parental broad spectrum antibiotics" — clinically this is parenteral (i.e. IV/IM) antibiotics, not the parents of the patient. This is a typo preserved verbatim in the original PDF and reproduced here for accuracy; clinical readers should read it as parenteral.

3Student Activity & References

Bedside / Lab Audit Task

Each student is requested to check the urine analysis of admitted departmental pregnant women for pyuria then evaluate pregnant women with +ve pyuria for symptoms of pyelonephritis with the aid of the tutor during bedside teaching part of clinical round.

NoteFor each case identified, the student should: (1) confirm pyuria on urine analysis, (2) take focused history for pyelonephritis symptoms (fever, flank pain, nausea, vomiting, costovertebral-angle tenderness), (3) check whether an MSU culture has been sent and the result reviewed, (4) note the current antibiotic regimen and whether it matches the sensitivity, and (5) escalate to senior staff if any red-flag feature is present.
