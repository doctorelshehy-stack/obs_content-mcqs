2Definition & Incidence

Definition

Systolic blood pressure ≥ 140 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure ≥ 90 mmHg on two separate occasions at least 6 hours apart during pregnancy.

Incidence: 10–20 % of all pregnancies.

DangerIt is the second leading cause of maternal death and perinatal morbidity and mortality after severe bleeding. Every antenatal clinic must screen for it at every visit.

3Classification of HTN in Pregnancy

Hypertension in pregnancy (HTNP) is classified into four categories:

1 · Chronic (pre-existing) hypertension

Diagnosed before the 20th week of gestation.

2 · Gestational hypertension

New hypertension after 20 weeks without proteinuria.

3 · Preeclampsia

Hypertension after 20 weeks with proteinuria / end-organ involvement.

4 · Chronic HTN + superimposed preeclampsia

Worsening BP + proteinuria after 20 weeks in a chronic hypertensive.

PearlThe 20-week threshold is the pivot of the classification: HTN appearing before 20 weeks = chronic; after 20 weeks with proteinuria = preeclampsia.

4Chronic (Pre-existing) Hypertension

Definition

BP ≥ 140/90 mmHg diagnosed either before pregnancy or develops before 20 weeks of gestation or both or persists more than 42 days postpartum.

Causes

  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Primary aldosteronism
  • Pheochromocytoma

Management

Mild chronic hypertensionSevere chronic hypertension
Serial antenatal screening. Start antihypertensive medications e.g. alpha methyldopa, labetalol or calcium channel blockers to maintain blood pressure less than 160/100 mmHg.
Serial blood pressure measurement. Serial BP measurement and urine protein assessment.
Serial urine protein assessment. Delivery at 37 wks.
Termination of pregnancy at term by induction of labor if cervix is favorable. If delivery planned before 34 wks, mother should be given steroids for fetal lung maturity.
WarnTarget BP in severe chronic HTN is < 160/100 mmHg — over-treating below this risks reduced uteroplacental perfusion and IUGR. The "lower is better" rule of chronic HTN in non-pregnant patients does not apply here.

5Chronic HTN + Superimposed Preeclampsia

Pre-existing hypertension associated with further worsening of BP after 20 weeks of gestation, followed by proteinuria (≥ 3 g/24 h).

NoteManagement is the same as preeclampsia (see §13). The superimposed category is reserved for the chronic hypertensive who develops new proteinuria or end-organ involvement — it carries the worst prognosis of the four categories.

6Gestational Hypertension

Definition

Pregnancy induced hypertension develops after 20 weeks of gestation with absence of proteinuria.

Diagnosis

  • No symptoms of preeclampsia.
  • Laboratory tests are normal.
  • Proteinuria is absent.
  • The key finding is sustained elevation of BP.

Management

  • Conservative outpatient management.
  • Serial laboratory testing to rule out preeclampsia development.
  • Antihypertensive treatment is only indicated if blood pressure exceeded 160/100 mmHg.

Time of Delivery

At term by either vaginal delivery if the cervix is favorable or by cesarean section.

WarnUp to 25–50% of gestational hypertension eventually progresses to preeclampsia — do not stop monitoring until 42 days postpartum. BP that persists > 42 days reclassifies the patient as chronic hypertensive.

7Preeclampsia — Definition, Risk Factors, Etiology

Definition

The presence of hypertension of at least 140/90 mmHg recorded on two separate occasions at least 4 hours apart and in the presence of at least 300 mg protein in a 24 hour's collection of urine after the 20th week gestation in a previously normotensive women.

Incidence: 5–10 % of pregnancies.

Risk factors

  • More in primigravida.
  • Conditions in which the placenta is enlarged (diabetes mellitus, multiple pregnancies, hydrops).
  • Pre-existing hypertension or renal diseases.
  • Pre-existing vascular disease (e.g. diabetes, autoimmune vasculitis).
  • Obesity.

Etiology

Preeclampsia is a disorder of unknown etiology that is peculiar to human pregnancy. Many theories regarding its etiology have been suggested, including:

  • Abnormal placentation.
  • Abnormal trophoblastic invasion. In normal implantation, the uterine spiral arteries undergo extensive remodeling as they are invaded by endovascular trophoblasts. In preeclampsia, there is incomplete trophoblastic invasion. Endothelial damage leads to pathologic capillary leak that can present in the mother as rapid weight gain, nondependent edema (face or hands), pulmonary edema, hemoconcentration, or a combination.
  • Immunologic phenomena.
  • Renin-angiotensin system.
  • Coagulation abnormalities.
  • Abnormal cardiovascular adaptation.
  • Dietary factors.
  • Genetic factors.
  • Vasculopathy and inflammatory changes.
PearlThe unifying hypothesis: incomplete trophoblastic invasion of spiral arteries → placental hypoperfusion → release of anti-angiogenic factors → systemic endothelial dysfunction → hypertension, proteinuria, edema.

8Pathology of Preeclampsia

Vasospasm and hemoconcentration are the main pathology of preeclampsia and account for the development of hypertension. They lead to vascular changes and local hypoxia of the surrounding tissues leading to hemorrhage, necrosis and other pathological changes.

  • 1. Central nervous system: ischemia, hemorrhages and oedema.
  • 2. Liver: subcapsular hemorrhage, periportal necrosis and infarctions.
  • 3. Kidney: decrease in renal blood flow, glomerular damage (glomerular endotheliosis) leading to:
    • decrease glomerular filtration rate by about 50%.
    • loss of protein in urine (albuminuria).
  • 4. Heart and lungs: myocardial and endocardial hemorrhage and necrosis. Lungs shows hemorrhage and secondary bronchopneumonia.
  • 5. Retina: hemorrhage, exudate and rarely retinal detachment in severe cases.
  • 6. Coagulation status:
    • Fibrin production is increased.
    • Fibrinolytic activity is decreased.
    • Factor VII, factor VIII concentrations are increased.
    • Fibrin degradation products (FDP) is increased.
    • Thrombocytopenia.
  • 7. Endocrine glands: necrosis and hemorrhage in pituitary, pancreas and adrenal glands.
DangerEvery organ system is a target — but the brain, liver, kidney, and coagulation system drive most of the maternal mortality. Glomerular filtration rate drops by ~50% even in "mild" preeclampsia.

9Diagnostic Criteria & Investigations

Signs

  1. Hypertension: Blood pressure: ≥ 140/90 mmHg after 20 weeks of gestation in a woman with previously normal blood pressure.
  2. Proteinuria: It is urinary protein greater than 0.3 gm/L in 24 hours collection or spot urine protein/creatinine ratio of 0.3 or greater.

Symptoms

  • Headache and blurred vision.
  • Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain.
  • Nausea and vomiting.

Investigations

  1. Complete urine examination: for proteinuria.
  2. Kidney function.
  3. Liver function tests.
  4. Coagulation status: Platelet count, fibrinogen and FDP as DIC may develop.
  5. Fundus examination.
  6. Fetal assessment tests:
    • Daily fetal movement assessment (kick counts).
    • Non stress test (NST) twice weekly.
    • Biophysical profile if nonreactive NST.
    • Amniotic fluid volume assessment weekly.
    • Ultrasound evaluation of fetal growth every 3 weeks.
WarnSymptoms are the RED FLAGS: headache + blurred vision + epigastric pain is the classic preeclampsia-with-severe-features triad — do not wait for BP to climb further.

10Screening of Preeclampsia

  • Mid-trimester mean blood pressure: If it is more than 90 mmHg, there is increased risk of developing PE.
  • Urinary assay for microalbuminuria.
  • Blood urate: serial increase is a warning sign.
  • Doppler velocimetry to detect uteroplacental hypoperfusion.
PearlUterine artery Doppler in the 2nd trimester (22–24 w) showing notch or raised resistance index is the single best non-invasive screen for preeclampsia in a primigravida.

11Types of Preeclampsia

Preeclampsia (mild)

Blood pressure < 160/110 mmHg with absence of symptoms and normal laboratory data.

Preeclampsia with severe features

Blood pressure: ≥ 160/110 mmHg on two occasions at least six hours apart in a woman on bed rest with:

  • ↑ Liver enzymes, hepatic hemorrhage & rupture.
  • ↑ Creatinine, acute tubular necrosis & renal failure.
  • HELLP syndrome.
  • IUGR, oligohydramnios, IUFD, placental abruption.
  • Presence of symptoms like headache, blurring of vision, epigastric or right upper quadrant pain, nausea and vomiting.

Other features

  • Oliguria (less than 500 ml of urine in 24 hours).
  • Pulmonary edema or cyanosis.
  • Thrombocytopenia (<100,000 platelets/ µL).
NoteIn 2013, The ACOG replaces the term of severe PE with a new term "preeclampsia with severe features".

12Complications of Preeclampsia

1 · Maternal effects

  • Accidental hemorrhage.
  • Acute renal failure.
  • HELLP syndrome (hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes and low platelet count).
  • Acute pulmonary edema & heart failure.
  • Ante partum eclampsia.
  • DIC.
  • Cerebrovascular accidents.
  • Residual and recurrent hypertension.

2 · Fetal effects

  • IUGR: due to the placental insufficiency caused by vasospasm.
  • IUFD.
DangerHELLP syndrome (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelet count) is the most feared maternal complication — and the only cure is delivery, regardless of gestational age.

13Treatment of Preeclampsia

Prophylactic

  1. Proper antenatal care: for early detection of high risk patients.
  2. Low dose aspirin.
  3. Calcium.
  4. Antioxidants.

Curative

Delivery remains the ultimate treatment for pre-eclampsia. When possible, vaginal delivery is preferable to avoid the added physiologic stressors of cesarean delivery. In case of cesarean delivery, regional anesthesia is preferred because it carries less maternal risk. In the presence of coagulopathy, use of regional anesthesia is contraindicated.

1 · General measures

Hospitalization for observation of maternal and fetal conditions.

2 · Medical treatment

  • Antihypetensive drugs: alpha-methyl-dopa (Aldomet), calcium channel blockers (Nifedipine), Labetalol and Hydralazine.
  • Diuretics: in cases of heart failure and pulmonary edema.

3 · Obstetric management

  • Magnesium sulphate to prevent convulsions (Its dose is 4–6 g loading dose followed by a continuous infusion at a rate of 1–2 gm/hour).
  • Control of maternal blood pressure within a safe range.
  • Induction of labor to initiate delivery.
DangerMgSO4 is the only drug that prevents eclampsia — every preeclampsia admission should be on a MgSO4 protocol. Antihypertensives control BP; they do not prevent fits.

14Timing of Delivery & Antihypertensive Dosing

Timing of delivery in preeclampsia

  • At ≥ 37 weeks → Delivery.
  • < 37 weeks → Expectant management (maternal and fetal monitoring).

Timing of delivery in preeclampsia with severe features

  • At ≥ 34 weeks: Delivery is indicated, after rapid maternal stabilization.
  • < 34 weeks: Expectant management is acceptable in a tertiary care center if blood pressure is adequately controlled with antihypertensive agents, good fetal and maternal status. Delivery is indicated if there is maternal or fetal complication.

Antihypertensives

DrugDose & ScheduleMaxTarget BP
Hydralazine 5–10 mg boluses every 20–30 min 20 mg Diastolic BP 90–100 mmHg and systolic BP 140–150 mmHg. Avoid hypotension because of the risk of decreased uteroplacental perfusion.
Labetalol 20–40 mg boluses, then 200 mg orally every 8 h 220 mg IV · 600 mg PO every 6 h
Nifedipine 10–20 mg orally every 30 min, then 10–20 mg every 4–6 h 50 mg acute · 120 mg/day
WarnThe therapeutic target in pregnancy is NOT "normal BP" — it is diastolic 90–100 / systolic 140–150. Drop below that and uteroplacental perfusion falls, risking fetal distress and IUGR.

15Severe PE — Flowchart & Indications

Severe PE diagnosed on the basis of blood pressure measurement or transient laboratory abnormality — at a tertiary health care hospital with on-site blood banking, ICU & NICU services.

Medications

  • Magnesium sulphate for 48 hours.
  • Antihypertensive drugs (initial I.V. then oral).
  • Steroids.

Observation

  • Maternal: Bp, urine protein, symptoms & weight.
  • Fetal: kicks, NST, BPP & serial U/S for growth & amniotic fluid.

Maternal indications (Urgent Delivery)

  • Symptoms.
  • Eclampsia (2%).
  • HELLP (20%).
  • Uncontrolled BP.
  • End organ damage (2–5%).
  • APH (abruption).
  • PROM or labour pains.

Fetal indications (Urgent Delivery)

  • Non-reassuring FHR tracing.
  • Persistent oligohydramnios.
  • FGR (40%).
  • Reaching 34 weeks.
WarnThe two strongest maternal triggers for delivery are HELLP (20%) and eclampsia (2%) — both make delivery non-negotiable regardless of gestational age.

16Initiation of the Process for Delivery

  • Vaginal delivery, unless obstetrically contraindicated, is the preferred method of delivery.
  • Oxytocin infusion for induction or augmentation of labor may be administered simultaneously with the magnesium sulphate infusion.
  • Total fluid intake is limited to 80–100 ml per hour (1 ml/kg/hour).
  • Continuous fetal monitoring should be performed.
  • Following delivery, the patient should be monitored under close observation for a minimum of 24 hours and magnesium sulphate should be continued for 48 hours.
PearlThe classic fluid-restriction rule (1 ml/kg/h) prevents iatrogenic pulmonary edema — the leading immediate cause of maternal death in severe preeclampsia is fluid overload, not BP.

17Eclampsia

Definition

Eclampsia is the development of convulsions or coma unrelated to other cerebral conditions during pregnancy or in the postpartum period in patients with signs and symptoms of preeclampsia.

Clinical features

It is that of PE plus fits (convulsions). The fit may follow stimuli of various kinds as visual, auditory or touch. Each fit occurs in 4 stages without intervals inbetween:

1

Premonitory stage

The pupils are dilated & the eyes move from side to side. Muscular twitches occur in the tongue, face & hands.

2

Tonic stage

All the voluntary muscles of the body undergo tonic contraction. The respiratory muscles are affected causing cyanosis, the swallowing muscles are affected causing accumulation of saliva in the mouth. The patient may acquire certain positions due to spasm of the muscles of the back.

3

Clonic stage

The voluntary muscles of the body undergo irregular intermittent contractions & relaxation. The tongue may be bitten. The patient may pass urine or stools involuntarily.

4

Coma stage

This stage may last for minutes, hours or even days. A new fit may start while the patient is still in coma or after recovery from coma. The body temperature rises during a fit because of increased muscular activity.

Causes

Cerebral edema and cerebral vasoconstriction leading to ischemia & cerebral hypoxia, microthrombi or retention of sodium ions causing cerebral irritability.

Differential diagnosis

  • Idiopathic: epilepsy.
  • Brain lesions: head trauma, encephalitis, abscess, tumor or aneurysm.
  • Metabolic: renal or hepatic failure, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia and hypoparathyroidism.
  • Toxic: arsenic poisoning, barbiturate withdrawal and alcohol withdrawal syndrome.

Types of eclampsia

Antepartum eclampsia

The fits start during pregnancy before the onset of labor.

Intrapartum eclampsia

The fits start for the first time during labor.

Postpartum eclampsia

The fits start after delivery, usually within the first 48 hours.

Signs of bad prognosis (Traditional or Eden's criteria)

Eclampsia is considered severe with bad prognosis if two or more of the following signs are present:

> 10Number of fits
> 6 hDuration of coma
> 120Pulse rate (beats/min)
> 39 °CTemperature
> 200Systolic BP (mmHg)
> 40Respiratory rate (/min)

Complications

  • Abruptio placentae.
  • Acute renal failure.
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage.
  • Pulmonary edema.
  • Aspiration pneumonia.
  • Retinal detachment.

Treatment

The basic principles in the management of eclampsia is stabilization of general condition by the following then termination of pregnancy.

Support of cardio-respiratory functions

  • Airway patency should be assessed and established to ensure adequate maternal oxygenation.
  • Suction should be used as needed and the patient protected from injury by making sure the bed side-rails are elevated and padded.
  • Oxygen should be administered to improve maternal oxygen concentration and to increase oxygen delivery to the fetus.

Control of convulsions and prevention of recurrent ones — Magnesium sulphate therapy

  • The dose is the same like preeclampsia.
  • Patients receiving magnesium sulphate therapy must be monitored for evidence of drug toxicity by:
    • Reflexes: absence of patellar reflex (first sign of toxicity).
    • Urinary output assessment: < 30 ml/hour in case of toxicity.
    • Respiratory rate: respiratory depression in case of toxicity.
  • In cases of magnesium toxicity: if magnesium toxicity is recognized, 10 ml of 10 % calcium gluconate is administered intravenously.

Delivery remains the ultimate treatment for eclampsia.

DangerThe 3-monitor check for MgSO4 toxicity: reflexes (patellar first to go) + urine output (<30 ml/h) + respiratory rate. The antidote is 10 ml of 10% calcium gluconate IV.
