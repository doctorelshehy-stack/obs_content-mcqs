1 ILOs of this chapter

  1. To understand the definition of Rh incompatibility and its etiology
  2. To be able to describe how such clinical problem can be diagnosed during pregnancy
  3. To be able to explain how to avoid the occurrence of such obstetric problem and how to manage it

2 Definition & Etiology

Rh incompatibility is an immunological disorder resulting in destruction of fetal red blood cells by antibodies which pass through placenta from maternal blood.

Rh genetics

There are 5 main Rh genes (C, D, E, c, and e); the most important gene is D. Rh factor is a lipoprotein component on the cell wall of red blood cells. About 85% of the population are Rh positive and about 15% are Rh negative.

When incompatibility occurs

It occurs when an Rh-negative mother is pregnant from an Rh-positive husband. The fetus will be Rh positive or Rh negative according to whether the father is homozygous (2D) or heterozygous (Dd):

  • Homozygous father: 100% of babies will be Rh positive.
  • Heterozygous father: 50% Rh positive and 50% Rh negative.

Sensitization triggers

Sensitization of an Rh-negative mother occurs when Rh-positive red blood cells enter maternal circulation:

During pregnancy

  • Placental separation (most important).
  • Abortion.
  • Ectopic pregnancy.
  • Vesicular mole.
  • Amniocentesis.
  • External cephalic version.
  • Antepartum hemorrhage.

Before pregnancy

  • Previous blood transfusion of Rh-positive blood.
  • In utero from her Rh-positive mother (grandmother syndrome).

3 Pathogenesis & Clinical varieties

When maternal antibodies pass into the fetal circulation they cause hemolysis of fetal RBCs and jaundice.

Pathogenesis cascade

Attachment of IgG to fetal RBCs causes hemolysis leading to:

  1. Anemia → anemic heart failure and generalized edema.
  2. Extramedullary hematopoiesis → hepatosplenomegaly → portal hypertension, ascites, hypoproteinemia, generalized edema.
  3. Excess bilirubin production.

Clinical varieties

Hydrops fetalis (most severe)
IUFD due to anemic heart failure. Macrosomic baby. Severe hemolytic anemia (cord Hb < 8 gm/dL), hepatosplenomegaly, generalized edema, ascites, scalp edema, hydrothorax, large edematous placenta.
Icterus gravis neonatorum (commonest)
Occurs after delivery. Nausea, vomiting, anemia (Hb 8-12 gm/dL), jaundice, hepatosplenomegaly. Severe cases: bilirubin crosses immature blood-brain barrier → kernicterus (extrapyramidal effects and convulsions). Yellow staining of amniotic fluid, cord and vernix.
Congenital hemolytic anemia (mildest)
Mild anemia at birth (Hb < 14 gm/dL), mild pallor and transient jaundice.

4 Investigations

I. Screening

  • Assessment of maternal and paternal blood group and Rh.
  • If mother is Rh-negative and husband Rh-positive: indirect Coomb's test to detect maternal immunization.
  • Critical titer: greater than 1/16 → significant possibility of sensitization & stillbirth.

II. Diagnosis of the affected fetus

During pregnancy: amniocentesis, cordocentesis, ultrasound.

After delivery: cord blood sampling.

Fetal or neonatal blood examined for:

  1. ABO group & Rh typing
  2. Hemoglobin & Hct value
  3. Reticulocytic count
  4. Direct Coomb's test
  5. Bilirubin level
  6. Serum protein level

Ultrasound

  • Preliminary before amniocentesis or cordocentesis.
  • Signs of hydrops fetalis: scalp edema, increased amniotic fluid, placental thickening, hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Doppler on middle cerebral artery (MCA) to predict fetal anemia by measuring peak systolic velocity.

Serial amniocentesis

Indications
High maternal antibody titer to certify degree of fetal hemolysis. Past history of hydrops fetalis or exchange transfusion.
Value
Indirect assessment of fetal RBC hemolysis by measuring amniotic fluid bilirubin by spectrophotometry plotted against gestational age on the Liley chart.

Timing

  • Before 22-24 weeks if high antibody titer.
  • Otherwise between 30-32 weeks.
  • If past history of IUFD: 10 weeks earlier than expected time of that event.

5 Management — Prophylactic & pregnancy

Prophylactic management

  • No Rh-positive blood is given to Rh-negative mother.
  • Anti-D immunoglobulin should be given to Rh-negative mothers after labor or abortion.

During pregnancy

Management algorithm by titer

If titer < 1/16 (unsensitized): no intervention required. At 28 weeks, a standard dose of 300 µg of anti-D immunoglobulin is administered.

If indirect Coomb's test above critical value: do amniocentesis and put results on Liley curve:

  • Zone 1 (A): Minimal or absent fetal affection. Allowed to deliver at term.
  • Zone 2 (B): Moderate affection (low & high zone B). Pregnancy can be allowed until AF bilirubin levels increase or until pregnancy reaches 32 weeks' gestation.
  • Zone 3 (C): Severe fetal affection. Risk of IUFD within 7-10 days. Must be delivered or transfused.
Modern update: MCA Doppler as a noninvasive test has eliminated the need for amniocentesis in many centers. Done every 1-2 weeks to detect anemia; testing may begin as early as 16 to 18 weeks. MCA peak systolic velocity is increased in severe anemia due to decreased blood viscosity.

Maternal plasmapheresis

To wash antibodies from maternal circulation. Indicated for severe cases before 24 weeks of pregnancy when intrauterine transfusion cannot be done.

6 Intrauterine fetal transfusion

Timing

24-32 weeks gestation.

Indications

  • Severe fetal affection: low hematocrit value, high zone 2 or zone 3 on Liley chart.
  • 10 weeks earlier than previous IUFD.

Type of blood

Group O, Rh-negative blood.

Procedure

Intraperitoneal
Needle passed into fetal peritoneal cavity guided by ultrasound.
Intravascular
Into the umbilical vein.

Amount of transfused blood: (Gestational age in weeks - 20) × 10 (i.e. 10 mL for each week after 20 weeks).

Complications

Fetal injuries, bradycardia, cord hematoma, fetal death, chorioamnionitis.

7 Obstetric & postpartum management

Route of delivery

  • Vaginal delivery preferred.
  • Caesarean section reserved for obstetric indication and compromised fetus.

Cord management at delivery

  • Cord immediately clamped to avoid further passage of antibodies from the placenta.
  • Divided 2-3 inches from the umbilicus to facilitate exchange transfusion.
  • Manual removal of the placenta should be avoided.

Postpartum screening & anti-D

Neonate Rh D-negative
Anti-D immunoglobulin is not necessary.
Neonate Rh D-positive + mother antibody negative
Standard dose of anti-D immunoglobulin is given in the 1st 72 hours.
Neonate Rh D-positive + mother antibody positive
No anti-D immunoglobulin. Mother's next pregnancy managed as Rh sensitized.
Both screened
Patient and infant are screened after delivery.

8 Neonate — Exchange transfusion & phototherapy

Immediate neonatal care

  • Immediate cord clamping.
  • Admission to neonatal ICU.
  • Cord blood sample for blood group, Rh, hemoglobin, hematocrit, serum bilirubin, direct Coomb's test.

Exchange transfusion — Indications

  1. Cord blood bilirubin > 5 mg%.
  2. Cord hemoglobin < 10 gm.
  3. Rapidly rising bilirubin in the first 24 hours.
  4. Clinical jaundice in the first 12 hours.
  5. Past history of severely affected baby.

Aims

  1. Correction of neonatal anemia.
  2. Removal of excess bilirubin.

Phototherapy & hydration

Used as adjuvant for exchange transfusion in cases of high but not toxic bilirubin.

Mechanism: Blue or blue-green light converts unconjugated bilirubin into non-toxic, water-soluble isomers which are excreted in bile and urine without the need for hepatic conjugation.

Phenobarbitone

Enzyme inducer that increases activity of glucuronyl transferase enzyme, leading to increased bilirubin conjugation and excretion.

9 ABO incompatibility

Differs from Rh incompatibility: Can affect the first baby because most group O women have Anti-A and Anti-B. Most are IgM type so cannot cross the placenta. No need for amniocentesis or preterm delivery. Mild condition that rarely causes significant anemia. Treated by phototherapy.

Comparison: ABO vs Rh incompatibility

Feature ABO incompatibility Rh incompatibility
First baby affected Yes (natural Anti-A / Anti-B) No (requires sensitization)
Antibody type crossing placenta Mostly IgM (cannot cross) IgG (crosses freely)
Amniocentesis / preterm delivery Not required Often required
Severity Mild; rare significant anemia Can be severe / fatal
Treatment Phototherapy Exchange transfusion ± IUT
Coomb's test Negative Positive

10 Causes of neonatal jaundice & Hydrops fetalis

Causes of neonatal jaundice

  1. Physiological jaundice.
  2. Rh incompatibility.
  3. ABO incompatibility.
  4. Congenital biliary atresia.
  5. Breast milk jaundice.
  6. Infections: hepatitis, STORCH.
  7. Congenital spherocytosis.
  8. Infant of diabetic mother.
  9. Drug induced.

Hydrops fetalis

Accumulation of fluid in the interstitial tissue (skin) and two or more body cavities (pleural, pericardial effusions or ascites).

Types

Immune hydrops fetalis
Due to Rh and ABO isoimmunization.
Non-immune hydrops fetalis — causes
Genetic: Aneuploidy syndromes.
Cardiovascular: Structural AVSD, hypoplastic Rt, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia.
Pulmonary: pulmonary lymphangiectasia.
Infections: CMV, Toxoplasma.
Renal/ GI: Congenital nephrosis, gut duplication, malrotation.
Hematological: Alpha thalassemia, aplastic anemia.
Placental: AV fistula, chorioangiomas.
Others: twin-to-twin transfusion, liver cirrhosis, true knots of the umbilical cord.
