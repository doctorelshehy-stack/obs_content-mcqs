Infectious Diseases with Pregnancy

Viral and protozoal infections in pregnancy are a leading cause of maternal and fetal morbidity. The TORCH acronym (Toxoplasma, Others, Rubella, Cytomegalovirus, Herpes) groups the pathogens that cross the placenta and produce congenital defects. Each pathogen has a unique transmission pattern, a distinct maternal vs fetal clinical picture, and a specific prevention / treatment strategy.

2Background & the TORCH Acronym

Why this chapter matters

Viral infections in pregnancy are major causes of maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality. Infections can develop in the neonate:

  • Trans-placentally.
  • Perinatally (from vaginal secretions or blood).
  • Postnatally (from breast milk or other sources).

TORCH — infections known to produce congenital defects

TToxoplasma

T. gondii — obligate intracellular protozoan. Cats are the definitive hosts. Congenital severity is highest in the 1st trimester.

OOthers

Parvovirus B19 (B19V), varicella-zoster virus (VZV), measles virus, enteroviruses, human immunodeficiency virus (HIV).

RRubella

RNA virus. Droplet infection. 2–3 week incubation. Congenital rubella syndrome is one of the most severe teratogenic infections.

CCytomegalovirus

Double-stranded DNA herpes virus. The most common congenital viral infection. Saliva, urine, body fluids, transplacental, breast milk.

HHerpes simplex

Double-stranded DNA virus. Sexual contact, ascending infection, transplacental. 50 % vertical transmission in primary HSV in labor — C-section is the prevention of choice.

Mnemonic

TORCH → Toxoplasma, Others, Rubella, CMV, Herpes — "the light of pregnancy". Each pathogen crosses the placenta and produces a characteristic congenital syndrome.

PearlThe classic TORCH screen (a single IgM / IgG panel) has been replaced in modern practice by pathogen-specific testing — maternal IgM + IgG with IgG avidity for CMV and toxoplasmosis, PCR on amniotic fluid for definitive fetal diagnosis, and viral PCR for HSV in active lesions. The acronym is still useful for memorization and clinical reasoning, but treatment decisions should be pathogen-specific.
NoteModern additions to the "Others" category include Zika virus (associated with microcephaly and other CNS defects), hepatitis B and C (vertical transmission at delivery, not classically teratogenic), and syphilis (congenital syphilis can produce a wide spectrum of findings — the "great imitator"). Not all of these are in the source's "Others" list; the visual retains the source's original list for source-faithfulness.

3Toxoplasmosis (T. gondii)

Pathogen & transmission

Caused by T. gondii, an obligate intracellular protozoan. Cats are the definitive hosts. Maternal infection is acquired by eating undercooked meat.

Congenital transmission

The severity of infection decreases with gestational age — most severe infections occur in the 1st trimester.

Clinical picture

Maternal

Acute maternal infection

Usually asymptomatic. Some patients may present with:

  • Posterior cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Fatigue.
  • Fever.
  • Maculopapular rash.
Fetal — Classic Triad

Congenital toxoplasmosis triad

1

Hydrocephalus

2

Intracranial calcifications

3

Chorioretinitis

Investigations

Maternal

By serology for detection of the specific IgM.

Fetal — PCR

Isolation of toxoplasma in the amniotic fluid by PCR.

Fetal — US

Ultrasound may be useful for detecting fetal anomalies as: hydrocephalus and intracranial calcifications.

Treatment

The drug of choice during pregnancy is Spiramycin, which can reduce the risk of congenital transmission.

PearlToxoplasmosis has an inverse relationship between transmission rate and severity: 1st-trimester transmission is rare but severe (classic triad), while 3rd-trimester transmission is common but subclinical at birth — the same principle that explains why rubella is severe in the 1st trimester (active organogenesis) but mild later. Spiramycin is used for primary maternal infection before fetal transmission is documented; once fetal infection is confirmed by amniotic PCR at ≥ 18 weeks, the regimen is typically switched to pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid (with the triple regimen usually deferred until after the 1st trimester due to teratogenicity risk).
NoteThe source notes that "Primary infection causes a life-long immunity and usually prevents re-infection." This is preserved verbatim in §3 lead. Clinically this means pre-pregnancy IgG seropositivity is protective — only primary infection in pregnancy is teratogenic. Reactivation in immunocompromised patients is rare but documented.

4Rubella

Pathogen

RNA virus — teratogenic

Rubella virus is one of the more teratogenic viruses. It is an RNA virus.

Mode / Incubation

Droplet infection · 2–3 weeks

Mode of infection: Droplet infection. Incubation period: 2–3 weeks.

Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS)

CRS is characterized by:

Growth

Intrauterine growth restriction

CNS

Intracranial calcifications, microcephaly

Eye

Cataracts

Cardiac

Cardiac defects (most commonly patent ductus arteriosus or pulmonary arterial hypoplasia)

Neuro

Neurologic disease (with a broad range of presentations, from behavior disorders to meningoencephalitis)

Bone / Liver

Osteitis, hepatosplenomegaly

Clinical picture

  • Maculopapular rash that persists for 3 days.
  • Generalized lymphadenopathy (especially postauricular and occipital), which may precede the rash.
  • Transient arthritis, malaise, and headache.

Diagnosis

Maternal

If there is suspicion of maternal exposure, both IgM and IgG should be done and repeated after 3 weeks.

  • IgM negative + IgG did not rise → no acute infection.
  • IgM negative + IgG level rise → infection may have occurred but reduced risk of congenital rubella.
  • IgM positive in any sample → acute rubella infection.

Fetal

By detecting viral RNA by PCR from chorionic villi, fetal blood, or amniotic sample.

Prevention

MMR vaccine should be offered to all women in childbearing age. It should not be given to pregnant women.

Treatment

  • Induction of abortion if infection occurs in the first trimester.
  • Immunoglobulin.
DangerFirst-trimester rubella infection has a ~ 85 % risk of congenital rubella syndrome. The combination of the source's "induction of abortion" recommendation with the severity of CRS (IUGR, microcephaly, cataracts, PDA, neuro disease) makes first-trimester primary rubella infection one of the few infections where termination of pregnancy is a medically and ethically legitimate discussion. The MMR vaccine remains the cornerstone of prevention — it is a live attenuated vaccine and is contraindicated in pregnancy (the source's "It should not be given to pregnant women").
PearlThe diagnostic algorithm in the source ("IgM negative + IgG did not rise → no acute infection", etc.) is a classic example of paired-serology interpretation — a single IgM is unreliable because of false positives and persistent IgM after vaccination. The gold standard is a ≥ 4-fold rise in IgG titer between acute and convalescent samples drawn 2–3 weeks apart. The source's 3-week repeat interval matches this standard.

5Cytomegalovirus (CMV)

The most common congenital viral infection

CMV is a double-stranded DNA herpes virus and represents the most common congenital viral infection.

Mode of infection

Contact with saliva, urine or other body fluids, transplacental transmission, and transmission via breast milk.

Clinical picture

Maternal

May be asymptomatic

May be asymptomatic. Symptoms include malaise, fever, generalized lymphadenopathy.

Fetal / Neonatal

Transplacental CMV consequences

Transplacental infection can result in:

  • Intrauterine growth restriction.
  • Sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Intracranial calcifications.
  • Microcephaly.
  • Hydrocephalus.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Delayed psychomotor development.
  • Optic atrophy.

Diagnosis

1. Maternal

Detection of CMV-IgM and IgG antibodies.

2. Fetal — US

Fetal ultrasound: microcephaly, ventriculomegaly, intracranial calcifications, oligohydramnios, and intrauterine growth restriction.

2. Fetal — PCR

Amniocentesis and cordocentesis for PCR DNA testing to diagnose intrauterine infection.

Management

  • Symptomatic treatment.
  • Passive immunization with CMV specific immunoglobulin.
DangerCongenital CMV is the leading non-genetic cause of sensorineural hearing loss in children. Even asymptomatic congenital CMV infection carries a 10–15 % risk of late-onset hearing loss and neurodevelopmental sequelae. The combination of high prevalence (1–2 % of all live births) and the absence of an effective vaccine makes CMV the single most important congenital infection in the developed world.
NoteThe source mentions "Passive immunization with CMV specific immunoglobulin." This reflects the CMV hyperimmune globulin literature (Nigro et al. 2005, Reveno et al. 2014) — a therapy that remains investigational and not standard of care in most guidelines. The visual preserves the source's recommendation verbatim, while this note flags the current evidence base for clinical readers.

6Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)

Pathogen & vertical transmission

It is a double stranded DNA virus. Neonatal herpes is acquired from passage through an infected birth canal. The risk of vertical transmission is:

  • 50 % for primary HSV infection in labor.
  • 0–4 % in women with recurrent disease.

Mode of infection

  • Direct contact with mucous membranes or skin infected with the virus, commonly through sexual contact.
  • Fetal infection with HSV can occur transplacentally, as an ascending infection from the cervix.

Clinical picture

  • Painful, vesicular lesions and ulcers.
  • Congenital infections are very rare and may produce disseminated or CNS disease.

Diagnosis & prevention

Diagnosis — Maternal

PCR for detection of IgM and IgG antibodies.

Prevention — C-section

Cesarean section delivery is recommended for all pregnancies complicated by primary genital HSV in labor.

Management

In pregnancy: Acyclovir. In labor: cesarean delivery.

DangerThe 50 % vs 0–4 % vertical transmission differential is the single most clinically important number in this section. It is the rationale for the source's "Cesarean section delivery is recommended for all pregnancies complicated by primary genital HSV in labor" — the dramatic risk difference between primary and recurrent HSV reflects the absence of protective maternal IgG in primary infection. In recurrent HSV, maternal IgG crosses the placenta and provides partial fetal protection, reducing vertical transmission to single-digit percentages.
PearlAcyclovir is safe in pregnancy — it is a category B drug with extensive safety data in pregnancy registries. Suppressive acyclovir therapy from 36 weeks gestation is commonly used in women with a history of recurrent genital HSV to reduce the risk of recurrence at delivery and the need for cesarean delivery. The visual preserves the source's "In pregnancy: Acyclovir" recommendation and pairs it with the "In labor: cesarean delivery" decision-making rule.
