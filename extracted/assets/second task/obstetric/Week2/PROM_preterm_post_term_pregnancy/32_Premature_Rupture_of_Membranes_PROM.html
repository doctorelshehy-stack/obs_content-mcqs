Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM)

A spontaneous rupture of the fetal membranes before the onset of labor — either term PROM (after 37 completed weeks) or preterm PROM / PPROM (before 37 completed weeks). This chapter covers the definition, the 5-item etiology (intrauterine infection, prior PPROM, trauma, amniocentesis, polyhydramnios), clinical presentation (sudden gush of clear or pale-yellow fluid), the 5-step diagnostic workup (speculum exam, nitrazine test, fern test, ultrasonography, fetal fibronectin), the 4 effects of preterm PROM, the gestational-age–stratified management (before 26 weeks: termination; 26–34 weeks: conservative with hospitalization + observation + antibiotics + corticosteroids + tocolysis for 48 h; after 34 weeks: delivery), and a complete chorioamnionitis sub-chapter (definition, 4-item etiology, fever > 38 °C + 7 signs diagnosis, definitive-treatment-with-antibiotics management).

2Definition (term vs preterm PROM)

📋 PROM — verbatim from source

"A spontaneous rupture of membranes before the onset of labor. It could be term after 37 completed weeks of gestation or preterm before 37 completed weeks."

Term PROM

Spontaneous rupture of membranes after 37 completed weeks of gestation (i.e., at term).

Preterm PROM (PPROM)

Spontaneous rupture of membranes before 37 completed weeks of gestation — the high-risk variant, associated with neonatal morbidity from prematurity.

🔑 KEY DEFINITION — memorize this

"PROM" = Premature Rupture of Membranes, in which the rupture occurs before the onset of labor. When the rupture occurs before 37 weeks it is called preterm PROM (PPROM); when it occurs after 37 weeks it is called term PROM. The phrase "before the onset of labor" is what distinguishes PROM from the normal rupture of membranes that occurs during labor.

3Etiology (5 risk factors)

Intrauterine infection(chorioamnionitis, subclinical deciduitis) — the most clinically important cause; infection weakens the chorioamniotic membranes and predisposes them to rupture.
Prior history of PPROMA previous preterm premature rupture of membranes is a major recurrent risk factor in subsequent pregnancies.
TraumaAbdominal trauma, motor-vehicle accidents, falls, or any event that mechanically stresses the membranes.
AmniocentesisIatrogenic rupture from invasive prenatal diagnostic procedures (amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling) can cause fluid leakage.
PolyhydramniosExcess amniotic fluid mechanically over-distends the uterus, increasing wall tension and membrane stress.

📝 MEMORY ANCHOR — 5 etiologies

INFECTION · PRIOR PPROM · TRAUMA · AMNIOCENTESIS · POLYHYDRAMNIOS — the classic 5-item etiology list. Intrauterine infection is the most important clinically because it is both a cause and a complication of PROM (chorioamnionitis, §11).

4Presentation (sudden gush of fluid)

💧 Typical clinical presentation (verbatim from source)

Patients present with a typical history of sudden gush of clear or pale yellow fluid leaking from vagina.

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — what to ask

A history of a sudden, single, large gush followed by continued leakage distinguishes PROM from urinary incontinence (continuous, no gush) or excessive vaginal discharge (gradual, no gush). The color — clear or pale yellow — is also characteristic: meconium-stained (green/brown) fluid suggests fetal distress, while foul-smelling fluid suggests chorioamnionitis (see §11).

5Diagnosis — 5 modalities

Diagnosis of PROM uses a stepwise approach: speculum examination is the first step, followed by the nitrazine test, fern test, ultrasonography, and (when the diagnosis remains uncertain) fetal fibronectin.

1. Speculum examination — the first step

🔍 Speculum examination

Speculum examination is the first step in the diagnosis of PROM — it is used to directly demonstrate leaking of fluid from the cervical os.

2. Nitrazine test — pH-based

🧪 Nitrazine test (pH principle)

  • The pH of amniotic fluid = 7 to 7.5 (alkaline).
  • The pH of vaginal discharge = 3.5 to 4.5 (acidic).
  • Procedure: yellow nitrazine paper is introduced in the vagina.
  • If it turns to blue → alkaline amniotic fluid is present → diagnosis of PROM confirmed.
  • If it remains yellow → amniotic fluid is not present.

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — Nitrazine color rule

Yellow → blue = +ve (amniotic fluid present). A ~3-log difference in pH (4 → 7) makes the test highly sensitive. False positives occur with blood, semen, bacterial vaginosis, and certain antiseptic solutions — all of which can also raise vaginal pH.

3. Fern test — arborization pattern

🌿 Fern test (crystallization pattern)

Fluid from the posterior vaginal fornix is swabbed on a glass slide and allowed to dry for 10 minutes. If on drying a fern pattern (arborization under microscopy) appears, then amniotic fluid is present (PROM).

📝 Note on the fern test

The fern pattern is created by the unique estrogen + electrolyte + protein composition of amniotic fluid. It is highly specific when a clean posterior-fornix sample is used. Blood and cervical mucus can sometimes produce atypical patterns — these are recognized as atypical ferning and should not be confused with the true amniotic-fluid pattern.

4. Ultrasonography

🖥️ Ultrasound finding

  • Oligohydramnios (low amniotic-fluid volume) — supports the diagnosis when history is suggestive but speculum is non-diagnostic.
  • Anhydramnios (absent amniotic fluid) — strongly suggests prolonged ROM with complete fluid loss.

5. Fetal fibronectin — biochemical confirmation

🧬 Fetal fibronectin protein

It is a glycoprotein present in large amounts in amniotic fluid. It can be detected in 39% of females with PROM by means of an ELISA test.

⚠️ WARN — sensitivity of fetal fibronectin for PROM

Fetal fibronectin is detected in only 39% of females with PROM — its sensitivity is limited. It is more useful as a rule-out test than a rule-in test. A negative fetal fibronectin result does not exclude PROM.

6Effects of preterm PROM (4 consequences)

Preterm laborThe most immediate and most common consequence — once the membranes rupture, uterine contractions typically begin within hours to days.
Pulmonary hypoplasiaDue to severe oligohydramnios — the fetus needs amniotic fluid for normal lung development; severe fluid loss impairs alveolar formation.
Skeletal & joint deformitiesDue to compression — without the cushioning amniotic fluid, the uterine wall compresses the fetus, leading to deformities such as clubfoot (Potter sequence).
ChorioamnionitisAscending infection through the ruptured membranes — see §11–§12 for full clinical picture and management.

🚨 DANGER — Pulmonary hypoplasia is irreversible

Pulmonary hypoplasia from prolonged severe oligohydramnios is not reversible after birth. This is why very early PPROM (< 24 weeks) carries a very high neonatal mortality — the lungs never develop adequately. This is also why before-26-weeks management (§8) leans toward termination: the fetal lung is too immature to develop outside, and severe oligohydramnios will prevent lung maturation in utero.

7Management — General (bed rest + sterile pads)

🛏️ Bed rest

Bed rest reduces the gravitational and mechanical stimulus on the cervix, may slow ongoing fluid loss, and facilitates close maternal and fetal monitoring.

🩹 Sterile pads for inspection

Sterile pads are used for inspection for meconium staining and signs of infection. Pads are changed regularly and inspected for: (a) meconium staining (greenish-brown — suggests fetal distress), (b) foul smell (suggests chorioamnionitis), (c) blood (suggests placental abruption or vasa previa).

8Management — Before 26 weeks (termination)

🚨 DANGER — Before 26 weeks

The risk of developing chorioamnionitis is very high, so termination of pregnancy is better.

Why termination before 26 weeks?

  • Very high chorioamnionitis risk — prolonged membrane rupture before 26 weeks almost always leads to ascending infection.
  • Pulmonary hypoplasia is likely — the lungs are not yet developed enough for survival outside the uterus, and severe oligohydramnios prevents further in-utero lung development.
  • Skeletal deformities are likely — compression-related deformities (Potter sequence) from prolonged severe oligohydramnios.
  • Fetal viability is below 24–26 weeks — most units cannot provide intensive care for a fetus delivered below this threshold.

9Management — 26–34 weeks (conservative, antibiotics, steroids, tocolysis)

🏥 Conservative treatment for 26–34 weeks' gestation (5 pillars)

Between 26 and 34 weeks, the goal is to delay delivery long enough for corticosteroids to mature the fetal lungs, while preventing/maternal-fetal infection. The 5 pillars are:

Pillar 1 — Hospitalization

🏥 Hospitalization

Admit the patient for close monitoring, bed rest, and rapid intervention if labor, infection, or fetal distress develops.

Pillar 2 — Maternal observation

👩 Maternal observation (vital signs, contractions, FBC, CRP)

Monitor: vital signs (for fever > 38 °C → chorioamnionitis, §11), uterine contractions (for preterm labor), full blood count (for leukocytosis), and C-reactive protein (for inflammation).

Pillar 3 — Fetal observation

👶 Fetal observation by cardiotocography (CTG)

Continuous or intermittent CTG monitoring to detect fetal tachycardia (> 160 bpm, a sign of chorioamnionitis) and fetal distress.

Pillar 4 — Antibiotics (IV → oral step-down)

💊 Antibiotic regimen — IV ampicillin + erythromycin, then oral step-down

IV phase (48 hours):

  • IV ampicillin 2 g
  • + Erythromycin 250 mg / 6 hours
  • Given together for the first 48 hours.

Oral step-down (next 5 days):

  • Oral amoxicillin 250 mg / 8 hours
  • + Erythromycin 250 mg / 6 hours
  • Given for 5 additional days.

Total antibiotic course: 7 days (2 days IV + 5 days oral).

Ampicillin 2 g IV + Erythromycin 250 mg/6h × 48h → Amoxicillin 250 mg/8h + Erythromycin 250 mg/6h × 5 d

Pillar 5 — Corticosteroids and tocolysis for 48 hours

💉 Corticosteroids and tocolysis for 48 hours

Corticosteroids (e.g., betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2, 24 h apart, or dexamethasone 6 mg IM × 4, 12 h apart) to accelerate fetal lung maturity, and tocolysis to delay delivery for the 48-hour window the corticosteroids need to act.

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — Why 48 hours?

Corticosteroids need ~48 hours to complete their effect on fetal lung surfactant production. Tocolysis is given to buy those 48 hours even when labor is actively established. After 48 hours, tocolysis is usually discontinued (its benefit beyond 48 h is unproven) but corticosteroids may be repeated only once, ≥ 14 days after the first course.

10Management — After 34 weeks (delivery)

🚼 After 34 weeks' gestation: delivery

After 34 weeks, the fetus is sufficiently mature that the risk of conservative management (chorioamnionitis, cord prolapse) outweighs the risk of prematurity. Delivery is indicated.

📝 Note on the 26-34 vs >34-week cutoff

The 34-week cutoff is the same as in the management of preterm labor (Chapter 31) and is anchored on the lung-maturation data for antenatal corticosteroids: by 34 weeks, most fetuses have produced enough endogenous surfactant that additional benefit from corticosteroids is minimal, and the risk of ongoing oligohydramnios + ascending infection becomes the dominant clinical concern.

11Chorioamnionitis — Definition, Etiology, Diagnosis

A. Definition

📋 Chorioamnionitis — verbatim from source

"Inflammation of fetal membranes which means intrauterine infection."

B. Etiology (4 risk factors)

Prolonged PROMThe single most important risk factor — ascending infection through the ruptured membranes.
Prolonged laborMultiple vaginal examinations during prolonged labor increase the risk of ascending infection.
Prenatal diagnostic proceduresAmniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling (CVS), and other invasive prenatal procedures can introduce pathogens.
Bacterial vaginosisDisruption of normal vaginal flora — most commonly Gardnerella vaginalis overgrowth — predisposes to ascending infection.

C. Diagnosis — fever > 38 °C + any of 7 signs

🚨 DANGER — Diagnostic rule

"Diagnosis: if there is fever > 38 °C with any of the following signs or investigations." The diagnosis is made when fever is present PLUS at least one of the 7 items below.

  1. Maternal tachycardia.
  2. Fetal tachycardia.
  3. Uterine tenderness.
  4. Foul smelling amniotic fluid.
  5. Maternal leukocytosis > 16,000/cc.
  6. Raised C-reactive protein > 2.5.
  7. High vaginal swab culture and stain positive.

⚠️ WARN — the > 2.5 CRP cutoff

The source's CRP cutoff of > 2.5 is presented verbatim. In modern clinical practice the upper limit varies by laboratory; many labs report normal CRP as < 5 mg/L and consider > 10 mg/L suspicious. The source's > 2.5 is preserved here as a relative elevation in the context of pregnancy (where baseline CRP may be slightly higher).

12Chorioamnionitis — Management (ampicillin + gentamicin ± clindamycin)

🚨 DANGER — Definitive treatment is delivery

"Definitive treatment is delivery and evacuation of the uterine contents." Antibiotics alone are not curative while the infected focus (the amniotic cavity) remains in place. Delivery removes the source of infection.

Mainstay of management — Antibiotics until delivery

💊 Antibiotic regimen — ampicillin + gentamicin (± clindamycin if CS)

Regimen until delivery:

  • Ampicillin 2 g IV every 6 hours.
  • + Gentamicin sulfate 2 mg/kg IV as a loading dose, then 1.5 mg/kg IV every 8 hours.
  • Continue until delivery.
  • If cesarean delivery is performed, clindamycin or metronidazole is added for anaerobic coverage.
Ampicillin 2 g IV q6h + Gentamicin 2 mg/kg IV load → 1.5 mg/kg IV q8h until delivery (± Clindamycin/Metronidazole if CS)

💎 CLINICAL PEARL — Why ± clindamycin only at cesarean?

Ampicillin + gentamicin already cover the most common ascending pathogens (Group B Strep, E. coli, other gram-negatives). Anaerobic coverage is added only at cesarean because the surgical incision breaches the skin and exposes deep pelvic tissues to anaerobic flora from the vagina. After a vaginal delivery, the same anaerobes have not been introduced to deep tissues, so additional anaerobic coverage is not required.
