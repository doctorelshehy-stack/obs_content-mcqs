1 ILOs of this chapter

  1. To understand the definition of macrosomia
  2. To be able to explain the risk factors of macrosomia
  3. To be able to describe the management and complications fetal macrosomia

2 Definition

Macrosomia: A fetal weight > 4,000 grams.

3 Risk factors

  • History of fetal macrosomia.
  • Maternal obesity.
  • Excessive weight gain during pregnancy.
  • Previous pregnancies: The risk increases with each pregnancy.
  • Male infants: typically weigh slightly more than female infants.
  • Maternal age: Women older than 35 are more likely to have a baby diagnosed with fetal macrosomia.

4 Causes

  1. Genetic factors.
  2. Maternal conditions such as diabetes can cause fetal macrosomia.
  3. Multiparity.
  4. Postterm pregnancy.

5 Clinical picture

  • Large fundal height.
  • Excessive amniotic fluid (polyhydramnios).

6 Complications

  • Prolonged and obstructed labor.
  • Shoulder dystocia.
  • Fetal injuries.
  • Meconium aspiration syndrome.
  • Genital tract lacerations.
  • Postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Uterine rupture.

7 Treatment

  1. Proper antenatal care: to prevent macrosomia and diagnose it before labor.
  2. Cesarean section: is safe for both mother and fetus.
  3. Postnatal: proper examination of the neonate and management of any problem.

8 Student activity · Questions · References

Student activity
Each student is requested to attend the weighing of a newborn in order to assign whether is macrosomic or not. In case of newborn diagnosed to be macrosomic, the etiology should be sought with the aid of the tutor of bedside part of the clinical round.
