1ILOs of this chapter

By the end of this chapter the student should be able to:

  • To understand the etiological pathogenesis of emesis with pregnancy
  • To be able to identify the clinical picture of hyperemesis gravidarum
  • To be able to describe the management of cases with hyperemesis gravidarum

2Causes of Vomiting in Pregnancy

Vomiting in pregnancy is not always a single disease — five etiological categories must be considered before attributing the symptom to pregnancy itself.

1 · Pregnancy-related (NVP)

Emesis gravidarum, hyperemesis gravidarum.

2 · Complications of Pregnancy

Severe pre-eclampsia, vesicular mole.

3 · Gynaecological Complications

Red degeneration of fibroid, torsion of ovarian cyst.

4 · Medical Causes

Food poisoning, gastritis, cholecystitis, pyelonephritis.

5 · Surgical Causes

Appendicitis, gall stones, peptic ulcer.

PearlAlways consider the non-pregnancy causes first in any patient with severe vomiting — missing appendicitis or pyelonephritis in pregnancy is a recognised cause of maternal mortality.

3Emesis Gravidarum vs. Hyperemesis Gravidarum

The same symptom on a spectrum — mild and self-limiting, or severe enough to threaten the mother's life.

Emesis Gravidarum

Nausea & vomiting on getting up in the morning, which starts early in pregnancy about the 12th week. It does not affect the general condition.

Hyperemesis Gravidarum (HG)

A condition of severe nausea and vomiting during pregnancy leading to fluid, electrolyte and acid-base imbalance, nutritional deficiency and weight loss.

PearlTime of onset is the bedside clue: emesis gravidarum classically begins around the 12th week, peaks, and resolves — while hyperemesis begins earlier, persists beyond the 1st trimester, and produces measurable metabolic derangement.

4Etiology and Pathogenesis

The cause is unknown but may be related to six overlapping mechanisms.

a

Hormonal factors

(NVP) are worse in pregnant women with conditions associated with elevated Human chorionic gonadotropins (hCG) levels (molar pregnancies, multiple gestations, and Down's syndrome). Progesterone decreases smooth muscle contractility and may alter gastric emptying and lead to increased nausea and vomiting. Thyroid dysfunction has also been studied as a possible mechanism for NVP and HG development due to cross reactivity between hCG and the thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH) receptor.

b

Immune system dysregulation

Immune mechanisms are implicated in NVP and HG development.

c

Gastrointestinal dysmotility

Alterations in lower esophageal sphincter (LES) resting pressure and esophageal peristalsis.

d

Psychosocial factors

Early studies proposed that NVP may be a psychosomatic illness.

e

Infection

About 90% of cases are infected with helicobacter pylori.

f

Allergic theory

The patient is allergic to corpus luteum of pregnancy, so some patients improve on antihistaminics and after the 1st trimester when the corpus luteum degenerates.

PearlThe allergic theory explains two otherwise puzzling clinical observations — that antihistamines help, and that symptoms often resolve in the 2nd trimester when the corpus luteum involutes and placental progesterone takes over.

5Prevention

  • Multivitamin with folic acid in early pregnancy.
  • Treating heartburn and acid reflux.

6Complications of Hyperemesis Gravidarum

  • Wernicke encephalopathy from deficiency of vitamin B1.
  • Bleeding diseases or embryopathy from vitamin K deficiency.
  • Malnutrition (immunosuppression, poor wound healing, muscle wasting).
  • Esophageal tears (Mallory–Weiss) and esophageal rupture.
  • Hepatic insufficiency, venous thrombosis, and acute tubular necrosis.
  • Placental dysfunction (preeclampsia, abruption, SGA).
DangerWernicke encephalopathy is the most feared complication of HG — it is preventable with thiamine before any glucose is given. Glucose without thiamine precipitates the encephalopathy by exhausting the last remaining thiamine stores in oxidative metabolism.

7Investigations

Laboratory

Kidney & liver functions, CBC, hematocrit & electrolytes & blood gases.

Urine

Ketonuria, oliguria, proteinuria, urine culture & sensitivity.

Fundus examination

Fundus examination of the eyes of the mother.

Ultrasonography

To exclude multiple pregnancy & vesicular mole.

WarnUltrasound is essential — molar pregnancy and multiple gestation are two of the most common correctable causes of severe HG, and the scan is the only reliable way to exclude them early.

8Management — Without Hypovolemia

Step-up approach for the ambulant, non-dehydrated patient: dietary adjustment → trigger avoidance → oral pharmacotherapy.

  1. Dietary changes (Section 9)
  2. Trigger avoidance (Section 10, part B)
  3. Pharmacotherapy (Section 10, part C → Section 11)

9Dietary Changes

  • Avoid empty or fully stomach.
  • Meals and snacks should be eaten slowly and in small amounts every one to two hours.
  • Eliminating coffee and spicy, odorous, high-fat, acidic, or very sweet foods, and instead consuming protein, salty, low-fat, bland, and/or dry food.
  • Avoid iron supplements which cause gastric irritation and can provoke nausea and vomiting.
  • Fluids: fluids at least 30 minutes before or after solid food to minimize the effect of a full stomach. Fluids are better tolerated if cold, clear, and carbonated.
PearlSuspending iron supplementation until vomiting settles is a simple change that often produces a dramatic improvement — iron tablets are among the most common avoidable triggers of NVP.

10Trigger Avoidance & Pharmacotherapy

B. Trigger avoidance

Avoidance of environmental triggers as odors, heat, humidity, noise or physical motion.

C. Pharmacotherapy

See the dose reference table in Section 11 for the complete regimen. The order below mirrors the source — start with the safest agent (pyridoxine) and step up to the more potent antiemetics as needed.

  1. Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) — first-line, antiemetic and safe in pregnancy.
  2. Antihistamine (H1 antagonists) — decreases stimulation of the vomiting center.
  3. Doxylamine — sedating antihistamine, often combined with pyridoxine.
  4. Diphenhydramine and Meclizine — for vestibular-component nausea.
  5. Dopamine antagonist — stimulate gastric motility and emptying.
  6. Metoclopramide — prokinetic + central antiemetic.
  7. Promethazine — phenothiazine, broad antiemetic.
  8. Serotonin antagonist — Ondansetron — for refractory cases.
  9. Acid-reducing agents — antacids, H2 blockers, proton pump inhibitors.

11Pharmacotherapy Dose Reference

Source-prescribed doses for the antiemetic regimen.

DrugDose & RouteNotes
Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) 10–25 mg orally every 6–8 h Max 100 mg/day.
Antihistamine (H1 antagonists) — Decreases stimulation of the vomiting center.
Doxylamine 10–40 mg orally Recommended dose.
Diphenhydramine & Meclizine 25–50 mg orally every 4–6 h Vestibular-component nausea.
Dopamine antagonist — Stimulates gastric motility and emptying.
Metoclopramide 5–10 mg orally, IV, or IM every 6–8 h Prokinetic + central antiemetic.
Promethazine 12.5–25 mg every four hours Phenothiazine class.
Serotonin antagonist — Ondansetron — Refractory cases.
Acid-reducing agents — Antacids, H2 blockers, proton pump inhibitors.
PearlThe combination pyridoxine (B6) + doxylamine is the only antiemetic combination formally classified as safe (Category A) in pregnancy — every other drug above is used when first-line therapy fails.

12Management — With Hypovolemia (Hyperemesis Gravidarum)

Diagnosis

Symptoms of hypovolemia

  • Lassitude.
  • Postural dizziness.
  • Thirst.

Signs

  • Manifestation of dehydration as loss of weight.
  • Dry skin and tongue.
  • Tachycardia.
  • Hypotension.
  • Oliguria.

Treatment

  1. Hospitalization.
  2. Replacement fluid therapy and nutrition: most patients respond to intravenous hydration (Saline and Ringer lactate) and a short period of gut rest, followed by reintroduction of oral intake and pharmacologic therapy.
  3. Thiamine supplementation (100 mg daily intramuscularly or intravenously) is given prior to administration of glucose to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy.
  4. Diet: after a short period of gut rest, begin with a diet consisting of bananas, rice, and then advance the diet as tolerated.
  5. Pharmacotherapy: non-oral routes of administration are necessary for patients with persistent vomiting. Antihistamine (H1 antagonists), dopamine antagonists, and serotonin antagonists.
    • Glucocorticoids for refractory cases: Methylprednisolone (16 mg) IV every 8 hours or hydrocortisone 100 mg IV twice daily.
  6. Tube feeding and parenteral nutrition.
DangerThiamine (100 mg IM/IV) MUST be given before glucose in any patient with persistent vomiting — administering glucose first risks precipitating Wernicke encephalopathy in a thiamine-depleted patient.

13Indications for Termination of Pregnancy

When the mother's life is at risk despite maximal supportive therapy, termination becomes the only remaining option.

Termination is indicated if any of the following develop:

  • Appearance of jaundice.
  • Proteinuria & progressive oliguria.
  • Creatinine rise.
  • Deterioration of the general condition despite treatment.
  • Encephalopathy.
  • Fundus changes.
DangerThese six signs indicate that conservative management is failing — the patient is developing end-organ damage (liver, kidney, brain, retina). Delaying termination beyond this point risks irreversible multi-organ failure.
Student Activity

14Student Activity

Each group of students is requested to pick up a case of vomiting with pregnancy among outpatient attendants of pregnant women at outpatient clinic then identify whether she has hyperemesis with pregnancy or not with the aid of outpatient specialist.

PearlBedside triage skill: a single morning episode of nausea without weight loss, dehydration, or ketonuria is emesis gravidarum — anything more sustained warrants the full HG workup (urinalysis, electrolytes, ultrasound to exclude mole/twins).
