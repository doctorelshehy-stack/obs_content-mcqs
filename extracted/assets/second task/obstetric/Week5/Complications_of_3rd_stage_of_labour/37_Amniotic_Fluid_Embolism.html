2 Definition

Amniotic fluid embolism (AFE): It is a condition in which, fetal fluid, tissue, or debris enters the maternal circulation via the placental bed and causes acute anaphylaxis.

Synonyms / clinical context

  • Also called "anaphylactoid syndrome of pregnancy" in modern literature (the term "embolism" is a misnomer — the reaction is anaphylactic rather than purely mechanical obstruction).
  • Rare (~1–2 per 40,000 deliveries in most series) but accounts for a disproportionate share of maternal mortality in developed countries (5–15% of maternal deaths).
  • Despite the name, it is NOT a true embolism in the classical sense — the dominant mechanism is anaphylaxis-like reaction to fetal antigens (similar to anaphylactic shock) combined with mechanical obstruction of pulmonary vessels by fetal debris.
Why "via the placental bed"? The placenta is a high-flow, low-resistance vascular interface between mother and fetus. Disruption of this barrier (e.g., by placental abruption, uterine trauma, or operative delivery) is the gateway for fetal material to enter the maternal venous circulation.

3 Conditions Allowing Amniotic Fluid to Enter the Maternal Circulation

Amniotic fluid may enter the maternal circulation in the following conditions (verbatim from source):

  1. Increased intrauterine pressure (accidental hemorrhage, oxytocin overdose with intact membranes).
  2. Opened uterine or endocervical veins (as in genital tract lacerations e.g. rupture uterus).
Two gateway mechanisms — mechanical summary:
  • ↑ Pressure gradient (abruption, oxytocin over-stimulation) — forces AF through intact barrier.
  • Open venous channels (lacerations, rupture uterus) — provides direct conduit for AF entry.

Potentiation by meconium

"The above factors also lead to fetal distress → meconium stained amniotic fluid → this potentiate the toxic nature of amniotic fluid and worsen the condition."

Meconium = potentiator, not initiator. Meconium-stained AF carries vasoactive substances (bile acids, free fatty acids) and particulate matter that amplify the anaphylactoid reaction. Fetal distress is therefore both a consequence of the entry conditions and a worsening factor once AF enters the circulation.

5 Risk Factors

The source lists the following risk factors (verbatim):

Risk factors (9 items, verbatim from source)

  1. Induced labor
  2. Advanced maternal age
  3. Multiparity
  4. Uterine rupture
  5. Abdominal trauma
  6. Placental abruption
  7. Diabetes
  8. Cervical lacerations
  9. Operative delivery

Pattern recognition — three clusters

Cluster A · Uterine over-distension / trauma

  • Uterine rupture
  • Placental abruption
  • Abdominal trauma
  • Operative delivery (CS, forceps, vacuum)
  • Cervical lacerations

Common theme: open venous channels or pressure surges that breach the maternal-fetal barrier.

Cluster B · Labor induction / uterine stimulation

  • Induced labor (oxytocin, prostaglandins)
  • Operative delivery (forceps, vacuum extraction)

Common theme: iatrogenic increases in intrauterine pressure — the same "↑ pressure" gateway described in §3.

Cluster C · Maternal factors

  • Advanced maternal age
  • Multiparity
  • Diabetes

Common theme: pre-existing maternal vulnerability that may predispose to a more severe anaphylactoid response.

Source caveat: AFE can occur in any pregnancy — most cases occur in women with NO identifiable risk factor. The list above describes statistical associations, not deterministic causes. A "low-risk" patient is not protected.

6 Clinical Picture

Source: "Immediately or shortly after difficult delivery:"

A. Respiratory distress (RDS)

  • Severe dyspnea — sudden onset, often the first symptom
  • Cyanosis — refractory to oxygen in severe cases
  • Tachypnea, accessory muscle use
  • Pulmonary crackles (when pulmonary edema develops)

B. Circulatory collapse

  • Extensive pulmonary vascular obstruction due to the amniotic fluid particulate matter
  • → Acute core pulmonale (right heart failure)
  • → Abrupt hypoxia (V/Q mismatch, ↓ cardiac output)
  • → Congestive heart failure (CHF)
  • → Hypotension, shock, cardiopulmonary arrest

C. Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

  • Triggered by tissue factor released from amniotic fluid
  • Bleeding from genital tract AND all other sites of trauma (IV sites, surgical wounds, etc.)
  • Consumptive coagulopathy — low fibrinogen, low platelets, prolonged PT/PTT, ↑ D-dimer

D. Final outcomes

  • Deep coma (from prolonged hypoxia)
  • Immediate death in more than 50% of cases
  • Survivors may have permanent hypoxic brain injury
Diagnostic suspicion rule (verbatim from source): "It is suspected in any case of sudden postpartum collapse and DIC." — AFE is a diagnosis of exclusion with no confirmatory test in real time. Maintain a low threshold when collapse + DIC occur together, especially peripartum.

E. The classic AFE triad

Hypoxia / respiratory distress Cardiovascular collapse / shock DIC / consumptive coagulopathy
Sudden dyspnea · cyanosis · pulmonary edema Hypotension · core pulmonale · arrest Bleeding from all sites · low fibrinogen
Mnemonic: "H-C-D" — Hypoxia, Collapse, DIC. Any 2 of 3 within minutes of delivery should trigger AFE protocol.

7 Investigations

Source investigations list (verbatim):

  1. Arterial blood gas, serum electrolytes, calcium and magnesium levels, coagulation profile, and complete blood count.
  2. CT, MRI on the lung.
  3. Autopsy: by finding AF debris (fetal squamous cells, lanugo hair, vernix) in pulmonary vessels.

Bedside / immediate

Test Expected finding in AFE Clinical purpose
Arterial blood gas (ABG) Severe hypoxemia (PaO₂ << 60), hyperventilation, metabolic acidosis from shock Quantify oxygenation defect · guide ventilator settings
Serum electrolytes Often deranged from shock / tissue hypoperfusion Baseline + monitor
Calcium & magnesium ↓ Calcium (binds to free fatty acids in AF) Hypocalcemia worsens myocardial depression — correct aggressively
Coagulation profile (PT, PTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer) ↑↑ PT/PTT · ↓↓ fibrinogen · ↑↑ D-dimer Confirms DIC; guides blood-product replacement
CBC ↓ Platelets · ↓ Hb (if bleeding) Tracks consumption and hemorrhage

Imaging

  • CT chest: may show diffuse pulmonary infiltrates, pulmonary edema, or filling defects in pulmonary arteries
  • MRI chest: rarely feasible in acute setting, but may show similar findings

Definitive / post-mortem

Autopsy finding (definitive for AFE)

Identification of amniotic fluid debris in pulmonary vessels:

  • Fetal squamous cells (shed skin)
  • Lanugo hair (fine fetal hair)
  • Vernix (waxy fetal coating)

This is the only histopathologically confirmatory test. In survivors, the diagnosis remains presumptive (clinical + lab + imaging).

No test confirms AFE in real time. The clinical triad (hypoxia + collapse + DIC) within minutes of a difficult delivery, in the absence of another clear cause, is the working definition used to start AFE resuscitation.

8 Management · Student Activity · Questions · References

A. Management (verbatim from source p. 204)

"In ICU: Cardiopulmonary support. Emergent delivery is required for both fetal and maternal benefits. Management of DIC. Corticosteroids. Heparin. Monitoring of different organs."

Management measures (verbatim, 6 items)

  1. Cardiopulmonary support (the cornerstone of AFE resuscitation)
  2. Emergent delivery — required for both fetal and maternal benefits
  3. Management of DIC — blood products (FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate)
  4. Corticosteroids — for the anaphylactoid / inflammatory component
  5. Heparin — controversial; may be used in the early hypercoagulable phase of DIC
  6. Monitoring of different organs — renal, hepatic, cerebral, respiratory

C. Practical ICU resuscitation sequence

  1. Call for help — multidisciplinary (obstetric, anesthesia, ICU, hematology, neonatology).
  2. Airway + Breathing — 100 % O₂, intubate early, lung-protective ventilation.
  3. Circulation — large-bore IV ×2, fluid resuscitate, inotropes/vasopressors.
  4. CPR if arrest — left uterine displacement (if pregnant), consider perimortem cesarean within 4–5 min (see Topic 36 §8 for the 5-minute rule).
  5. Empirical AFE bundle — corticosteroids, calcium, blood products.
  6. Lab + imaging — ABG, coags, fibrinogen, CBC, electrolytes, CT chest.
  7. Multi-organ monitoring — urine output, LFTs, neuro status, ventilator parameters.
Corticosteroids in AFE: given for the anaphylactoid / systemic inflammatory component. The benefit is empirical, not from randomized trials (AFE is too rare for RCTs).
Heparin in AFE-DIC: controversial. The source lists it, but modern practice usually reserves heparin for the early hypercoagulable phase of DIC, not the active bleeding phase. Use clinical judgment.
