Topic 34 · Chapter 34 · Obstructed Labor

Obstructed Labor

Mechanical failure of delivery — the fetus cannot traverse the birth canal despite good (adequate) uterine contractions, because of a passage problem (maternal bony pelvis or soft tissue) or a passenger problem (fetal size, lie, presentation, position, or malformation). Obstructed labor is one of the most serious obstetric emergencies, and a leading cause of maternal and perinatal mortality in low-resource settings. The condition is almost entirely preventable through proper antenatal care, early detection of risk factors, and strict use of the partogram; once established, delivery is by cesarean section — vaginal delivery is contraindicated.

2Definition · The 3 P's revisited

📘 KEY DEFINITION · Obstructed Labor

Failure of delivery of fetus in spite of good uterine contractions due to mechanical obstruction.

💡 CLINICAL PEARL · Why "obstructed" ≠ "prolonged" labor

The keyword in the definition is "in spite of good uterine contractions". Prolonged labor may be caused by inefficient contractions (uterine inertia — see Chapter 33); obstructed labor is caused by a mechanical block despite contractions that are doing their job. The distinction matters because the management is fundamentally different: in obstructed labor, more oxytocin will not help — and may rupture the uterus. The only definitive treatment is cesarean section.

📝 NOTE · The 3 P's of dystocia (recall from Chapter 33)

Whenever labor is not progressing, systematically evaluate the 3 P's: Power (contractions), Passages (maternal pelvis + soft tissues), and Passenger (fetus). Obstructed labor is a Passages and/or Passenger problem with a normal Power — that combination is the diagnostic signature.

3Causes · Maternal (Passages) + Fetal (Passenger)

The obstruction is always mechanical. Causes are split into two groups — passages (maternal) and passenger (fetal) — reflecting the two halves of the fetopelvic relationship.

Maternal causes (the passages)

  • Contracted pelvis
  • Abnormal pelvis: android or anthropoid
  • Pelvic tumor: fibroid, ovarian tumor
  • Tumors of the rectum, bladder or pelvic bone
  • Stenosis in cervix
  • Vaginal septum
  • Constricting ring in uterus

Fetal causes (the passenger)

  • Macrosomic baby
  • Malpresentation
  • Malposition
  • Malformed fetus (hydrocephalos, conjoint twins, fetal ascitis)
  • Locked twins

⚠️ WARN · Fetal malformation as an obstruction cause

Three specific fetal malformations are singled out as causes: hydrocephalos (enlarged head cannot engage), conjoint twins (interlocking fetal parts), and fetal ascitis (distended abdomen). The source spelling "hydrocephalos" (Greek genitive-style) is preserved verbatim — the more familiar English form is hydrocephalus. All three are diagnosable on obstetric ultrasound during antenatal care, which is why early detection can prevent obstructed labor.

📝 MEMORY ANCHOR · Source spelling preserved

The source uses "hydrocephalos", "ascitis" (not "ascites"), and "conjoint twins" (one word, not "conjoined"). All three forms are preserved verbatim in the lists above. The terms are clinically well-understood despite the variant spellings; what matters is the underlying pathology — an enlarged or distorted fetal part that cannot pass the pelvic inlet or outlet.

4Diagnosis · General + Abdominal + Vaginal examination

The diagnosis is a clinical diagnosis — based on a triad of examination findings: (1) prolonged labor with maternal distress on general inspection, (2) characteristic abdominal findings, and (3) characteristic vaginal findings. No single sign is diagnostic; the combination is.

A · General examination

  • The labor has been prolonged (lasting more than 12 hours).
  • The mother appears exhausted, anxious and weak.
  • Features of maternal distress (dehydration, exhaustion and ketoacidosis, tachypnea, scanty urine and raised temperature).

B · Abdominal examination

Abdominal findings

  • Uterus: hard and tender.
  • Pathological retraction ring (Bandl's ring) — see Chapter 33 §10.
  • Distended bladder.
  • Fetal heart sound: fetal distress or may be absent.

C · Vaginal examination

Vaginal findings

  • Vulva: is edematous.
  • Vagina: is dry and hot.
  • Cervix: is fully or partially dilated, edematous.
  • The membranes: are ruptured.
  • The presenting part: is high and not engaged or impacted in the pelvis. There is large caput and moulding.
  • The cause of obstruction can be detected.

💡 CLINICAL PEARL · The 12-hour partogram rule

The source's first diagnostic criterion is "labor lasting more than 12 hours". This is a deliberate operationalization of the WHO partogram's action line: once a woman's labor curve crosses the alert line, action is expected within 4 hours (or sooner); if no progress is made and labor has gone beyond 12 hours total, obstructed labor should be the working diagnosis until proven otherwise. Strict partogram use is therefore the single most effective prevention tool — and the source cites this explicitly in §5.

🚨 DANGER · "Large caput and moulding" — the cardinal vaginal sign

The combination of large caput succedaneum (edematous swelling of the presenting scalp from prolonged pressure against the pelvic bones) and moulding (over-riding of the fetal skull bones) is the cardinal vaginal sign of obstructed labor. It tells you two things simultaneously: (1) the head has been trying to descend for a long time, and (2) the pelvis is too small to let it through. Continuing to push in this scenario risks uterine rupture — the decision must shift to cesarean section.

5Management · Prevention + General + Obstetrics

Management has three pillars — Prevention (antenatal), General management (stabilize the mother), and Obstetric management (deliver the baby). All three are required; none is optional.

A · Prevention

Prevention · The most effective pillar

  1. Proper assessment of pregnant women with regular ANC visit — every pregnancy should be assessed antenatally for risk factors (contracted pelvis, malpresentation, macrosomia, previous obstructed labor).
  2. Early detection of the maternal or fetal causes which may precipitate to obstructed labor — clinical pelvimetry, obstetric ultrasound for fetal weight/lie/presentation, and review of obstetric history all contribute.
  3. Partogram should be strictly followed — the partogram is the single most effective bedside tool for early detection of prolonged labor.

B · General management

General management · Stabilize the mother

  1. Assessment of the general condition of the mother — vitals, level of consciousness, hydration, urine output.
  2. Correction of dehydration with IV fluid — the prolonged, exhausted, ketotic mother is hypovolemic; aggressive rehydration is the first resuscitation step.
  3. Broad spectrum antibiotics — the membranes are ruptured, vaginal examinations have been done, and there is real risk of sepsis / chorioamnionitis.
  4. Catheterization — the bladder is often distended (visible on abdominal exam as a suprapubic bulge) and catheterization both relieves the obstruction to the presenting part and allows accurate urine-output monitoring.
  5. Correction of acidosis — the combination of dehydration, ketoacidosis (from prolonged labor with inadequate intake), and lactic acidosis (from tissue hypoxia) must be corrected before surgery.

C · Obstetric management

Obstetric management · Deliver the baby

  1. Cesarean section with active management of third stage of labor for fear of postpartum hemorrhage — CS is the only acceptable mode of delivery in established obstructed labor. Vaginal delivery (instrumental or not) is contraindicated — it would risk uterine rupture and fetal trauma.
  2. Exploration of the birth canal — after CS, the entire birth canal (cervix, vagina, vulva, bladder) must be carefully examined for pressure-induced injuries: cervical tears, vaginal lacerations, vesicovaginal fistula. A missed bladder injury can present days later as a fistula.

🚨 DANGER · Active management of third stage is non-negotiable

After CS for obstructed labor, the uterus is at extreme risk of atony + postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) — it has been working against a mechanical obstruction for hours, and the lower segment is thinned and bruised. Active management of the third stage of labor (AMTSL) — prophylactic uterotonic (oxytocin 10 IU IV/IM, or carboprost in atony), controlled cord traction, and uterine massage — is therefore mandatory, not optional. The source uses the phrase "for fear of postpartum hemorrhage" to emphasize that this is the dominant concern in the third stage.

⚠️ WARN · Why instrumental delivery is contraindicated

In obstructed labor, the presenting part is either high and unengaged or impacted in the pelvis — neither is a safe situation for forceps or ventouse. Attempted instrumental delivery in this setting risks: fetal intracranial hemorrhage, shoulder dystocia, maternal soft-tissue injuries (cervical, vaginal, perineal), and uterine rupture if a fetal part is forcibly pulled through. Cesarean section is the only safe route.

6Complications · Maternal + Fetal

Obstructed labor is a dual-injury condition: both mother and fetus are at risk. The maternal complications are dominated by hemorrhage, rupture, and infection; the fetal complications are dominated by asphyxia and trauma.

🤰 Maternal complications

  • Postpartum hemorrhage — atonic uterus after prolonged obstructed labor (see §5 DANGER above).
  • Rupture uterus — the thinned lower segment ruptures if obstruction is not relieved; surgical emergency with high mortality.
  • Sepsis — prolonged rupture of membranes + multiple vaginal examinations + devitalized tissue = puerperal sepsis / septic shock.
  • Fistula (vesicovaginal) — pressure necrosis of the bladder base against the fetal head → ischemic sloughing → VVF; presents days to weeks postpartum with continuous urinary leakage.

👶 Fetal complications

  • Neonatal sepsis — ascending infection from the birth canal after prolonged ROM.
  • Convulsions (fits) — from hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy secondary to prolonged fetal distress.
  • Severe asphyxia — the fetal head is compressed against the bony pelvis for hours; placental perfusion is reduced; fetal pH falls.
  • Death — stillbirth or early neonatal death; obstructed labor is a leading cause of perinatal mortality in low-resource settings.

📝 MEMORY ANCHOR · The "HARD STOP" mnemonic for maternal complications

Memorize the four maternal complications in source order: Postpartum hemorrhage → Rupture uterus → Sepsis → Fistula (vesicovaginal). The pattern is immediate (PPH) → acute (rupture) → subacute (sepsis) → delayed (fistula) — a useful way to remember the timeline of these injuries. The fetal mnemonic in source order: Neonatal sepsis → Convulsions → Severe asphyxia → Death (NCSD).

💡 CLINICAL PEARL · Vesicovaginal fistula — the social devastation

Of all four maternal complications, vesicovaginal fistula (VVF) is the one with the most lasting social impact: a young woman is left with continuous urinary incontinence for the rest of her life (unless surgically repaired). The mechanism is pressure necrosis — the fetal head compresses the bladder base against the pubic symphysis, cutting off blood supply; the ischemic tissue sloughs 5–7 days postpartum, leaving a fistula. It is almost entirely preventable by timely CS, which is the strongest argument for the prevention pillar in §5.

7Student activity · Questions · References

📚 Student activity

The students with their tutor are requested to check the departmental emergency records in order to find admitted (referred cases of obstructed labor) then revise the picked records concerning the presentation and management of each case.

This is a case-based learning exercise: the goal is to find real records of obstructed labor (not textbook cases) and reconstruct — from the documentation — what the presentation looked like, what was done, and what the outcome was. Focus on: (a) the clinical signs that were documented (prolonged labor, caput, moulding, maternal distress, fetal distress); (b) the management sequence (general resuscitation → cesarean section → active third stage → birth-canal exploration); and (c) the maternal + fetal complications (look for PPH, sepsis, VVF, asphyxia, neonatal death).
