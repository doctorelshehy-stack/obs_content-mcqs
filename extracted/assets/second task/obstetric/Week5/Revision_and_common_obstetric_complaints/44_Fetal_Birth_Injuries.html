2 Causes of birth trauma

Birth trauma: Marked compression or sudden compression-decompression during delivery.

Trauma can arise from three classic mechanisms plus iatrogenic causes:

The 3 P’s

Passages
Contracted pelvis, soft tissue obstruction.
Passenger
Fetal macrosomia, malpresentation, malposition.
Power
Precipitate labor.

Practice (Iatrogenic)

  • Instrumental delivery: ventouse, forceps.
  • Intrauterine manipulation: internal podalic version, breech extraction.

3 Types — Fractures

Fractures usually result from traumatic deliveries as in shoulder dystocia and difficult instrumental deliveries.

Hierarchy of frequency

  1. Clavicle (most frequent).
  2. Humerus.
  3. Femur.
  4. Skull bones.

By bone type

Clavicular fractures
Best treated conservatively. Mild analgesia may be helpful.
Long bone fractures
May require simple splinting to immobilise the limb and thus reduce pain.

Skull fractures

More serious: The possibility of underlying hemorrhage must be considered.
Outlook: The majority of neonatal fractures will heal with conservative treatment.

4 Types — Cephalohematomas

These result from bleeding between the periosteum and skull bones, and take the shape of the underlying skull bone.

Rule out associated injury: Skull fracture or intracranial bleeding should always be ruled out.
Prognosis: Most cephalohematomas are benign and resolve without problems.

Cephalohaematoma vs Caput Succedaneum — Comparison

Feature Cephalohaematoma Caput Succedaneum
Onset Develops hours or days after birth. Present at birth.
Nature Localized hematoma to one bone, limited by sutures at its edges. Diffuse tissue oedema overlying more than one bone.
Edges Well-defined edges. Ill-defined edges.
Consistency Elastic (does not pit on pressure). Soft (pits on pressure).
Resolution Disappears within few weeks. Disappears within 1-2 days.

5 Nerve palsies

Erb’s palsy

  • Most common nerve palsy.
  • Due to damage to the brachial plexus (C5, C6 and C7).
  • Commonly associated with traction on the neck and shoulders during difficult deliveries.
  • Result: flaccid arm held in a pronated and internally rotated position.
Early management
Physiotherapy can help.
Advanced options
Surgical treatment techniques to repair damaged nerves are now available.

Facial palsy

  • Commonly caused by obstetric maneuvers, such as the use of forceps causing pressure damage.
  • Most are probably the result of external pressure causing a lower motor neuron injury.
  • Prognosis is excellent.
Upper motor neuron lesion: If suspected, the infant should be investigated for possible cerebral injury or congenital disorders.
Treatment: No specific treatment is required for facial palsy.

6 Sternomastoid tumors

  • Result from bleeding into the sternomastoid muscle.
  • Not normally recognized at birth.
  • Do not become obvious until a few weeks of age.
Management
Physiotherapy is required to prevent contracture of the muscle.
Prognosis: Good.

7 Lacerations

  • The infant may suffer skin lacerations during delivery, usually during Caesarean section.
  • They are usually superficial and heal without problems.
  • Suturing may be needed for deeper wounds.
