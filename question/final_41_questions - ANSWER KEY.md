### Q1 — Preterm Labour (1+1+3 = 5 marks)

**Definition (1 mark):**
Preterm labour is defined as the presence of regular uterine contractions (≥4 in 20 min or ≥8 in 60 min) that produce progressive cervical effacement and dilatation between 20+0 and 36+6 weeks of gestation (after the age of viability and before 37 completed weeks).

**2 Causes / Risk Factors (1 mark):**
1. Previous preterm birth (strongest predictor)
2. Multiple pregnancy (twins/triplets → uterine overdistension)
3. Cervical insufficiency / short cervix
4. Intrauterine infection / chorioamnionitis
5. Premature rupture of membranes (PPROM)
6. Uterine anomalies (septate, bicornuate uterus)
7. Smoking, alcohol, or drug abuse
8. Maternal stress / low BMI

**Lines of Treatment (3 marks):**
1. **Confirmation & assessment** — Monitor contractions, sterile speculum exam, digital cervical exam / transvaginal ultrasound cervical length, fetal fibronectin test if available.
2. **Corticosteroids** — Betamethasone 12 mg IM × 2 doses, 24 h apart (or dexamethasone 6 mg IM × 4 doses q12h) to accelerate fetal lung maturity.
3. **Tocolysis** (short-term, to delay delivery for steroid effect and in-utero transfer):
   - Calcium channel blockers (Nifedipine 10–20 mg PO q4–6h)
   - Oxytocin receptor antagonist (Atosiban)
   - NSAIDs (Indomethacin) if <32 weeks
   - Beta-agonists (Terbutaline) — second line
4. **Magnesium sulfate** — For fetal neuroprotection (reduces risk of cerebral palsy), used if delivery imminent <32–34 weeks.
5. **Antibiotics** — If PPROM or suspected infection; GBS prophylaxis (Penicillin G or Ampicillin).
6. **Fetal monitoring** — Continuous CTG monitoring, biophysical profile.
7. **Antenatal surveillance** — Serial ultrasound for growth, amniotic fluid volume, umbilical artery Doppler.
8. **Delivery planning** — Mode of delivery based on presentation, gestational age, and fetal status. Consider maternal transfer to a unit with NICU facilities.

### Q2 — Internal Podalic Version (1+1+3 = 5 marks)

**Definition (1 mark):**
Internal podalic version is an obstetric manoeuvre in which the operator introduces a hand into the uterine cavity, grasps one or both fetal feet, and brings them down through the cervix, converting the fetal presentation to a breech, followed by breech extraction.

**Indications (1 mark):**
1. Transverse lie of the **second twin** after delivery of the first twin (most common current indication)
2. Transverse lie with cord prolapse and a live fetus (in multipara with fully dilated cervix)
3. Transverse lie in a multiparous woman at full dilatation (rare in modern practice — CS is preferred)
4. Emergency delivery of a second twin with malpresentation

**Precautions (1.5 marks):**
1. Cervix must be **fully dilated** (complete)
2. Adequate **uterine relaxation** (inhalational anaesthesia / tocolytic)
3. **Membranes intact** until just before the manoeuvre (preferably — to maintain lie and prevent constriction ring)
4. **Fetus alive** and of reasonable size (not severely preterm or macrosomic)
5. **No cephalopelvic disproportion**
6. **Skill and experience** of the operator
7. Ultrasound to confirm fetal lie, position, and number of fetuses
8. Empty the bladder
9. Continuous fetal monitoring (if time permits)
10. Ready for C-section if manoeuvre fails

**Technique (1.5 marks):**
1. Place the patient in lithotomy position, empty bladder, and maintain strict asepsis.
2. Administer anaesthesia (general or epidural) and/or tocolytic for uterine relaxation.
3. If membranes are intact, rupture them just before the manoeuvre.
4. Identify fetal presentation and back by ultrasound ± internal palpation.
5. Pass the whole hand into the uterus, identify the fetal back, and follow it down to the breech to grasp one or both feet (grasp the lower foot if possible).
6. Bring the foot/feet down through the cervix steadily and gently.
7. Allow spontaneous delivery of the trunk until the umbilicus, then perform **breech extraction**.
8. Deliver the after-coming head using Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit manoeuvre or forceps.

### Q3 — Habitual Abortion (1+1+1+2 = 5 marks)

**Definition (1 mark):**
Habitual (recurrent) abortion is defined as the occurrence of **three or more consecutive spontaneous pregnancy losses** before 20–24 weeks of gestation (the age of viability).

**2 Causes (1 mark):**
1. **Chromosomal abnormalities** — Parental balanced translocation (most common cause in first trimester)
2. **Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS)** — Lupus anticoagulant / anticardiolipin antibodies → thrombotic pregnancy loss
3. **Uterine anomalies** — Septate uterus, bicornuate uterus, intrauterine adhesions (Asherman's), fibroids
4. **Cervical insufficiency** — Painless cervical dilatation in the second trimester
5. **Hormonal disorders** — Luteal phase defect, thyroid disease (hypothyroidism), diabetes mellitus
6. **Thrombophilias** — Factor V Leiden, prothrombin mutation, protein C/S deficiency
7. **Infections** (rare as cause of recurrent loss) — Toxoplasma, CMV, etc.

**2 Investigations (1 mark):**
1. **Parental karyotyping** (peripheral blood) — to detect balanced translocations
2. **Antiphospholipid antibody panel** — Lupus anticoagulant (LA), anticardiolipin antibodies (aCL), anti-β2 glycoprotein I
3. **Hysterosalpingography (HSG) or hysteroscopy** — to evaluate uterine cavity anatomy
4. **Thyroid function tests** (TSH, FT4) and anti-thyroid antibodies
5. **Transvaginal ultrasound** — cervical length measurement, uterine anomaly screening
6. **Progesterone level** (mid-luteal phase)
7. **Thrombophilia screen**
8. **Endometrial biopsy** — for luteal phase defect / chronic endometritis

**Lines of Treatment (2 marks):**
1. **Genetic counselling** — If balanced translocation found; consider IVF-PGT.
2. **Antiphospholipid syndrome** — Low-dose aspirin (75–100 mg/day) + prophylactic LMWH (Enoxaparin) throughout pregnancy and 6 weeks postpartum.
3. **Cervical insufficiency** — Cervical cerclage (McDonald or Shirodkar) at 12–14 weeks.
4. **Uterine anomalies** — Hysteroscopic septoplasty (septate uterus), adhesiolysis (Asherman's).
5. **Hormonal support** — Progesterone supplementation (vaginal or IM) from early pregnancy until 34–36 weeks for luteal phase defect.
6. **Thyroid disorder** — Levothyroxine to maintain TSH <2.5 mIU/L.
7. **Lifestyle measures** — Avoid smoking, alcohol, stress; folic acid 400–500 mcg/day preconception; optimal weight.
8. **Empirical treatment** — If no cause found, consider supportive antenatal care, progesterone, and close monitoring.

### Q4 — Hydatidiform Mole (Complete Mole) — 5 marks

**Diagnosis:** Complete hydatidiform mole (snow-storm appearance on TVUS, markedly elevated β-hCG at 121,000 mIU/mL at 10 weeks).

**1. Incidence (1 mark):**
- In Western countries: 1 in 600–1000 pregnancies.
- In Asia / developing countries: 1 in 200–500 pregnancies (higher incidence).
- Complete mole is more common than partial mole.

**2. 2 Risk Factors (1 mark):**
1. **Advanced maternal age** (>35–40 years) — risk increases 2–10×
2. **Previous hydatidiform mole** — risk of recurrence ~1% after one mole, ~15–28% after two
3. **Extremes of reproductive age** (<20 years or >40 years)
4. **Low socioeconomic status** / dietary deficiencies (low carotene, vitamin A, folic acid)
5. **Nulliparity or previous spontaneous abortion**

**3. Treatment (2 marks):**
1. **Suction evacuation & curettage** (S&E) — Treatment of choice for molar pregnancy, regardless of uterine size. Perform under ultrasound guidance. Use oxytocin infusion after evacuation to reduce bleeding.
2. **Hysterectomy** — For patients ≥40 years who do not desire future fertility (reduces risk of malignant transformation but does NOT eliminate risk of metastases).
3. **Prophylactic chemotherapy** (Methotrexate or Actinomycin-D) — Not routinely used; may be considered for high-risk patients (β-hCG >100,000, large uterus, theca-lutein cysts >6 cm, age >40) in some protocols.
4. **Management of complications** — Immediate: haemorrhage, uterine perforation; Late: persistent GTN (gestational trophoblastic neoplasia).
5. **Rhogam** — If the patient is Rh-negative.

**4. Post-treatment Follow-up (1 mark):**
1. **Serial β-hCG measurement**:
   - Weekly until undetectable (normal, <5 mIU/mL) for **3 consecutive weeks**.
   - Then monthly for **6–12 months** (some guidelines recommend 6 months, others 12 months).
2. **Contraception**:
   - Advise reliable contraception (OCPs or barrier) for **at least 6–12 months** after hCG returns to normal.
   - Avoid pregnancy during the entire follow-up period.
3. **Chest X-ray** at baseline and as indicated (to rule out pulmonary metastases).
4. **Monitoring for malignant transformation**:
   - Rising or plateauing hCG → suspect GTN → staging and chemotherapy.
5. **Ultrasound** — Only if clinically indicated (persistent bleeding, etc.).
6. **Total follow-up** — HCG monitoring for 6 months (if regression was prompt and spontaneous) up to 12 months.

### Q5 — Decreased Fetal Movement / Intrauterine Fetal Death (IUFD) — 5 marks

**LMP:** 6 October 2024 (DD/MM/YYYY format assumed).
**Condition:** Decreased fetal movement → Suspect intrauterine fetal compromise / IUFD (stillbirth / intrauterine fetal death).

**1. Gestational Age According to LMP (1 mark):**
- **Naegele's rule:** LMP + 280 days (40 weeks) = LMP + 9 months + 7 days.
- **EDD = 13 July 2025.**
- At presentation (presumably near/at term), the gestational age would be approximately 38–40 weeks depending on the exact date. *(If the exam presumes the patient presents shortly after decreased movement is noted, GA at presentation is term.)*

**2. 2 Causes of Decreased Fetal Movement / IUFD (1 mark):**
1. **Placental insufficiency** — Pre-eclampsia, IUGR, abruption, post-term pregnancy
2. **Cord accidents** — True knot, nuchal cord, cord prolapse, cord thrombosis
3. **Maternal medical disorders** — Diabetes mellitus (poorly controlled), hypertensive disorders, SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome
4. **Fetal causes** — Severe IUGR, congenital anomalies, fetal infection (TORCH), feto-maternal haemorrhage
5. **Infection** — Chorioamnionitis, maternal sepsis
6. **Maternal trauma**
7. **Unknown/idiopathic** (~25–30% of IUFD)

**3. How to Diagnose (2 marks):**
1. **Clinical assessment** — Ask about fetal movement count (<10 kicks in 2 hours is abnormal). Listen for fetal heart sounds with Doppler/stethoscope.
2. **Ultrasound**:
   - Confirm **absence of cardiac activity** (real-time B-mode + M-mode) — definitive diagnosis of IUFD.
   - Assess amniotic fluid volume, placental appearance, signs of abruption.
3. **CTG (cardiotocography)** — Non-reactive tracing with absent FHR variability if fetus alive but compromised; flatline tracing in IUFD.
4. **Kick chart** — Formal fetal movement counting (e.g., Sadovsky, Cardiff count).
5. **Biophysical profile (BPP)** — Score ≤4 with absent fetal breathing, movement, tone, and low AFI suggests severe compromise.
6. **Doppler studies** — Abnormal umbilical artery Doppler (absent or reversed end-diastolic flow) suggests severe placental insufficiency.
7. **Maternal investigations** — CBC, LFTs, coagulation profile, Kleihauer-Betke test (feto-maternal haemorrhage), TORCH screen, urine culture, blood glucose/HbA1c, thrombophilia screen.

**4. 2 Complications of IUFD (1 mark):**
1. **Consumptive coagulopathy (DIC)** — Release of thromboplastin from the dead fetus into maternal circulation, especially if the dead fetus is retained >3–4 weeks.
2. **Maternal psychological trauma** — Depression, anxiety, PTSD, postpartum depression.
3. **Infection / Sepsis** — Retained dead fetus can cause chorioamnionitis, endometritis.
4. **Intrauterine infection** leading to maternal sepsis.
5. **Postpartum haemorrhage** (uterine atony associated with stillbirth).
6. **Long-term psycho-emotional consequences** — Grief reaction, marital stress, anxiety in subsequent pregnancy.

### Q6 — Face Presentation (Mentum Presenting) — 5 marks

**Diagnosis:** Face presentation (mentum = chin is the presenting part). The head is hyperextended so the occiput is against the fetal back and the face presents.

**1. 2 Causes of Face Presentation (1 mark):**
1. **Fetal neck hyperextension** (most common cause)
2. **Fetal anomalies** — Anencephaly, congenital goitre, neck tumours (cystic hygroma), nuchal cord
3. **Prematurity** (small fetus can hyperextend its head more easily)
4. **Multiparity** (lax uterine and abdominal muscles)
5. **Cephalopelvic disproportion (CPD)**
6. **Polyhydramnios**
7. **Uterine fibroids** (lower segment)
8. **Placenta previa** (lower segment mass prevents head flexion)

**2. Mechanism of Labour for Face Presentation with Mentum Anterior (2 marks):**

The mechanism differs depending on mentum-anterior vs. mentum-posterior. Only mento-anterior can deliver vaginally.

*For Mentum Anterior:*
1. **Engagement** — The biparietal diameter (9.5 cm) enters the pelvic inlet. The presenting diameter is the submento-bregmatic (9.5 cm).
2. **Descent** — Occurs as the face descends through the pelvis.
3. **Internal rotation** — The mentum rotates **anteriorly** (90°) toward the symphysis pubis.
4. **Extension** (further extension at the neck) — As the chin comes under the pubic arch, the head extends further to deliver the chin, mouth, nose, eyes, and brow in sequence over the perineum.
5. **Flexion** — After the chin is born, the occiput sweeps the perineum, and the head delivers by flexion (the head flexes after the mentum has cleared the pubis).
6. **Restitution & External rotation** — The head rotates back to align with the shoulders.

*For Mentum Posterior:* The mentum rotates posteriorly to the hollow of the sacrum. Vaginal delivery is impossible unless it rotates to mento-anterior (rare). Most require C-section.

**3. Lines of Management (2 marks):**
1. **Confirm diagnosis** — By vaginal examination (feel the nose, mouth, malar bones, chin) and ultrasound (confirm hyperextension, rule out fetal anomaly).
2. **Assess pelvic adequacy** — Clinical pelvimetry to rule out CPD.
3. **Continuous fetal monitoring** — Face presentation has higher risk of fetal distress, cord prolapse, and meconium aspiration.
4. **Determine mentum position**:
   - **Mentum anterior** — Allow **vaginal delivery** (spontaneous or outlet forceps if needed). Observe progress. Most deliver spontaneously.
   - **Mentum posterior** / **mentum transverse** — **Caesarean section** (vaginal delivery only possible if rotation to mento-anterior occurs, which is rare).
5. **Prepare neonatal team** — The newborn will have facial oedema/ecchymosis (self-limiting). Check for airway compromise, especially if forceps used.
6. **Trial of labour** — For mento-anterior, allow adequate time for descent. Arrest disorders require C-section.

### Q7 — Acute Uterine Inversion — 5 marks

**Diagnosis:** Acute puerperal uterine inversion caused by mismanagement of the third stage of labour (fundal pressure on an uncontracted uterus + cord pulling before placental separation → fundus telescopes through the cervix).

**1. Aetiology of This Condition (1.5 marks):**
- **Mismanagement of the 3rd stage of labour:**
  1. **Crede's manoeuvre** — Vigorous fundal pressure on a relaxed (uncontracted) uterus
  2. **Cord traction** before placental separation (pulling the cord while the uterus is not contracted)
  3. **Vigorous fundal pressure** attempted simultaneously with cord traction
- **Predisposing factors:**
  1. Fundal attachment of the placenta
  2. Uterine atony (relaxed uterus)
  3. Precipitate labour (rapid delivery with a relaxed uterus)
  4. Short umbilical cord
  5. Congenital uterine weakness / uterine anomalies
  6. Placenta accreta (increased adherence prevents separation, making traction more likely to invert the uterus)

**2. Degrees of Uterine Inversion (1.5 marks):**

| Degree | Description |
|--------|------------|
| **1st degree (Incomplete)** | The fundus is inverted but remains within the uterine cavity (above the internal os). |
| **2nd degree (Complete)** | The inverted fundus has passed through the cervical os but remains within the vagina. |
| **3rd degree (Prolapsed)** | The entire inverted uterus protrudes outside the introitus (visible externally). |
| **4th degree (Total)** | Both the uterus and the vagina are inverted (prolapsed outside). |

**3. Lines of Treatment (2 marks):**

**Immediate Management:**
1. **Resuscitation** — IV fluids (crystalloids, blood products), treat neurogenic + haemorrhagic shock. Group and cross-match. Call for help (anaesthesia, senior obstetrician).
2. **Do NOT attempt to remove the placenta** if still attached — this increases haemorrhage.

**Manual Replacement (Johnson's Manoeuvre):**
1. Give **tocolytics** to relax the uterus: Terbutaline 0.25 mg IV, or magnesium sulfate, or general anaesthesia with halogenated agents (halothane, sevoflurane).
2. Place the palm of the hand on the inverted fundus, fingers directed toward the cervix.
3. Push the fundus **up through the cervix** (toward the umbilicus) using steady, continuous pressure.
4. The **last part to be replaced is the part that came out first** (the fundus is replaced last).
5. Keep the hand in place until the uterus contracts (give uterotonics: oxytocin, ergometrine) to prevent re-inversion.

**Hydrostatic Replacement (O'Sullivan's Technique):**
- Fill the vagina with warm saline (37°C) via tubing to create hydrostatic pressure that pushes the fundus back into the uterine cavity.

**Surgical Management (if manual/hydrostatic fails):**
1. **Huntington's procedure** — Laparotomy. Grasp the fundus with Allis/Babcock forceps through the inversion ring and pull it up stepwise.
2. **Haultain's procedure** — Incise the posterior cervical constriction ring to release the inverted fundus.
3. **Total abdominal hysterectomy** — If severe haemorrhage, sepsis, or failed reduction with tissue necrosis.

**Post-reduction:**
1. **Uterotonics** — Oxytocin infusion + ergometrine IM to maintain uterine contraction.
2. **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** (e.g., Ampicillin + Gentamicin + Metronidazole) to prevent infection.
3. **Monitor** — Vital signs, blood loss, urine output, signs of DIC.

## III) Family Medicine Questions

### Q1. 3 Red Flag Symptoms During Antenatal Care (3 marks)

1. **Vaginal bleeding** at any stage of pregnancy (threatened abortion, abruption, placenta previa)
2. **Severe headache** ± visual disturbances (blurring, scotomata, flashing lights) — may indicate severe preeclampsia / impending eclampsia
3. **Severe epigastric / right upper quadrant pain** — may indicate HELLP syndrome, acute fatty liver of pregnancy, or severe preeclampsia with hepatic involvement
4. **Convulsions / loss of consciousness** — eclampsia / seizure disorder
5. **Reduced fetal movements** (<10 kicks in 2 hours) — may indicate fetal compromise / IUFD
6. **Leakage of liquor / watery vaginal discharge** (PROM / PPROM)
7. **Severe vomiting** (hyperemesis gravidarum → ketosis, electrolyte disturbance)
8. **Fever** — may indicate chorioamnionitis or systemic infection
9. **Oliguria or anuria** — preeclampsia, renal impairment
10. **Chest pain / shortness of breath** — pulmonary embolism, peripartum cardiomyopathy

### Q2. Four Health Education Messages for a 22-Year-Old P1+0 Female Seeking Antenatal Care (2 marks—0.5 each)

1. **Nutrition & Supplementation** — Eat a balanced diet rich in iron, calcium, and protein. Take daily iron (60 mg) and folic acid (400–500 mcg) supplements. Avoid raw/undercooked food to prevent toxoplasmosis.
2. **Immunization** — Ensure tetanus toxoid vaccination (2 doses in pregnancy, 4 wks apart). Influenza vaccine recommended in any trimester. Avoid live vaccines (MMR, varicella).
3. **Recognition of Danger Signs** — Report any: vaginal bleeding, leaking, severe headache, visual changes, epigastric pain, reduced fetal movements, or fever immediately.
4. **Rest & Positioning** — Get adequate rest, sleep on the **left lateral side** (optimises placental blood flow, reduces supine hypotension). Avoid heavy lifting/exhaustion.
5. **Family Planning** — Discuss postpartum contraception options. For a P1+0 patient, offer long-acting reversible contraception (LARC) — IUD or implant.
6. **Hygiene & Lifestyle** — Maintain good perineal hygiene. Avoid smoking, alcohol, and unprescribed medications. Exercise moderately (walking, swimming).
7. **Breastfeeding Education** — Discuss benefits of exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months, latch technique, and breast care.
8. **Follow-up Schedule** — Attend scheduled ANC visits (monthly until 28 wks, every 2 wks 28–36 wks, weekly from 36 wks).

---

### Q3. Three Common Emotional and Psychological Postpartum Problems (3 marks)

1. **Postpartum ("Baby") Blues** — Transient, self-limiting (days 2–5 postpartum). Characterised by mood swings, tearfulness, irritability, anxiety, insomnia. Prevalence ~40–80%. Resolves spontaneously within 2 weeks. Support and reassurance are sufficient.
2. **Postpartum Depression (PPD)** — More persistent (≥2 weeks). Symptoms: depressed mood, loss of interest, anhedonia, excessive guilt, sleep/appetite disturbance, difficulty bonding with the baby. Prevalence ~10–15%. Requires screening (Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale), counselling, ± pharmacotherapy (SSRIs).
3. **Postpartum Psychosis** — Rare (1–2/1,000 deliveries) but a **psychiatric emergency**. Acute onset of delusions, hallucinations, disorganised behaviour, confusion. Usually occurs within 2 weeks postpartum. Strongly associated with bipolar disorder. Requires **immediate hospitalisation** and antipsychotic therapy.

### Q4. Four Educational Messages for a Lactating Mother to Prevent Breast Problems (2 marks—0.5 each)

1. **Proper latch and positioning** — Ensure the baby's mouth covers the entire areola, not just the nipple. The baby's chin should touch the breast, and the lower lip should be turned outward. This prevents cracked/ sore nipples.
2. **Frequent feeding / empty both breasts** — Feed on demand (every 2–3 hours). Empty one breast fully before switching to the other (hindmilk contains more fat). This prevents **breast engorgement** and **mastitis**.
3. **Breast hygiene** — Wash breasts with plain water (no soap on nipples). Keep nipples dry between feeds. Change breast pads frequently. Avoid plastic-backed nursing pads (trap moisture). This prevents **nipple fissures** and **thrush**.
4. **Manage engorgement early** — Warm compresses or a warm shower before feeding stimulates let-down. Express milk manually or with a pump if the baby is unable to feed. Cold compresses between feeds reduce swelling. This prevents progression to **mastitis / breast abscess**.
5. **Treat cracked nipples immediately** — Express colostrum/milk onto the nipple after feeding (natural healing). Use lanolin cream if needed. If severe, consult a lactation specialist. Continued breastfeeding is usually safe.
6. **Wear a supportive nursing bra** — Correctly fitting, cotton bra. Avoid underwire bras (block milk ducts, increase risk of mastitis/blocked ducts).

### Q5. Mariam — Contraception for Postpartum Depression on Antidepressants (1+4 = 5 marks)

**Case Summary:** Mariam, 32 y/o, primiparous, PPD, on antidepressant therapy, concerned about mental health impact of contraception, wants long-term reliable method.

**A. Most Suitable Contraceptive Method (1 mark):**

**The Copper Intrauterine Device (Cu-IUD) — e.g., Copper T 380A (ParaGard).**

**Rationale:**
- **Non-hormonal** — No oestrogen or progestin → zero effect on mood, no drug interaction with antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs).
- **Long-acting** — Provides 5–10 years of reliable contraception (T>99% efficacy).
- **Quick return to fertility** after removal.
- No increased risk of venous thromboembolism (safe in PPD).
- No effect on breast milk quantity/quality (safe in breastfeeding — not stated but relevant in postpartum).

**Alternative that may also be acceptable:** Progestin-only implant (Nexplanon/Implanon) or LNG-IUS (Mirena) — these have lower systemic hormone levels and less mood impact than COCs, but the **Cu-IUD is the safest** for someone with active depression and antidepressant use.

**B. Four Contraindications for the Copper IUD (4 marks):**

1. **Current pregnancy** (suspected or confirmed — absolute contraindication)
2. **Unexplained vaginal bleeding** (before a definitive diagnosis is made — must rule out malignancy, infection)
3. **Current pelvic infection** — Acute pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), purulent cervicitis, chlamydia/gonorrhoea infection (must be treated first)
4. **Postpartum or post-abortion endometritis** / septic abortion within the past 3 months
5. **Uterine cavity distortion** — Congenital anomalies (septate/bicornuate uterus), large fibroids, or any anatomical abnormality that prevents proper placement
6. **Copper allergy / Wilson's disease** — Absolute contraindication (copper-containing device)
7. **Immunosuppression / high risk of STIs** — Relative contraindication (increased risk of PID)
8. **Genital tract malignancy** — Cervical, endometrial, or ovarian cancer (absolute)

*Note: Provide any 4 of the above for full marks.*
