# ANSWER KEY — Other Qs - MCQs Only

*Answers with brief one-line justifications.*

---

**Q1:** b) Inter-conception care — Premarital care addresses couples before marriage; inter-conception care is between pregnancies, not a component of premarital care.

**Q2:** c) Contraception counselling — Premarital care includes genetic, lifestyle, and health counselling; contraception counselling is typically after marriage.

**Q3:** a) First day of LMP + 8 months + 1 week — Naegele's rule: LMP + 7 days − 3 months. Equivalent to LMP + 9 months + 7 days (or + 8 months + 1 week in some textbooks).

**Q4:** c) Diabetes mellitus is more common in advanced maternal age — Older age is a risk factor for gestational and pre-existing diabetes.

**Q5:** c) 11.5 to 16 kg — IOM guidelines for normal BMI (18.5–24.9): 11.5–16 kg total weight gain.

**Q6:** b) 16–18 weeks — Multiparous women typically feel quickening at 16–18 weeks (primigravida at 18–20 weeks).

**Q7:** b) Children with fetal alcohol syndrome have developmental, behavioral, and learning problems — FAS is associated with significant neurodevelopmental impairments; complete abstinence is recommended in pregnancy.

**Q8:** a) Postpartum blues — Onset day 3–5 postpartum with insomnia, anxiety, crying spells; self-limited and distinct from PPD/psychosis.

**Q9:** c) Cesarean section — Prior C-section is a significant risk factor for PPD (postpartum depression) due to surgical recovery, pain, and potential bonding difficulties.

**Q10:** a) 26–30 weeks — WHO eight-visit schedule: visits at <12, 20, 26, 30, 34, 36, 38, 40 weeks; at 24 weeks the next scheduled visit is 26 weeks (falls in 26–30 range).

**Q11:** d) It is contraindicated because of her obesity — BMI 31 alone is not an absolute contraindication for CHC (WHO MEC category 2 for BMI 30–34).

**Q12:** d) A levonorgestrel IUD or Depo-Provera — Both are long-acting, require no daily or coital action, and are highly effective.

**Q13:** a) Irregular bleeding — Breakthrough bleeding/spotting is the most common reason for discontinuing COCs.

**Q14:** d) Supplementation with vitamin A — High-dose vitamin A is teratogenic; routine supplementation is not recommended in pregnancy.

**Q15:** e) Can be done from 15–20 weeks of gestation — Quad test (AFP, hCG, uE3, inhibin A) window is 15–20 weeks, optimally 16–18 weeks.

**Q16:** b) Promotion for proper ANC — Premarital care includes genetic, lifestyle, and health counselling as well as screening for substance abuse. Promotion for proper ANC is part of antenatal (not premarital) care.

**Q17:** d) During the child immunization visits — Inter-conception care is opportunistically provided when the mother brings the child for immunizations.

**Q18:** a) First day of LMP + 3 months + 1 week — Another way to express Naegele's rule is LMP + 3 months + 1 week (since 9 months − 6 months of backward calculation = 3 months + 1 week).

**Q19:** d) Performance of anatomic ultrasound in the second trimester — While valuable, anatomic ultrasound is not essential for the basic objective of a healthy mother and baby; the other listed elements are core ANC components.

**Q20:** e) Detected murmur of mitral stenosis — Mitral stenosis is a significant cardiac lesion that increases pregnancy risk (pulmonary congestion, arrhythmias, maternal mortality).

**Q21:** b) At 16–18 weeks — Multiparous women feel fetal movements earlier (16–18 weeks) than primigravidas (18–20 weeks).

**Q22:** c) Diabetes mellitus is more commonly found in advanced maternal age — Increased age is associated with higher risk of GDM and type 2 diabetes.

**Q23:** b) Abstain completely from alcohol during pregnancy — There is no known safe level of alcohol in pregnancy; complete abstinence is recommended.

**Q24:** b) 12 gm/dl — Normal first-trimester hemoglobin is ≥11 g/dL; 12 g/dL is within normal range.

**Q25:** b) 5 mg of folic acid once per day beginning at least one month prior to conception and continuing through the first trimester — High-dose (4–5 mg) folic acid is recommended for women with a previous NTD-affected pregnancy.

**Q26:** d) Inadequate weight gain can lead to decreased fetal growth and development — This is the most accurate and evidence-based response about the importance of weight gain.

**Q27:** b) Intrauterine growth restriction — Inadequate gestational weight gain is associated with IUGR and low birth weight.

**Q28:** e) None of the above — All listed options (identify malnutrition, provide data for nutrition plan, identify at-risk individuals, establish baseline) are purposes of nutritional assessment.

**Q29:** a) Is performed for all patients — Comprehensive nutritional assessment is targeted at at-risk or malnourished patients, not all patients.

**Q30:** d) 24-hour recall — The 24-hour dietary recall is the easiest, most popular, and least burdensome method.

**Q31:** d) All of the above — Recall error, intentional alteration of truth, and atypical intake are all common errors with 24-hour recall.

**Q32:** b) A measurement and evaluation of growth, development, and body composition — Anthropometry involves measuring body dimensions and composition.

**Q33:** a) Gaining 11.5–16 kg is recommended for healthy fetal growth — IOM guideline for normal BMI (18.5–24.9).

**Q34:** d) The woman's weight gain is appropriate for this stage of pregnancy — 3.8 kg at 14 weeks (first trimester gain of 1–4 kg) is appropriate.

**Q35:** d) Nutritional iodine requirements generally can be met through intake of iodized salt — This is factual and appropriate; the other statements contain inaccuracies.

**Q36:** a) It is important to eat iron-rich foods like meat every day — Iron needs are high in pregnancy; iron-rich foods are a key message.

**Q37:** c) Iron and folate — Iron and folic acid are routinely recommended supplements in pregnancy.

**Q38:** d) Review the woman's current dietary intake — Assessment of current intake is the first step before planning dietary changes.

**Q39:** a) It is a representation of the optimal number of servings that a person should eat each day from each of the basic food groups — This is the standard definition of the food pyramid.

**Q40:** b) Vegetables — The base of the food guide pyramid is grains; the second layer (from bottom) is vegetables and fruits. Vegetables are in the second layer.

**Q41:** c) Constipation is common with iron supplements — Iron supplements frequently cause constipation as a side effect.

**Q42:** b) Orange juice — Vitamin C (in orange juice) enhances non-heme iron absorption.

**Q43:** c) If I drink lots of milk, I will increase my iron level faster — Milk inhibits iron absorption; this statement indicates a misunderstanding.

**Q44:** a, c, d — Iron supplements with orange juice (vitamin C enhances absorption), lean meats/eggs (heme iron), and dark green leafy vegetables (non-heme iron) are recommended. Option b (20 mg/day) is too low for treatment-dose iron.

**Q45:** d) Rales on lung examination — Rales indicate pulmonary congestion, a sign of worsening cardiac function in pregnancy.

**Q46:** d) Eisenmenger's syndrome — Maternal mortality in Eisenmenger's syndrome is 30–50%; termination is strongly advised.

**Q47:** c) 2–4% — Offspring of a mother with structural heart disease have a 2–4% risk of congenital heart disease (vs. ~1% baseline).

**Q48:** d) Echocardiography — Echocardiography is the investigation of choice for detecting structural cardiac abnormalities in pregnancy (safe, non-invasive, accurate).

**Q49:** e) Prophylactic antibiotics will be administered to prevent endocarditis — Antibiotic prophylaxis is recommended for women with valvular heart disease in labor to prevent infective endocarditis.

**Q50:** a) Mandatory application of forceps or ventouse — Instrumental delivery is not mandatory; second stage may be shortened if needed but is not obligatory.

**Q51:** e) Maternal trauma is more frequent with vacuum extractor than with obstetric forceps — Forceps cause more maternal perineal trauma than vacuum extraction.

**Q52:** b) Direct mento-posterior position — Forceps are contraindicated in face presentation with mento-posterior (cannot deliver safely).

**Q53:** d) Can be used during cesarean section — Forceps may be used to deliver the fetal head at cesarean section.

**Q54:** e) The fetal head should be extended — Traction with forceps flexes the head; extension occurs at delivery of the head over the perineum.

**Q55:** b) The occipito-anterior position — Forceps can be applied in various positions (OA, OP, OT); OA is not a prerequisite.

**Q56:** d) Suspected fetal coagulopathy — Vacuum extraction can cause cephalhematoma and intracranial hemorrhage; contraindicated with fetal coagulopathy.

**Q57:** a) The bladder must be full to identify the lower uterine segment — The bladder should be emptied via catheter before CS to reduce injury risk and improve access.

**Q58:** e) Epidural anesthesia is associated with high risk of postoperative aspiration — Epidural is regional anesthesia with minimal aspiration risk; general anesthesia carries aspiration risk.

**Q59:** e) Breathing exercises are to be started only after the puerperium — Breathing exercises and early mobilization should begin soon after CS to prevent atelectasis and thromboembolism.

**Q60:** c) Elective CS under regional anesthesia is safe for the mother as normal delivery — CS is major abdominal surgery with higher morbidity and mortality than vaginal delivery.

**Q61:** d) Old cervical tear — Old cervical tear is not an indication for cesarean section.

**Q62:** d) Vaginal prolapse — Vaginal prolapse is not an indication for cesarean delivery.

**Q63:** a) Increased prevalence of contracted pelvis — The prevalence of contracted pelvis has not increased; this is not a cause of rising CS rates.

**Q64:** b) Hematoma formation is less likely with the lower segment CS — The lower segment has better hemostasis, less tension on the incision, and heals with a stronger scar than the upper segment.

**Q65:** c) Safer in the presence of pelvic adhesions — Classical CS allows entry into the uterus above the level of adhesions, making it safer when dense pelvic adhesions are present.

**Q66:** e) Fetal weight not less than 4 kg in the current pregnancy — VBAC prerequisites include estimated fetal weight *less than* 4 kg (macrosomia is a relative contraindication).

**Q67:** c) Previous lower segment CS — Previous LSCS is an indication for classical CS only in specific circumstances, not a general indication.

**Q68:** e) Bicornuate uterus — A bicornuate uterus alone is not an indication for cesarean hysterectomy.

**Q69:** d) Mid-forceps is applied for the unengaged head — Forceps require the head to be engaged; mid-forceps is applied when the head is engaged but at mid-pelvis.

**Q70:** b) Uterine inertia — Shortening of the umbilical cord is not a recognised cause of uterine inertia. Uterine inertia can be caused by overdistension, grand multiparity, and other factors.

**Q71:** b) Performed through a vertical incision in the lower uterine segment — Upper segment (classical) CS involves a vertical incision in the upper uterine segment, not the lower segment.

**Q72:** d) Placenta increta — Placenta increta (placenta accreta spectrum) is an indication for emergency cesarean hysterectomy.

**Q73:** a: False, b: True — Ventouse extraction: cephalohematoma incidence is higher with ventouse than forceps (a = False); typical negative pressure is ~0.8 kg/cm² (b = True). Options c/d appear malformed and unrelated to the stem.

**Q74:** d) Residual volume — Residual volume decreases in pregnancy due to diaphragmatic elevation; tidal volume, ventilation, PO₂, and pH increase.

**Q75:** c) Increase in heart rate — Heart rate increases by 10–20 bpm in pregnancy, contributing to increased cardiac output (along with increased stroke volume).

**Q76:** e) 1-Hour postprandial blood glucose — This is a test for gestational diabetes, not a routine screening test at the booking visit.

**Q77:** d) Umbilical soufflé — A uterine soufflé is heard over the uterine vessels; an umbilical soufflé is the sound of fetal blood flowing through the umbilical arteries, synchronous with the FHR.

**Q78:** c) Visual changes — Visual disturbances (blurred vision, scotomata) may indicate severe preeclampsia/eclampsia and require urgent evaluation.

**Q79:** d) Residual volume — Same as Q74; residual volume decreases, not increases.

**Q80:** c) Visual changes — Same as Q78; visual changes are the most worrisome symptom suggesting preeclampsia.

**Q81:** e) 1-Hour postprandial blood glucose — Same as Q76.

**Q82:** d) Umbilical soufflé — Same as Q77 — the soufflé heard over the fetal abdomen that is synchronous with the FHS is the umbilical soufflé.

**Q83:** c) Increase in heart rate — Same as Q75.

**Q84:** e) All of the above — Blood transfusion carries infection risk (Hep B, HIV, etc.), cross-matching difficulties, and transfusion reactions — all are valid risks.

**Q85:** c) 3 weeks — The vaginal ring is worn for 3 weeks, followed by a 1-week hormone-free interval.

**Q86:** d) Combined oral contraceptive pill — Estrogen-containing COCs can suppress milk production; progestin-only methods (mini-pill, Norplant, DMPA) are safe during lactation.

**Q87:** c) Women postabortion — Fertility returns earliest after abortion (~2 weeks); non-breastfeeding postpartum (~4–6 weeks) and breastfeeding women later.

**Q88:** b) Indicates the presence of infection — Foul-smelling lochia is a sign of postpartum endometritis/infection.

**Q89:** d) Pain in left calf with dorsiflexion of left foot — Positive Homan's sign suggests deep vein thrombosis and requires immediate investigation.

**Q90:** a, b, c — Genetic counselling includes family history assessment, preparing couples for the possibility of a child with special needs, and referral for genetic testing. Contraceptive counselling is separate.

**Q91:** a) True — The statement about menopause and its management is true.

**Q92:** b) False — Progestin-only contraceptives (mini-pill, DMPA, implant) do not negatively affect milk production; only estrogen-containing methods do.

**Q93:** b) Promotion for proper ANC — Same reasoning as Q16 — promotion for proper ANC is an antenatal (not premarital) care component.

**Q94:** d) During the child immunization visits — Same as Q17.

**Q95:** a) There is history of recurrent abortion — Recurrent abortion (≥3 spontaneous losses) is an indication for genetic counselling and karyotyping of both partners.

**Q96:** a) First day of LMP + 8 months + 1 week — Same as Q3 — the alternative expression of Naegele's rule.

**Q97:** c) Diabetes mellitus is more commonly found in advanced maternal age — Same as Q4/Q22.

**Q98:** d) Performance of anatomic ultrasound in the second trimester — Same as Q19.

**Q99:** a) History of smoking with discontinuation 2 months ago — Smoking cessation before pregnancy reduces risks; past smoking with discontinuation is not a current risk factor.

**Q100:** a, d, e — Elevated blood pressure, obesity, and mitral stenosis are findings that increase pregnancy risk. Retroverted uterus and thrombosed hemorrhoid do not.

**Q101:** c) 11.5 to 16 kg — IOM guideline for normal BMI (18.5–24.9).

**Q102:** d) Intrauterine growth retardation — IUGR is associated with *inadequate* weight gain, not excessive weight gain.

**Q103:** b) At 16 weeks — Multiparous women typically feel fetal movements around 16–18 weeks; the closest option is 16 weeks.

**Q104:** b) Abstain completely from alcohol during pregnancy — Same as Q23.

**Q105:** e) Can be done from 15-20 weeks of gestation — The quadruple test (quad screen) is optimally performed between 15+0 and 20+6 weeks of gestation.

**Q106:** c) Hypercholesterolemia — Hypertension, smoking, preeclampsia, and diabetes are known causes of IUGR; hypercholesterolemia alone is not.

**Q107:** c) Perform two serum beta HCG tests 46 hours apart — For pregnancy of unknown location with stable patient, serial beta hCG measurements help differentiate ectopic from early IUP.

**Q108:** c) Umbilical soufflé — The normal soufflé heard over the fetal abdomen at the same rate as FHS is the umbilical soufflé (fetal blood flow in umbilical vessels).

**Q109:** d) Trophoblastic cells — HCG is produced by the syncytiotrophoblast of the placenta.

**Q110:** d) Blighted ovum — Anembryonic pregnancy (blighted ovum): gestational sac with absent fetal parts/embryo.

**Q111:** d) The incidence of left kidney involvement is more than the right kidney — The right kidney is more commonly affected in pregnancy due to uterine dextrorotation and compression of the right ureter.

**Q112:** d) Admit the patient, start antihypertensive, MgSO₄ and terminate the pregnancy — Proteinuria +3 with presumed preeclampsia/eclampsia requires admission, antihypertensives, magnesium sulfate, and delivery.

**Q113:** a) IV methergin after delivery — Methylergometrine (Methergin) causes vasoconstriction and hypertension; contraindicated in heart disease. Oxytocin is preferred.

**Q114:** a) 750 ml/min — Uterine blood flow at term is approximately 500–800 ml/min (up to 10% of cardiac output).

**Q115:** b) Treat with intramuscular methotrexate — Meets methotrexate criteria: HCG <5000 IU/mL, mass <3.5–4 cm, no cardiac activity, hemodynamically stable.

**Q116:** c) Testing for TORCH — TORCH screening is not recommended in the standard workup for recurrent pregnancy loss; thyroid, APS, and genetic testing are indicated.

**Q117:** b) Implantation occurs most commonly on posterior wall near the fundus — This is the most common implantation site.

**Q118:** d) Increased alveolar residual volume — Residual volume decreases in pregnancy; fibrinogen, cardiac output, and GFR increase.

**Q119:** *Image not available* — Cannot determine which Leopold maneuver is shown without the image. Common exam question: first maneuver (fundal grip) determines presentation.

**Q120:** b) It is due to increased blood flow through the umbilical artery — Uterine soufflé is due to increased blood flow through the *uterine* arteries, synchronous with maternal pulse.

**Q121:** b) Hysterectomy — For complete hydatidiform mole with completed family, hysterectomy may be the treatment of choice to eliminate the risk of persistent trophoblastic disease.

**Q122:** b) Furosemide — Diuretics are not recommended for management of hypertension in pregnancy (reduce placental perfusion).

**Q123:** b) Mitral stenosis — Mitral stenosis is the most common rheumatic heart disease encountered in pregnancy.

**Q124:** d) Disappears within two days — Cephalhematoma resolves over weeks to months, not days.

**Q125:** a) Fetal distress — Fetal heart rate abnormalities (late decelerations, bradycardia) are the earliest and most common warning sign of uterine scar rupture.

**Q126:** b) Two weeks — The uterus becomes a pelvic organ by approximately 2 weeks postpartum (involution).

**Q127:** d) Preterm labor — Ventouse is contraindicated in preterm labor (<34 weeks) due to fetal skull fragility and risk of intracranial hemorrhage.

**Q128:** c) Rubra, serosa, alba — The correct sequence of lochia: rubra (days 1–3), serosa (days 4–10), alba (days 11–42).

**Q129:** a) Involvement of the anal sphincter is classified as 4th degree perineal tear — 3rd degree involves anal sphincter; 4th degree involves rectal mucosa.

**Q130:** b) C8, T1 — Klumpke's palsy involves the lower trunk (C8–T1 nerve roots), affecting the hand and forearm.

**Q131:** d) Persistent mento-posterior — Face presentation with mento-posterior cannot be delivered vaginally; CS is required.

**Q132:** c) The mere presence of Couvelaire uterus — Couvelaire uterus (uteroplacental apoplexy) alone is not an indication for hysterectomy; it usually responds to conservative management.

**Q133:** a) Abruption placenta — Classic triad: hypertensive pregnant woman + abdominal pain + vaginal bleeding + tense/tender uterus + absent FHR = abruptio placenta.

**Q134:** d) Mono-chorionic and di-amniotic in monozygotic twins — This is the most common placentation for monozygotic twins (70% are monochorionic diamniotic).

**Q135:** c) Administer betamethasone — In preterm labour without signs of infection, betamethasone (corticosteroid) is given to accelerate fetal lung maturity before delivery.

**Q136:** c) Cesarean section — Central placenta previa with heavy bleeding at term mandates emergency cesarean section regardless of fetal presentation.

**Q137:** b) Tubal ectopic pregnancy — Ectopic pregnancy is not a cause of IUGR; the other options (PIH, heart disease, anemia) are.

**Q138:** c) Microcephaly — Microcephaly can be associated with polyhydramnios due to impaired fetal swallowing. Bilateral renal agenesis causes severe oligohydramnios, not polyhydramnios.

**Q139:** a) Perform Rhesus antibody titre — For an Rh-negative woman at booking, the next step is to check for Rh antibodies (indirect Coombs test).

**Q140:** b) External cephalic version — ECV is performed before labor for breech presentation; techniques for delivering the after-coming head include Piper's forceps, Burns-Marshall, and Jaw flexion shoulder traction.

**Q141:** d) Interspinous diameter — The interspinous diameter (~10 cm) is the smallest diameter of the true pelvis.

**Q142:** a) Stop oxytocin infusion — Hyperstimulation (4 contractions/10 min) with fetal bradycardia: first step is to stop oxytocin and check fetal status.

**Q143:** d) Face presentation when the chin lies under the symphysis pubis — Face presentation with mento-anterior (chin anterior) can deliver vaginally.

**Q144:** c) Brow — Brow presentation cannot deliver vaginally; cesarean section is mandatory.

**Q145:** c) Uterine atony — Uterine atony is the most common cause of postpartum hemorrhage (>70% of cases).

**Q146:** d) More common in multiparous women — Pathological retraction ring (Bandl's ring) is more common in obstructed labor, especially in primigravidas, not multiparas.

**Q147:** d) Carry out balloon tamponade — When uterotonics fail for uterine atony, balloon tamponade (Bakri balloon) is the next step before surgical interventions.

**Q148:** e) Stage I — Cervical dilatation 6 cm with effective contractions is in the first stage of labor (active phase).

**Q149:** b) Cervical insufficiency — Cervical insufficiency is a cause of preterm birth, not a type of abnormal uterine action (dystocia).

**Q150:** c) Heparin — Heparin is a large molecule that does not cross the placental barrier; it is the anticoagulant of choice in pregnancy.

**Q151:** a) Blood transfusion to raise hemoglobin to 9 gm% — Severe anemia (Hb 6 g/dL) at 38 weeks requires blood transfusion to reach a safe level before delivery.

**Q152:** d) Decidual reaction — In ectopic pregnancy, the endometrium shows a decidual reaction (Arias-Stella reaction) without chorionic villi.

**Q153:** e) There is increased incidence of hydramnios and preeclampsia — Diabetes in pregnancy is associated with polyhydramnios and preeclampsia.

**Q154:** e) Drink ginger syrup — Ginger is an evidence-based non-pharmacological treatment for nausea in early pregnancy.

**Q155:** e) 75 gm — WHO recommends a 75 g oral glucose load for the OGTT in pregnancy (2-hour test).

**Q156:** b) Protamine sulphate — Protamine sulfate is the specific antidote for heparin.

**Q157:** c) Quickening — Quickening (maternal perception of fetal movement) is a probable sign, not a sure sign of pregnancy.

**Q158:** b) Decreased basal metabolic rate — BMR increases (~20%) in pregnancy due to increased metabolic demands.

**Q159:** a) Turtle sign — Turtle sign (shoulder retraction after head delivery) is associated with shoulder dystocia, not intrauterine fetal demise. Spalding sign (overlapping skull bones) is a sign of fetal demise.

**Q160:** e) Disseminated intravascular coagulopathy — DIC is the most important cause of maternal death in placental abruption, not meconium aspiration syndrome.

**Q161:** e) Increased maternal serum alpha fetoprotein — Increased MSAFP is a marker for open neural tube defects and abdominal wall defects (gastroschisis, omphalocele), not abnormal uterine artery Doppler.

**Q162:** c) Administer broad spectrum antibiotics and corticosteroids and manage conservatively if the temperature decreases — PPROM at 32 weeks with infection: treat infection first, give steroids, then reconsider management based on response.

**Q163:** c) Commence intramuscular corticosteroids and oral nifedipine — Preterm labor at 32 weeks: give corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity and tocolysis (nifedipine) to delay delivery.

**Q164:** a) Has different blood groups — Monozygotic twins result from one fertilized ovum and have the same blood group (identical genotype).

**Q165:** c) Fundal pressure — Fundal pressure is contraindicated in shoulder dystocia (may worsen impaction and cause fetal injury); HELPERR maneuvers include McRoberts, episiotomy, posterior arm delivery, and rotational maneuvers.

**Q166:** c) Rubella — Live attenuated vaccines (MMR, varicella, rubella) are contraindicated in pregnancy. Killed/inactivated vaccines (Hep B, pneumococcus, tetanus, influenza) are safe.

**Q167:** d) Duodenal atresia — Fetal GI obstruction (duodenal atresia, esophageal atresia) prevents amniotic fluid swallowing, leading to polyhydramnios.

**Q168:** e) Two fetal heart rate accelerations are noted in 20 minutes — A reactive NST is defined as ≥2 accelerations of ≥15 bpm lasting ≥15 seconds in 20 minutes.

**Q169:** c) Methotrexate — Persistent elevated beta-hCG after molar pregnancy evacuation indicates gestational trophoblastic neoplasia; chemotherapy (methotrexate) is first-line treatment.

**Q170:** f) There is increased incidence of hydramnios and preeclampsia — Multiple pregnancy (twins) is associated with increased incidence of both polyhydramnios and preeclampsia.
